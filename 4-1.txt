Question 1:
Shenkar et al. (2008) develop the metaphor on “cultural friction”.

a. What is cultural friction and why do the authors believe that this metaphor is more suitable in social science research compared to cultural distance?
W9-3
What is cultural friction?
Cultural friction means the tension, resistance, conflict, and negotiation that happen when cultural systems actually meet. Shenkar et al. argue that “cultural distance” is too static and abstract because it treats culture like a measurable gap between countries. It assumes cultures are separate, stable, and comparable, like physical distance. But in real international business, cultures do not simply “sit apart”; they interact through firms, managers, employees, governments, and local communities. 
The metaphor emphasizes that intercultural encounters are dynamic and socially constructed rather than static. It recognizes that actors from different cultures may have unequal power, different interests, and conflicting goals. 
The friction metaphor is more suitable because it focuses on actual encounters, not only numerical differences. It also captures power relations, conflict, hierarchy, and local resistance. For example, when a multinational enters a host country, the issue is not only how “far” the home and host cultures are, but how actors respond to each other, who has power, and whether local groups accept or resist the firm. Shenkar et al. therefore see friction as more realistic for social science because culture is socially constructed, dynamic, and embedded in relationships. 
Why is it more suitable?
Shenkar et al. (2008) argue that cultural friction is better than cultural distance because cultural distance became too mechanical and static. Hofstede (1983) helped popularize the idea that national cultures can be compared through measurable dimensions, such as power distance, uncertainty avoidance, individualism, and masculinity. Kogut and Singh (1988) then turned these dimensions into a single cultural distance index. This made culture easy to measure, but also oversimplified it. Shenkar et al. criticize this because it treats culture like geographic distance: fixed, symmetric, and measurable from one country to another. 
The problem is that real cultural encounters are not just about “how far apart” two countries are. They involve contact, negotiation, conflict, power differences, and adaptation. Cultural distance assumes cultures are separate and static, while cultural friction focuses on what happens when cultures actually meet. 
Therefore, Shenkar et al. believe friction is more suitable for social science research because it captures the dynamic, relational, and political nature of cross-cultural interaction better than Hofstede-based or Kogut and Singh-style distance measures. 

b. Tung and Verbeke (2010) stress that there are ten common assumptions on cultural distance dimensions in applied IB and management research. These assumptions are categorized into three categories of limitations. What are the three categories of limitations and what do these types of limitations imply? Provide for each type of limitation an example.
W9-4
Tung and Verbeke classify the ten assumptions into three types of limitations.
Type I: Generic limitations. These are broad problems in cultural distance research, especially the assumptions of symmetry and stability. Symmetry means assuming that distance from country A to B is the same as from B to A. For example, German managers may adjust differently in the U.S. than American managers in Germany. Stability means assuming cultural scores do not change over time, which ignores cultural evolution. 
Type II: Remediable weaknesses in empirical design. These can be improved through better research design. Examples include assuming a linear relationship between cultural distance and outcomes, or assuming cultural distance clearly causes entry mode or performance. For instance, higher cultural distance may not always reduce performance; it may sometimes create learning or complementarities. 
Type III: Weaknesses requiring reconceptualization. These are deeper theoretical problems. They include assuming national cultures are homogeneous, cultural distance is always negative, firm characteristics do not matter, and dimensions can be aggregated into one index. For example, China or the U.S. may contain major regional and ethnic cultural variation. 
 
c. How does the cultural friction metaphor project by Shenkar et al. (2008) address (or not address) each of these three types of limitations? Explain your answer carefully and ensure that you relate the theory on the friction metaphor to each category of limitations in a structured manner.
W9-3
Shenkar et al.’s cultural friction metaphor addresses many, but not all, of Tung and Verbeke’s limitations.
For Type I generic limitations, friction improves on distance because it rejects simple symmetry and stability. Friction is relational: the experience of one actor meeting another may differ depending on power, identity, and interests. This helps explain why country A’s experience with country B may not equal country B’s experience with country A. Friction also treats culture as dynamic because cultural interaction changes over time through responses and counter-responses. 
For Type II remediable empirical weaknesses, friction helps by warning researchers not to assume simple linear or automatic causal effects. Cultural difference does not mechanically produce one outcome. Instead, outcomes depend on the specific encounter, actors, interests, and context. However, friction is more of a theoretical metaphor than a direct statistical method, so researchers still need careful empirical design.
For Type III reconceptualization weaknesses, friction is especially useful. It directly challenges the idea that national culture is homogeneous or that distance is always negative. It emphasizes local constituencies, hidden voices, power asymmetry, and conflict. It also discourages reducing culture to one aggregate index. However, friction may overemphasize tension and conflict and may understate cooperation or harmony. So, it strongly advances reconceptualization, but it does not completely solve measurement problems.

Question 2:

Foreign investment has been an engine of growth worldwide and has been a major subject of research for IB scholars.
a. Li and Filer (2007) discuss two different governance systems related to ordering. What are these two governance systems? Make sure you explain each of them carefully.
W11-2
Li and Filer (2007) discuss two governance systems: public ordering and private ordering. These systems explain how economic exchanges and investments are protected in different institutional environments.
Public ordering refers to a governance system based on formal rules, laws, and institutions. In this system, governments create transparent laws, independent courts interpret those laws, and the state enforces contracts fairly and efficiently. Investors rely mainly on public legal institutions to protect property rights and resolve disputes. This type of governance is common in rule-based economies, especially many Western countries. Because investors trust legal institutions, they are more willing to engage in indirect or portfolio investment, where they have less direct control over assets. 
Private ordering exists when public legal systems are weak, inefficient, or unreliable. In such environments, investors cannot depend fully on courts or formal institutions. Instead, they rely on private relationships, networks, personal control, and informal arrangements to protect their investments. Li and Filer argue that direct investment (FDI) is preferred in these environments because investors maintain greater managerial control and can privately safeguard their interests. This system is more relation-based and often appears in countries with weak rule of law. 
 
b. Buckley et al. (2007) discuss two general principles of the theory of FDI (cf., Buckley and Casson, 1976). What are these two principles?
W4-3

Buckley et al. (2007), building on Buckley and Casson (1976), explain that the theory of FDI rests on two major principles.
The first principle is that firms internalize imperfect or missing external markets when doing so is more efficient than using the market. External markets for knowledge, technology, or intermediate products are often inefficient because of transaction costs, uncertainty, and risks of opportunism. Firms therefore choose FDI to internalize these transactions within the organization. Instead of licensing or exporting, firms establish subsidiaries abroad to maintain control over their proprietary knowledge and reduce transaction costs. Internalization continues until the costs of organizing activities internally become greater than the benefits. 
The second principle is that firms choose locations that minimize the overall costs of operations. Firms invest in countries that provide advantages such as market size, natural resources, lower production costs, strategic assets, or geographic proximity. Buckley et al. explain that firms may engage in market-seeking, resource-seeking, or efficiency-seeking FDI depending on the benefits offered by host countries. Thus, FDI location decisions are shaped by both internalization advantages and location advantages.
 
c. Delios and Beamish (1999) argue that the institutional environment influence foreign investment ownership.
1.	In their opinion, how does institutional environment influence foreign investment ownership and why? (15 points)
W4-1
Delios and Beamish (1999) argue that the institutional environment strongly influences foreign investment ownership because institutions affect transaction risks, property-right protection, and the efficiency of governance structures.
In countries with strong institutional environments, such as effective legal systems and secure property rights, foreign firms are more likely to take higher ownership positions. Strong institutions reduce uncertainty and transactional hazards, allowing firms to operate with greater confidence and control. Foreign investors feel safer transferring proprietary assets and knowledge because contracts can be enforced effectively and opportunism is less likely. 
In contrast, weak institutional environments increase uncertainty and risk. Poor legal protection, political instability, or weak contract enforcement create hazards for foreign investors. Under these conditions, firms may prefer lower ownership levels or joint ventures because local partners provide local knowledge, legitimacy, and assistance in navigating institutional challenges. Shared ownership also reduces exposure to risk and resource commitment. Delios and Beamish therefore argue that ownership choice is not determined only by transaction-cost considerations, but also by the broader institutional environment of the host country. 
Their findings suggest that institutional and experience variables are often more important than purely transactional variables in explaining ownership structures.

Yes, Delios and Beamish’s arguments are generally consistent with Devarakonda et al. (2021). Both studies argue that institutions influence governance design because institutions shape transaction costs, uncertainty, and dispute-resolution mechanisms.

Question 3:
a. Explain three of the four dimensions explained by Geert Hofstede (1983). Make sure you name each of the cultural dimensions and explain what they each entail.
W9-1
Hofstede (1983) developed four major cultural dimensions to explain how national cultures differ. Three important dimensions are Individualism versus Collectivism, Power Distance, and Uncertainty Avoidance.
The first dimension is Individualism versus Collectivism. Individualistic societies emphasize personal goals, independence, and individual achievement. People are expected to take care of themselves and prioritize personal success. In collectivist societies, individuals are strongly connected to groups such as family, organizations, or communities, and group loyalty and harmony are highly valued. Hofstede found that wealthier countries often score higher on individualism. 
The second dimension is Power Distance. This refers to the extent to which societies accept unequal distributions of power. In high power-distance societies, hierarchy and authority are accepted as normal, and subordinates are less likely to challenge leaders. In low power-distance societies, people prefer equality, consultation, and participative decision making. 
The third dimension is Uncertainty Avoidance. This dimension measures how comfortable societies are with uncertainty and ambiguity. Strong uncertainty-avoidance cultures prefer rules, structure, predictability, and security. Weak uncertainty-avoidance cultures are more tolerant of ambiguity, risk, and change. 
 
b. According Holburn and Zelner (2010), MNEs have two different types of political capabilities. Which two capabilities are they referring to?
W6-5
According to Holburn and Zelner (2010), MNEs differ in their political capabilities because firms develop these capabilities through two main mechanisms: organizational learning and cognitive imprinting.
First, organizational learning means that firms learn from repeated experience in their home-country political environment. If a firm comes from a country with weak institutional constraints, strong political conflict, or high rent-seeking, it must learn how to deal with government actors, influence policy, build coalitions, and protect itself from harmful policy changes. Over time, these repeated experiences become routines and capabilities that the firm can use abroad.
Second, cognitive imprinting means that managers are shaped by the political and institutional environment in which they operate. Their home-country experience affects how they interpret political risk, uncertainty, and government behavior. Managers from politically difficult environments may see policy risk as normal and manageable, while others may see it as too dangerous.
Together, these two mechanisms explain why some MNEs are better able to enter and operate in risky host countries.
 
c. Why do Boddewyn and Brewer (1994) believe that governments in IB research are considered a variable rather than a constant? Explain your answer.
W6-2
Boddewyn and Brewer (1994) argue that governments in international business research should be viewed as a variable rather than a constant because governments differ greatly across countries and actively shape business activities. International firms operate across multiple political systems, and these systems vary in laws, regulations, political stability, ideology, and government intervention. Therefore, governments cannot simply be treated as fixed background conditions. 
The authors explain that governments influence firms’ entry, operations, competition, and survival. States create market imperfections by controlling trade, investment, taxation, regulation, and ownership rules. Because countries have different political economies and levels of openness, firms must adapt their strategies to each government environment. For example, one government may encourage foreign investment, while another may impose restrictions or expropriate foreign assets. 
Boddewyn and Brewer also argue that governments are not only constraints but can also act as a factor of production. Firms may try to influence governments through lobbying, bargaining, coalition building, or partnerships to gain competitive advantages. Political behavior therefore becomes an important strategic activity for multinational firms.
Overall, the authors believe governments are dynamic, diverse, and strategically important actors in IB, which is why they should be treated as variables that shape international business outcomes rather than as passive constants.

Question 4:
a. Reuer et al (2014) argue that there is an inherent, but fundamental tension that IJVs are confronted with. What is this tension that they are referring to? Explain the tension.
W1-4

Reuer et al. (2014) argue that IJVs face a fundamental tension between administrative control by parent firms and delegation of authority to local management.
On one side, IJVs require strong administrative control because they are hybrid organizational forms created by two or more independent firms working together under incomplete contracts. Since contracts cannot specify every possible future contingency, there is always a risk of opportunism, conflict, or coordination problems between partners. The board of directors helps monitor the venture, coordinate activities, protect parent interests, and resolve disputes. Board involvement is especially important when partners compete outside the alliance or when the venture involves R&D activities, which create knowledge appropriation risks and contractual incompleteness. 
On the other side, IJVs operate in foreign environments with different cultures and uncertain local conditions. Under these circumstances, excessive board involvement can reduce efficiency because board members may lack local knowledge and may respond slowly to local changes. Delegating authority to local management can therefore improve responsiveness and operational effectiveness. 
Thus, the tension is between the need for parent firms to control and coordinate the venture versus the need to give flexibility and decision-making authority to local managers.
 
b. Davis (1971) provides several examples how to make a theoretical contribution (i.e., 
when something is ‘interesting’). Mention 3 types of theoretical contributions that he 
suggests authors can make and explain why they are contributions according to Davis.
W1-1 (Canvas presentation)

Davis (1971) argues that a theory becomes “interesting” when it challenges assumptions that people normally take for granted. He suggests several ways authors can make theoretical contributions.
The first type is an Organizing Proposition. This occurs when something assumed to be unorganized or chaotic is shown to have an underlying structure or pattern. For example, Marx argued that capitalist society, which seemed disorganized and conflictual, actually had a systematic structure shaped by class relations. This is a contribution because it changes how people interpret reality. 
The second type is a Composition Proposition. This occurs when something assumed to be made of many different parts is shown to be fundamentally one phenomenon, or vice versa. For example, Durkheim showed that suicide, usually viewed as an individual act, was also a social phenomenon shaped by social forces. This is interesting because it reverses common assumptions about the nature of the phenomenon. 
The third type is a Generalization Proposition. This occurs when something believed to apply only in a local or specific setting is shown to apply more broadly. Freud’s ideas about unconscious behavior are an example because they generalized insights about specific cases to wider human behavior. This is considered a contribution because it expands the scope of understanding.

Question 2: Peng (2001) argues that the resource-based view (RBV) is a neglected theory in international business.

A. According Peng (2001), how can the resource based view be helpful in explaining firm's internationalization strategies?
W3-4

Peng (2001) argues that the Resource-Based View (RBV) helps explain internationalization by focusing on the firm’s unique resources and capabilities rather than only external market conditions. According to RBV, firms expand internationally when they possess valuable, rare, difficult-to-imitate, and non-substitutable resources that can create competitive advantage abroad. 
Traditional international business theories mainly emphasized industry conditions or transaction costs. In contrast, RBV explains why some firms succeed internationally while others do not, even within the same industry. Firms internationalize because they want to exploit their firm-specific advantages in foreign markets. These advantages may include managerial know-how, organizational routines, technological knowledge, international experience, brand reputation, or human capital. 
Peng also explains that RBV is useful for understanding multinational corporations, strategic alliances, market entry decisions, and international entrepreneurship. For example, firms with strong organizational capabilities are better able to manage international diversification and transfer knowledge across subsidiaries. 
Overall, Peng argues that RBV improves our understanding of internationalization because it focuses on internal firm capabilities as the main drivers of foreign expansion and international competitive advantage.

B. What are the differences between TCE and the RBV in explaining market entry (according Peng 2001)?
W3-4

According to Peng (2001), Transaction Cost Economics (TCE) and the Resource-Based View (RBV) explain market entry from different perspectives. TCE focuses mainly on minimizing transaction costs and reducing uncertainty, while RBV focuses on leveraging firm-specific resources and capabilities. 
TCE argues that firms choose entry modes based on efficiency and control. Firms internalize activities when market transactions are costly or risky. For example, if opportunism, uncertainty, or information asymmetry are high, firms may prefer wholly owned subsidiaries instead of licensing or exporting. The main concern of TCE is cost minimization and governance efficiency.
In contrast, RBV emphasizes the strategic value of firm resources. Firms choose entry modes that best exploit and protect their valuable capabilities. According to Peng, firms internationalize because they possess unique assets such as managerial knowledge, organizational capabilities, technology, or experience that can generate competitive advantage in foreign markets. 
Another important difference is that TCE mainly explains how firms reduce risks and transaction costs, whereas RBV explains how firms create value and sustain competitive advantage internationally. RBV also highlights learning, knowledge transfer, and capability development across subsidiaries and alliances.
Regarding market entry, TCE predicts entry modes that maximize efficiency and minimize hazards, while RBV predicts entry modes that best exploit or protect valuable firm-specific resources. For example, a firm with strong technological knowledge may prefer a wholly owned subsidiary to better protect proprietary capabilities.
Therefore, TCE is more efficiency-oriented, while RBV is more capability- and strategy-oriented in explaining foreign market entry.

Question 3: Peng et al. (2008) discuss the need for an institution-based view of international business strategy (in particular for emerging economies).

A. What is the strategy tripod?
W4-4

Peng et al. (2008) argue that firm strategy and performance can be explained through the “strategy tripod,” which consists of three complementary perspectives: the industry-based view, the resource-based view, and the institution-based view. 
The first leg is the industry-based view, mainly associated with Porter (1980). This perspective argues that industry conditions, such as competition, entry barriers, and market structure, shape firm strategy and performance.
The second leg is the resource-based view (RBV), associated with Barney (1991). RBV argues that firms gain competitive advantage through valuable, rare, difficult-to-imitate, and non-substitutable resources and capabilities. Differences between firms explain differences in performance.
Peng et al. argue that these two perspectives are incomplete because they often ignore institutions. Therefore, they propose the institution-based view as the third leg of the tripod. Institutions are the “rules of the game,” including formal institutions such as laws and regulations and informal institutions such as culture, norms, and networks.
According to Peng et al., strategic choices are shaped not only by industry competition and firm resources, but also by institutional conditions and transitions. This perspective is especially important in emerging economies where institutions are changing rapidly. 

B. Why are institutional conditions and transitions helpful in explaining strategy and performance?
W4-4

Peng et al. (2008) argue that institutional conditions and institutional transitions are important because they strongly influence firms’ strategic choices and performance, especially in emerging economies. 
Institutions are the formal and informal “rules of the game” that shape economic and social interactions. Formal institutions include laws, regulations, and political systems, while informal institutions include culture, norms, and relationships. These institutions influence how firms compete, cooperate, and enter foreign markets.
In developed economies, institutions are usually stable and often taken for granted. However, in emerging economies, institutions are weaker, unstable, or rapidly changing. Peng et al. call these changes “institutional transitions.” Because the rules are changing and uncertain, firms must continuously adapt their strategies.
Institutional conditions affect transaction costs, uncertainty, political risk, and access to resources. For example, weak legal systems may encourage firms to rely more on personal networks and alliances. Government policies, corruption, and market reforms can also shape entry mode choices, investment strategies, and firm performance.
Peng et al. argue that institutions are not just background conditions. Instead, institutions directly influence what strategies firms can use and which competitive advantages they can develop. Therefore, institutional conditions and transitions help explain why firms behave differently across countries and why some firms perform better than others in emerging markets.

C. Transaction cost economics and institutional theory are both helpful in explaining firm's internationalization strategies (e.g., entry mode choice). What are the similarities and differences between these two theories?
W4-4, W4-2

Transaction Cost Economics (TCE) and institutional theory are both useful for explaining firms’ internationalization strategies, especially entry mode choice. Both theories recognize that firms operate under uncertainty and that the external environment influences strategic decisions. They also agree that firms try to reduce risks and improve performance when entering foreign markets. 
However, the two theories focus on different mechanisms.
TCE mainly focuses on efficiency and cost minimization. According to TCE, firms choose entry modes based on transaction costs caused by uncertainty, opportunism, weak contracts, or imperfect markets. Firms internalize activities through wholly owned subsidiaries when market transactions are too costly or risky. Meyer (2001) explains that weak institutions in transition economies increase transaction costs because firms face unclear regulations, corruption, weak legal systems, and information problems. Therefore, firms adjust entry modes to reduce these costs.
Institutional theory, in contrast, focuses more broadly on the impact of formal and informal institutions on firm behavior. Peng et al. (2008) argue that institutions shape strategic choices because they determine the “rules of the game.” Firms respond not only to efficiency concerns but also to legitimacy, political pressures, social norms, and institutional expectations. Institutional theory also emphasizes that institutions can both constrain and enable firms.
Another difference is that TCE focuses mainly on economic efficiency, while institutional theory includes political, legal, and social influences. TCE assumes firms seek the most efficient governance structure, whereas institutional theory explains how firms adapt to institutional environments and transitions.
Overall, TCE explains internationalization mainly through transaction costs and governance efficiency, while institutional theory explains it through broader institutional constraints, legitimacy, and changing rules of the game.

Question 4: Husted and Allen (2006) investigate corporate social responsibility in the multinational enterprise.

A. According Davies (1979), why is this paper "interesting?
W10-1, W1-1

According to Davis (1971), a paper is considered “interesting” when it challenges assumptions that people normally take for granted. Interesting theories deny accepted beliefs and present a new way of understanding reality. Davis explains that “interesting theories are those which deny certain assumptions of their audience.” He further argues that interesting theories often follow the logic of “what seems to be X is actually non-X.”
The paper by Husted and Allen (2006) is interesting because it challenges the common assumption that corporate social responsibility (CSR) is mainly a universal ethical activity that is implemented similarly across countries. Instead, the authors argue that CSR in multinational enterprises (MNEs) is shaped by both strategic motives and institutional pressures.
The paper also challenges the assumption that CSR is only motivated by morality or philanthropy. Husted and Allen show that firms may use CSR strategically to improve competitiveness, legitimacy, and stakeholder relationships in foreign markets. At the same time, they argue that local institutional environments, such as regulations, culture, and social expectations, strongly influence CSR practices.
Therefore, the paper is “interesting” because it reframes CSR from being a purely ethical activity into a phenomenon that is both strategically driven and institutionally embedded. This directly challenges traditional assumptions about CSR in multinational firms.
 
B. Explain the strategic approach and institutional approach in relation to CSR for multinational enterprise (MNE).
W10-1

Husted and Allen (2006) explain two main approaches to understanding CSR in multinational enterprises (MNEs): the strategic approach and the institutional approach. 
The strategic approach views CSR as a tool that helps firms achieve competitive advantage. Under this perspective, MNEs engage in CSR activities because these activities improve reputation, customer loyalty, legitimacy, and long-term profitability. CSR is therefore connected to business objectives and firm performance. Companies may design CSR programs that fit local market opportunities, improve relationships with stakeholders, or strengthen their brand image. In this view, CSR is proactive and economically motivated.
In contrast, the institutional approach argues that CSR is shaped by the institutional environment of the host country. Institutions include laws, regulations, social norms, culture, NGOs, and stakeholder expectations. MNEs adopt CSR practices because they face pressure to gain legitimacy and conform to local expectations. Different countries may have different standards regarding labor rights, environmental protection, or social responsibility. Therefore, CSR practices vary across institutional contexts. 
Another difference is that the strategic approach focuses more on managerial choice and firm-specific interests, whereas the institutional approach focuses more on environmental constraints and social expectations.
The institutional approach also emphasizes legitimacy and liability of foreignness (LOF). Foreign firms may use CSR to reduce distrust and improve their social acceptance in host countries. Strategic approaches instead emphasize how CSR can create value and support organizational goals.
The strategic approach focuses more on efficiency and competitive advantage, while the institutional approach focuses on legitimacy and adaptation to local environments. Husted and Allen argue that both approaches are important because MNEs must balance global business strategy with local institutional pressures when developing CSR practices.

Question 1
a. Two hypotheses of the product-cycle model were examined by Cantwell (1995). Which hypothesis does he disagree with (and why) and which hypothesis does he agree with (and why)?
W8-1

Cantwell (1995) reexamines two major hypotheses from Vernon’s product-cycle model. He disagrees with the first hypothesis, which argues that innovation activities are mainly located in the multinational corporation’s home country. Cantwell rejects this idea because historical patent evidence shows that many multinational corporations had already dispersed their technological and research activities internationally long before the recent globalization era. He demonstrates that firms, especially large European chemical firms and American electrical companies, conducted significant innovation activities abroad as early as the interwar period. Therefore, innovation was not always centralized in the parent company’s home country.
However, Cantwell agrees with the second hypothesis, which states that international expansion is led by technologically advanced firms. He argues that technology leaders possess stronger innovative capabilities, greater profits, and better organizational resources, allowing them to internationalize more successfully than competitors. Over time, these leading firms developed global technological networks to exploit specialized knowledge and “centers of excellence” located in different countries. Thus, Cantwell supports the idea that technology leadership drives internationalization, although the form of internationalization has evolved toward globally integrated innovation networks.

 
b. Li and Filer (2007) discuss the costs and benefits of two legal systems. What are these legal systems. What are their characteristics, and can we think of them as pure ends of the legal spectrum?
W11-2

Li and Filer (2007) discuss two broad governance or legal systems: rule-based systems (public ordering) and relation-based systems (private ordering). 
A rule-based system relies on formal laws, transparent legal procedures, independent courts, and impartial enforcement by the state. In these systems, businesses and investors depend mainly on public rules and legal contracts for protection. Rule-based systems also require strong public information infrastructure, such as free media, reliable accounting standards, and high public trust. The advantage of this system is that it lowers transaction costs for large-scale and impersonal business activities because contracts can be enforced efficiently through public institutions. 
In contrast, relation-based systems depend on private relationships, personal networks, and informal arrangements to protect property rights and conduct business. In these systems, public institutions are weaker, legal enforcement may be inconsistent, and investors rely more on trusted personal connections. Although this system may work in smaller or localized markets, transaction costs rise as business networks expand. 
Li and Filer argue that these systems should not be viewed as completely pure forms. Instead, governance exists on a continuum between public ordering and private ordering, and most countries contain elements of both systems. 

 
c. What will companies need to do differently once they have internationalized their technological activities into countries will operate under these two different legal systems?

Once firms internationalize their technological activities into countries with different legal systems, they must adapt how they manage innovation, protect intellectual property, and govern business relationships. In rule-based systems, companies can rely more heavily on formal contracts, patents, courts, and public institutions to protect their technological assets and investments. Strong legal enforcement, transparent regulations, reliable accounting standards, and independent courts reduce uncertainty and transaction costs. Therefore, multinational corporations can coordinate research activities through standardized procedures, formal agreements, and broader collaboration with external partners such as universities, suppliers, and research institutions.
In contrast, firms operating in relation-based or private-ordering systems must depend more on personal relationships, local networks, and direct managerial control. Because public enforcement mechanisms may be weak or inconsistent, firms cannot rely entirely on formal legal protections. Instead, they often need trusted local partners, stronger internal monitoring, and tighter control over technological knowledge. Companies may prefer wholly owned subsidiaries or direct investment because these provide greater private protection and operational control. 
Cantwell’s discussion of global technological networks also implies that multinational firms must learn to integrate geographically dispersed innovation centers while adapting to local institutional environments. Technology leaders are successful not only because they innovate, but because they can coordinate international learning across different governance systems. In rule-based systems, firms benefit from openness and collaboration, whereas in relation-based systems, success depends more on relationship management, trust-building, and careful protection of proprietary knowledge. Therefore, multinational firms must balance global integration with local institutional adaptation when internationalizing technological activities.

a. Roy and Oliver (2009) write about partner selection in joint ventures. They argue that the rule of laws leads to two types of partnering concerns. What are those two concerns?
W11-4

Roy and Oliver (2009) argue that the host-country rule of law influences two major partnering concerns in international joint ventures (IJVs): appropriation concerns and coordination cost concerns. 
The first concern is appropriation concerns. These refer to fears that a partner may behave opportunistically and unfairly capture benefits, resources, or profits from the joint venture. In countries with weak rule of law, property rights protection and contract enforcement are unreliable. As a result, firms worry that their partner may misuse knowledge, violate agreements, or take advantage of legal loopholes. Roy and Oliver argue that stronger rule of law reduces these concerns because firms can rely on courts and legal institutions to protect their interests. 
The second concern is coordination cost concerns. These involve the difficulties and costs of coordinating activities, sharing information, and managing interdependence between partners. Weak legal systems create ambiguity, uncertainty, and monitoring challenges, which increase the need for coordination and supervision between firms. In contrast, strong legal systems reduce uncertainty and make coordination easier and more efficient. 
Thus, rule of law affects IJVs by shaping both the risk of opportunism and the costs of coordinating collaborative activities.

 
b. Reur, Klijn and Liokas (2014) argue that there are two fundamental tensions in international joint ventures with respect to board involvement. Explain this tensions.
W1-4

Reuer, Klijn, and Lioukas (2014) argue that international joint ventures (IJVs) face a fundamental tension regarding the level of board involvement. This tension comes from two competing needs.
The first tension is the need for administrative control. Because IJVs involve two or more independent firms working together under incomplete contracts, there is always a risk of opportunism, conflict, and coordination problems between partners. Therefore, boards become important governance mechanisms that help monitor the venture, align partner interests, coordinate activities, and resolve disputes. The need for board involvement becomes even stronger when partners compete in the same markets or when the venture involves R&D activities, since these situations create higher risks of knowledge appropriation and conflict. 
The second tension is the need to delegate authority to local management. IJVs operate across countries and cultures, and local managers often possess better knowledge about the host-country environment, customer preferences, regulations, and operational conditions. When cultural differences or environmental uncertainty are high, heavy board involvement may slow decision-making and reduce flexibility. In such cases, delegating authority to local managers can improve efficiency and responsiveness. 
Thus, IJV boards must balance control and coordination with flexibility and local adaptation.


c. If you were to apply the arguments of Roy and Oliver to the article by Reuer et al, how would the rule of law affect board involvement?
W11-4, W1-4

Applying Roy and Oliver’s (2009) arguments to Reuer et al. (2014), the strength of the host-country rule of law would significantly influence the level of board involvement in international joint ventures (IJVs). Specifically, weaker rule of law would likely increase board involvement, while stronger rule of law would reduce the need for intensive board oversight.
Roy and Oliver explain that weak rule of law creates two major concerns for firms: appropriation concerns and coordination cost concerns. When legal systems are weak, firms cannot fully rely on courts, contract enforcement, or property-right protection. As a result, firms become more worried about opportunistic behavior, unfair appropriation of knowledge, and coordination difficulties with partners. Under these conditions, parent firms would likely increase board involvement to provide stronger administrative control over the venture.
This logic fits closely with Reuer et al.’s argument that boards act as ex post governance mechanisms that help monitor behavior, coordinate activities, and resolve conflicts in IJVs. If the host-country legal system is weak, boards would need to meet more frequently, monitor management more closely, and become more involved in strategic decisions to protect parent firms from exchange hazards and opportunism.
In contrast, when rule of law is strong, formal institutions reduce uncertainty and improve contract enforcement. This lowers appropriation and coordination concerns, reducing the need for heavy board monitoring. In such environments, firms can rely more on legal protections and formal governance systems rather than intensive board involvement. This would also support greater delegation of authority to local managers, which Reuer et al. argue becomes more efficient when monitoring costs are lower and local adaptation is important. 
Therefore, applying Roy and Oliver’s framework suggests a negative relationship between rule of law and board involvement: weaker legal systems increase board involvement, while stronger legal systems reduce it.

Question 3
According to Zaheer (1995), liability for foreignness can arise from at least four, nor necessarily independent sources. Identify and briefly explain three of them.
W5-1

Zaheer (1995) argues that multinational enterprises (MNEs) face a “liability of foreignness,” meaning that foreign firms experience additional costs that local firms do not face. She identifies four major sources of these disadvantages.
The first source is costs associated with spatial distance. These include transportation, communication, travel, and coordination costs that arise because firms operate across geographic distance and time zones. Managing activities across countries becomes more difficult and expensive compared to domestic firms.
The second source is firm-specific unfamiliarity with the local environment. Foreign firms often lack local knowledge, relationships, and experience in the host country. This can make it harder to understand customer preferences, regulations, and informal business practices.
The third source is host-country-based discrimination or lack of legitimacy. Foreign firms may face economic nationalism, distrust, or weaker legitimacy compared to domestic firms. Governments, customers, suppliers, or regulators may prefer local firms, creating disadvantages for foreign entrants.
Zaheer also notes a fourth source: restrictions imposed by the home country, such as export controls or political regulations. Overall, these liabilities reduce the competitiveness and profitability of foreign firms relative to local firms.


 
b. Using Delios and Beamish’s (1999) TCE logic, explain how the specificity of assets, and the complementaries of assets affect the ownership position of the MNE in the foreign market. Use the appropriate TCE language and develop the arguments for each category of assets (i.e., specificity and complementarity) in separate paragraphs. 
W4-1

Delios and Beamish (1999) use Transaction Cost Economics (TCE) to explain how asset specificity influences foreign ownership decisions. Asset specificity refers to firm-specific assets such as technological knowledge, R&D capabilities, marketing expertise, or proprietary know-how. According to TCE, highly specific assets are vulnerable to opportunism and unwanted dissemination if shared through market contracts. As asset specificity increases, transaction hazards also increase, making market-based exchange less efficient. Therefore, firms prefer higher ownership positions, such as wholly owned subsidiaries, because greater ownership provides stronger control and better protection of proprietary assets. Delios and Beamish argue that firms internalize transactions to minimize the risks associated with asset leakage and opportunistic behavior. 
In contrast, complementary assets have the opposite effect on ownership levels. Complementary assets are local assets that foreign firms need in order to compete effectively in the host country, such as distribution channels, government relationships, local market knowledge, or supplier networks. When these assets are difficult to obtain through markets because of transaction costs or market imperfections, firms often form joint ventures with local partners. In TCE terms, joint ventures become efficient governance structures because they allow firms to access complementary local assets while sharing costs and risks. As the need for complementary assets increases, firms are more likely to accept lower ownership positions in the foreign investment. 
Thus, TCE predicts that asset specificity increases ownership, while dependence on complementary assets decreases ownership.

a.	What Buckley 2002 said about losing importance of IB? (5 points) W2-4 
Buckley (2002) argued that International Business (IB) research was “running out of steam” because the field no longer had a clear “big question” that united scholars. He explained that earlier IB research was successful because it focused on three major questions: explaining foreign direct investment (FDI), explaining multinational enterprises (MNEs), and understanding globalization and internationalization. 
According to Buckley, these earlier questions had already been largely answered, and IB scholars had not yet identified a new central research problem. As a result, the field risked losing its distinct identity and influence compared to other management disciplines. He worried that IB research could become fragmented across areas such as strategy, economics, marketing, and sociology without a common focus. 
Buckley suggested several possible future questions, such as globalization trends, cultural differences, and the rise of nations like China, but he believed none had yet become dominant enough to guide the whole field. Therefore, he concluded that IB research needed a new “big question” to regain its intellectual energy and relevance. 

b.	What Peng 2004 said Being agree or disagree about some topics? (10 points) W2-3 
Peng (2004) agreed with Buckley that International Business (IB) research needs a “big question” to guide and unify the field. He believed that a strong central question helps scholars work together, improves scientific progress, and increases the prestige of IB research. 
However, Peng disagreed with Buckley’s conclusion that IB research was running out of steam. Peng argued that the field already has a continuing big question: “What determines the international success and failure of firms?” According to Peng, this question has always been at the center of IB research and will continue to guide future studies. 
Peng also disagreed with Buckley’s suggested future questions. For example, he argued that studying the rise of nations focuses too much on the country level instead of the firm level, which is the core of IB research. He also believed that globalization alone is too broad and overlaps heavily with other disciplines such as political economy and geography. 
Overall, Peng believed the IB field remained strong because it still studies how firms succeed internationally, which gives the discipline a unique comparative advantage. Therefore, he was much more optimistic about the future of IB research than Buckley. 

c.	Why cannot we see IB as a separate filed? (15 points)
International Business (IB) cannot be seen as a completely separate field because it is highly interdisciplinary and overlaps with many other business and social science disciplines. IB research studies international activities of firms, but to explain these activities it uses theories and methods from economics, strategy, finance, marketing, sociology, political science, and organizational behavior. (Caves, 1998)
Caves (1998) explained that IB “cuts across” traditional business disciplines instead of focusing on one specific type of decision. For example, IB research includes international strategy, cross-border finance, global marketing, and international HRM. Because of this broad scope, the field faces “centrifugal forces” that push researchers toward their own disciplines rather than toward a unified IB theory.
Buckley and Lessard (2005) also argued that IB operates at multiple levels of analysis, including the individual manager, the firm, the industry, and the institutional environment. Different IB studies focus on different levels and use different theoretical perspectives. Internalization theory focuses on firms, globalization research focuses on industries, and institutional theory focuses on the macro-environment. 
Another reason is that many IB topics are shared with other disciplines. For example, globalization is also studied in political economy and geography, while culture is studied in sociology and psychology. Peng (2004) argued that some proposed IB questions are not unique to IB because other disciplines already study them extensively. 
Therefore, IB is better understood as an integrative and interdisciplinary field rather than a completely independent discipline. Its strength comes from combining different theories and perspectives to explain international business phenomena.

Institutional characteristics shape the transaction costs MNEs face before and after entering a foreign market. In Meyer (2001)’s argument, institutions reduce uncertainty by creating stable “rules of the game.” But in transition economies, institutions are still developing, so rules are unclear and unstable. This increases the cost of searching, negotiating, contracting, monitoring, and enforcing agreements.

At the post-entry stage, weak protection of intellectual property is especially important. As a result, firms may prefer more hierarchical governance, such as wholly owned subsidiaries, to reduce exposure to unreliable institutions. 

c.	How do legal institutions affect MNE’s decisions to opt for specific governance solutions in IJV contracts when institutional differences are severe.
W4-5

Devarakonda et al. (2021) argue that legal institutions affect how MNEs design governance mechanisms in international joint venture contracts. When IJV partners come from countries with very different legal institutions, they face higher uncertainty about how disputes will be resolved. Courts may differ in legal tradition, fairness, procedures, enforcement time, enforcement cost, and protection of minority shareholders. Because of this uncertainty, partners are more likely to include arbitration provisions in their contracts instead of relying only on public courts. The hypotheses explain this logic. However, arbitration is not free. Partners must negotiate arbitration clauses, select rules, and accept the cost of private dispute resolution. If firms are already familiar with weaker institutions, they may understand court risks better and may not need arbitration as much.

b.	With which hypothesis does Cantwell (1995) agree and with which one does he disagree in his seminal paper on globalization and product cycle models? 
W8-1 (Like Test 003-Q1a)

Cantwell (1995) revisits two hypotheses from the product cycle model. The first hypothesis says that innovations are almost always located in the home country of the parent company, usually near the firm’s technological headquarters. In the product life cycle logic, this means that in the new product stage, innovation starts at home because the home market has advanced demand, skilled labor, R&D scale economies, and close links between research, production, and users. Then, as the product matures, production may move abroad to serve foreign markets or reduce costs. Cantwell rejects this hypothesis. The second hypothesis says that international dispersion of activity is led by technology leaders. Cantwell generally agrees with this hypothesis historically. Technology leaders have the strongest firm-specific advantages, so they are more able to internationalize. However, Cantwell updates the argument. In modern globalization, technology leaders are not only moving mature production abroad; they are also building international R&D networks to access foreign centers of excellence. Therefore, H1 is rejected, while H2 is supported but revised for a more globalized innovation system.

b.	In relation to market entry strategies, please explain two (out of three) key differences between the RBV and transaction cost economics? 
W3-4

Peng (2001) explains that the RBV and transaction cost economics both help explain foreign market entry, but they differ in three main ways.
First, TCE explains entry mode through external market failure, while RBV explains it through firm resource heterogeneity. In TCE, firms choose higher-control entry modes when external markets are inefficient because partners may act opportunistically or contracts may be hard to enforce. In RBV, the problem is not only opportunism. The issue is that firms have different resources and capabilities. A foreign partner may not have the ability to absorb, use, or protect the MNE’s knowledge. Therefore, a firm may choose FDI or a wholly owned subsidiary because its own capabilities are stronger than those of outside partners, not only because markets fail (Peng, 2001). 
Second, TCE is more static, while RBV is more dynamic and longitudinal. TCE often treats market entry as a one-time decision based on current transaction conditions, such as asset specificity, uncertainty, and risk of opportunism. RBV sees internationalization as a learning process. Each entry helps the firm develop new knowledge, routines, and capabilities that affect later entries. Thus, entry mode choice changes as the firm gains experience (Peng, 2001). 
Third, TCE focuses mainly on exploiting firm-specific advantages, while RBV focuses on both exploiting and developing advantages. TCE asks how the firm can best protect and use its existing assets abroad. RBV asks not only how the firm uses existing resources, but also how international expansion helps create new capabilities. Therefore, RBV is broader because it explains entry mode as part of capability building, learning, and long-term competitive advantage (Peng, 2001). 
 
c.	Knowledge is often regarded as a key resource in internationalization strategies. Please explain which two main assumptions of TCE, the evolutionary theory of the multinational corporation rejects. 
W3-1, W3-5

Kogut and Zander’s evolutionary theory treats the firm as a social community that creates, transfers, and recombines knowledge. In this view, the MNE exists not mainly because markets fail, but because firms are often better organizational vehicles for transferring knowledge across borders. Kogut and Zander (1993) argue that the firm’s productive knowledge creates comparative advantage, and that the transfer mode depends on the characteristics of knowledge, such as codifiability, complexity, and teachability. Less codifiable and harder-to-teach knowledge is more likely to be transferred internally through wholly owned operations. 
The first TCE assumption they challenge is opportunism. Traditional TCE argues that firms internalize transactions because external partners may behave opportunistically. Kogut and Zander reject the need for this assumption. They argue that internal transfer can be chosen simply because the firm is more efficient at transferring complex and tacit knowledge than outside parties. The second assumption is bounded rationality. Verbeke (2003) explains that Kogut and Zander force scholars to rethink both bounded rationality and opportunism in Williamsonian TCE. Their evolutionary view puts less emphasis on transaction cost minimization and more emphasis on value creation, learning, and capability development. 
Thus, the evolutionary theory rejects the idea that MNE strategy is explained only by transaction hazards. Instead, it argues that knowledge, learning, and organizational capability explain why MNEs exist and how they choose entry or technology transfer modes.

1. Introduction
Davis (1971) - That?s interesting!
Davis (1971) distinguishes ?interesting? from ?non-interesting? theories: interesting theories deny an audience?s taken-for-granted assumptions, while non-interesting ones merely affirm them. He proposes a ?sociology of the interesting? that traces how an audience?s mind moves from one accepted theory to another, anchored in a dialectical contrast between seeming and being. Davis develops twelve logical categories,organization, composition, abstraction, generalization, stabilization, function, evaluation, co-relation, co-existence, co-variation, opposition, and causation,that index the structure of interesting propositions. He explores how laymen-versus-expert assumptions complicate universal appeal and offers a consensus-creation technique that articulates apparent dissensus into a higher-level shared assumption, which is then refuted to produce a broadly interesting claim.
Robinson (1981) - Background of IB: World War II to present
Robinson (1981) provides a historical overview of international business since World War II, identifying four periods defined by the number and type of decision-relevant actors. The two-actor era (1945?1955) featured U.S. firms and foreign partners; the three-actor era (1955?1970) added host states as MNCs proliferated; the four-actor ?time of troubles? of the 1970s introduced parent states amid Bretton Woods collapse, resource nationalism, and rising activism; and the early 1980s ushered in a multi-actor era of NGOs, unions, and transnational regulatory bodies. Robinson predicts a shift from ownership-heavy MNCs to leaner international service and trading firms competing on information access, and criticizes management education for clinging to outdated MNC-centric models.
Vernon (1994) - IB curriculum
Vernon (1994) reflects on the evolution of international business research and curriculum from the postwar era to the early 1990s. He identifies three early areas: foreign trade challenges for national firms, headquarters management of multinational enterprises, and comparative business practices abroad, with the third long neglected because of assumed U.S. universalism. Through the Multinational Enterprise Project at Harvard, Vernon helped institutionalize MNE scholarship and trained a generation of IB researchers. He critiques the premature dissolution of dedicated international divisions in firms and business schools, arguing that functional departments lacked capacity for genuine global integration, and calls for revived comparative business systems research that escapes U.S.-centric blind spots.
Reuer, Klijn, and Lioukas (2014) - Board involvement in IJVs
Reuer, Klijn, and Lioukas (2014) examine the determinants of board involvement in international joint ventures (IJVs), integrating corporate governance and alliance governance literatures. IJV boards act as formal control mechanisms that coordinate activities, monitor performance, and reconcile parent-firm objectives, but they may also delegate authority when local knowledge is critical. Four hypotheses are tested: board involvement rises with end-market overlap between parents and with R&D activities that heighten coordination needs and knowledge-leakage risk, and falls with parent cultural distance and host-country uncertainty that limit central oversight. All four hypotheses receive empirical support. Governance design should reflect strategic stakes and environmental complexity rather than apply uniformly across ventures.


2. Future
Caves (1998) - Research on IB
Caves (1998) surveys the methodological challenges and research opportunities facing international business, centered on the multinational enterprise. He highlights three problems: the difficulty of controlling for opportunity sets when firm strategies are endogenous, survivor bias from focusing on still-operating MNEs, and chronic shortages of longitudinal firm-level data. He encourages greater use of census and national microdata despite their difficulty. Looking ahead, he urges work on market-specific managerial capabilities driving new FDI forms, MNE financial flows and exchange-rate responses, technology and development effects on host countries, and trust and governance in alliances and joint ventures. Caves calls for breadth of subject matter coupled with analytical rigor, foreshadowing Buckley (2002).
Buckley (2002) - Research agenda running out
Buckley (2002) warns that international business research has lost momentum and lacks a unifying ?big question? to anchor future scholarship. He maps three historical phases,foreign direct investment, the multinational enterprise, and globalization,each driven by a salient empirical puzzle and matched theoretical innovations such as internalization theory (Buckley & Casson, 1976) and Vernon?s product cycle. Today, however, the agenda has drifted toward conceptual proliferation and description, turning IB into a ?funnel? field that imports more than it exports. To regain intellectual identity, Buckley proposes four candidate empirical questions on national sequencing, cultural distinctiveness, measuring globalization, and the future of capitalism, urging a return to stylized facts.
Peng (2004) - The big question
Peng (2004) responds to Buckley?s (2002) claim that international business has lost its big question, arguing instead that IB has always been organized around a single unifying inquiry: what determines firms? international success and failure. He recasts Buckley?s three historical waves,FDI flows, the nature of MNEs, and globalization,as expressions of this enduring question rather than separate paradigms. Peng critiques Buckley?s three candidate questions as nation-level, conceptually ambiguous, or merely methodological. Drawing on Kuhn?s (1962) view of scholarly maturation, he urges IB to embrace its firm-level comparative advantage and remain interdisciplinary, rejecting Buckley?s ?scientific mercantilism.? Open intellectual exchange, not boundary protection, will sustain IB?s relevance and vitality.
Buckley and Lessard (2005) - Regaining the edge
Buckley and Lessard (2005) diagnose a ?missing middle? in international business research: a widening gap between subject-oriented global phenomena and IB-specific theory development. They argue that scholars increasingly explain outsourcing, emerging markets, and globalization using adjacent frameworks,transaction cost economics, the resource-based view, institutional theory,without translating them into distinctly international insights, leaving IB intellectually marginalized. Restoring the historically productive recursion between problem and theory, exemplified by internalization theory (Buckley & Casson, 1976), requires that every theory-driven IB paper explicitly state its derivation and contribution, however incremental. They also urge clearer differentiation between general theories with broad reach and specific theories grounded in particular industries or organizational forms.
Griffith, Cavusgil, and Xu (2008) - Emerging themes
Griffith, Cavusgil, and Xu (2008) respond to Buckley?s (2002) claim that international business has exhausted its big questions, arguing instead that earlier questions have not yet been fully resolved and require renewed engagement rather than abandonment. Combining a citation analysis of six leading IB-related journals (1996?2006) with a Delphi study of prolific scholars, they map the field?s intellectual core, transaction cost economics, Dunning?s eclectic paradigm, Hofstede?s cultural dimensions, the Uppsala process, and resource- and knowledge-based views, and chart emerging directions. Promising future themes include firm performance and knowledge management, the measurement of globalization, emerging-market multinationals, and born-global entrepreneurs, calling for multilevel and methodologically rigorous theory-driven contributions.

3. Economic Theories
Kogut and Zander (1993) - Knowledge of the firm and evolutionary theory
Kogut and Zander (1993) develop an evolutionary, knowledge-based theory of the multinational corporation, framing firms as social communities specialized in creating, transferring, and recombining knowledge. Challenging the internalization view (Buckley & Casson, 1976; Williamson, 1975), they argue that MNCs exist and expand internationally because they transfer tacit knowledge more efficiently than markets. Three knowledge attributes, codifiability, teachability, and complexity, predict whether technology is transferred internally to wholly owned subsidiaries or externally through licensing. Empirically, less codifiable, less teachable, and more complex knowledge stays inside the firm. They position knowledge-based and transaction-cost perspectives as complementary, treating MNCs as path-dependent repositories of capability that evolve through cumulative experiential learning across borders.
Shaver and Flyer (2000) - Agglomeration economies, firm heterogeneity, and FDI
Shaver and Flyer (2000) challenge the conventional view that geographic clustering uniformly enhances firm performance through agglomeration economies (Marshall, 1920; Porter, 1998). Introducing firm heterogeneity as a critical moderator, they argue that strong firms with superior technology, human capital, and supply networks already internalize many of the benefits clusters provide and risk leaking valuable resources to rivals, while weaker firms benefit disproportionately. This produces adverse selection: capable firms avoid clusters, weaker ones gravitate to them. Empirically, using foreign greenfield investments in U.S. manufacturing, they confirm that less capable firms cluster more, more capable firms locate independently, and non-clustering firms exhibit higher survival rates, especially in strongly agglomerated industries.
Griffith and Harvey (2001) - Resource perspective of dynamic capabilities
Griffith and Harvey (2001) introduce the concept of global dynamic capabilities (GDC), defined as a firm?s ability to assemble difficult-to-imitate combinations of resources across markets while maintaining global coherence and local responsiveness. Integrating the resource-based view (Barney, 1991; Wernerfelt, 1984) with the market-based view (Srivastava et al., 1998), they argue that power in international relationships is the mechanism through which GDCs are realized. Four constructs anchor their model: asset specificity, predictability, market knowledge gaps, and market type. Tested on foreign distributors of U.S. manufacturers, three of four hypotheses are supported: higher asset specificity, predictability, and knowledge gaps strengthen distributor power, integrating internal and external resource perspectives.
Peng (2001) - Resource-based view
Peng (2001) positions the resource-based view (RBV) as a transformative theoretical innovation in international business and frames its diffusion using Rogers?s (1983) innovation framework: relative advantage, compatibility, simplicity, testability, and observability. He compares RBV with transaction cost economics, noting that TCE treats internalization as a response to opportunism while RBV emphasizes resource heterogeneity, dynamic capability building, and continuous learning. A 2?2 framework organizes RBV-based IB research by firm size and international experience, generating four research streams that include large MNEs, large but inexperienced emerging-market firms, born globals, and small domestic firms. IB scholarship reciprocally enriches RBV through insights on local embeddedness, path-dependent learning, and overcoming liability of foreignness.
Verbeke (2003) - Evolutionary view of the MNE and future of internalization theory
Verbeke (2003) revisits Kogut and Zander?s (1993) knowledge-based theory of the multinational enterprise and proposes an evolutionary synthesis with transaction cost economics. He argues that a firm?s technological competence base and governance structure co-evolve through organizational routines that coordinate learning and control. Kogut and Zander?s contributions, broadening MNE analysis beyond transaction-cost minimization, qualifying opportunism and bounded rationality, and embedding decisions in path-dependent routines, enrich rather than supplant internalization theory. The choice between transaction cost and knowledge-based lenses should depend on research purpose: TCE for boundary and governance decisions, KBV for learning and capability development. Within Dunning?s (1980) OLI paradigm, ?ownership? aligns with KBV while ?internalization? remains TCE-anchored.
Dunning (2009) - Location and the MNE
Dunning (2009) reasserts location as the neglected pillar of the OLI paradigm. Three structural shifts, the rise of intellectual capital, deepening globalization, and reorganization of capitalism, have changed where and why firms invest. Strategic-asset-seeking FDI in knowledge-based sectors increasingly seeks location-bound complementary assets such as skilled labor, R&D networks, and innovation clusters rather than natural endowments. Spatial transaction costs and clustering economies make location itself a dynamic competitive advantage. Dunning critiques static trade theories anchored in factor immobility and revises Vernon (1966), urging researchers and policymakers to integrate spatial dimensions into firm strategy and to build place-based assets such as institutional quality, R&D infrastructure, and education systems.


4. Institutions, FDI
Delios and Beamish (1999) - Ownership of Japanese firms: transactional, institutional
Delios and Beamish (1999) compare strong and weak institutional environment. Integrating transaction cost economics, internalization theory (Buckley & Casson, 1976), institutional theory, and experiential learning (Johanson & Vahlne, 1977), they argue that ownership decisions go beyond protecting firm-specific advantages. Empirically, institutional and experience-based variables predict ownership levels more consistently than transaction-related ones; asset specificity shows weak and sometimes counterintuitive effects, possibly reflecting relational governance through keiretsu networks. Firms operating in environments with strong intellectual property protection and those with deeper host-country experience tend toward wholly owned subsidiaries, revealing the multidimensional nature of foreign equity decisions.
Meyer (2001) - Institutions, transaction costs
Meyer (2001) integrates institutional theory and transaction cost economics to explain entry-mode choices in transition economies, drawing on Eastern European evidence. Unstable, ambiguous, and rapidly changing institutions raise search, negotiation, and contracting costs, pushing multinational firms toward hierarchical entry such as wholly owned subsidiaries to safeguard tacit knowledge and intellectual property. Internalization, however, imposes adjustment and cultural-adaptation costs, so joint ventures and network arrangements may be preferred early in transition when local knowledge is scarce. As institutions strengthen, through better contract enforcement and reduced regulatory uncertainty, transaction costs fall, encouraging firms to migrate toward higher-control entries. Some transaction costs stem not from institutions but from the inherent tacitness of managerial knowledge.
Buckley et al. (2007) - Chinese outward FDI
The study builds on the two general principles of FDI theory. 1. Internalize imperfect or missing external market. 2. Choose location that minimize overall costs of operation. Buckley et al. (2007) provide one of the first comprehensive empirical tests of motives behind China?s outward foreign direct investment during its transition to a market economy. Using panel data on 49 host countries and twelve hypotheses, they find that market-seeking motives dominate: larger host markets significantly attract investment, while traditional resource- and strategic-asset-seeking variables are not significant. Cultural proximity, proxied by ethnic Chinese populations, raises ODI. Counterintuitively, host-country political risk is negatively associated and inflation positively associated with ODI, indicating Chinese firms tolerate, or are drawn to, environments that deter Western multinationals. Exports complement investment while imports substitute for it, revealing state-influenced, capital-market-imperfect drivers of Chinese internationalization.
Peng et al. (2008) - Institution-based view
Peng, Wang, and Jiang (2008) advocate integrating an institution-based view (IBV) into international business strategy alongside the industry-based and resource-based perspectives, forming a ?strategy tripod.? Defining institutions as the formal and informal ?rules of the game? (North, 1990; Scott, 1995), they argue that emerging economies? institutional transitions, reforms in property rights, corporate law, and transparency, directly shape firm strategy and performance. Four illustrations anchor the framework: antidumping as institutionalized liability of foreignness; India?s IT/BPO rise; corporate growth in China where guanxi substitutes for weak formal institutions; and concentrated ownership governance with principal?principal conflicts. The IBV complements rather than replaces existing theories, helping firms both adapt to and shape evolving institutional environments.
Devarakonda et al. (2021) - Legal institutions and the design of dispute resolution
Devarakonda, McCann, and Reuer (2017) examine why partners in international joint ventures adopt private arbitration clauses ex ante. Grounding their argument in transaction cost economics, they argue that when partners come from countries with different legal traditions, procedural uncertainty in courts, high litigation costs, or weak shareholder protection, public courts become inefficient and unpredictable. Arbitration provides a neutral, flexible, and faster alternative. Empirically, using 104 IJVs, they show that institutional distance between partners? home countries increases the likelihood of arbitration clauses, whereas board-based governance responds more to transactional features such as asset specificity. Different governance instruments thus address distinct uncertainties, institutional versus transactional, deepening understanding of contractual foresight in cross-border partnerships. In IJV, the weaker or more uncertain the legal institutional framework in host country, the greater the involvement of board in dispute resolution and governance.

5. Sociological Theories
Zaheer (1995) - Liability of foreignness
Zaheer (1995) provides one of the earliest empirical demonstrations of the liability of foreignness (LOF), the additional costs faced by multinational subsidiaries relative to local firms. LOF arises from spatial distance, unfamiliarity with the host market, host-country discrimination, and home-country reputational constraints. Comparing Western banks in New York with Japanese banks in Tokyo, she tests whether foreign subsidiaries fare better by importing firm-specific organizational practices (resource-based view) or by mimicking local routines (institutional theory). The evidence confirms LOF and supports the importation hypothesis: subsidiaries that retained parent-firm market or bureaucratic controls outperformed those that imitated host-country routines, challenging the assumption that isomorphic adaptation always enhances local legitimacy and performance.

Kostova and Zaheer (1999) - Organizational legitimacy
Kostova and Zaheer (1999) develop a theoretical framework for understanding the legitimacy of multinational enterprises across multiple institutional environments. Building on Scott (1995), who distinguishes regulatory, normative, and cognitive pillars, they separate external legitimacy at MNE and subunit levels from internal legitimacy granted by other parts of the firm. Three sources of complexity, environmental, organizational, and legitimation-process, generate ten propositions. Institutional distance between home and host increases legitimacy challenges; geocentric MNEs manage tensions better than ethnocentric or polycentric ones; subsidiary survival depends on both internal and external acceptance. Because stakeholders rely on heuristics, negative legitimacy spillovers across subsidiaries or co-national MNEs are stronger than positive ones, making reputation a central concern.

Guler et al. (2002) - Global competition
Guler, GuillÇn, and Macpherson (2002) examine the cross-national diffusion of ISO 9000 quality certification, framing it as both a technical and an institutional process. Drawing on new institutional theory, they identify three isomorphic mechanisms: coercive pressure from powerful governments and multinational customers, normative pressure from technical and scientific knowledge, and mimetic pressure rooted in role-equivalent competitive positioning across the global trade network. Empirically, coercive and mimetic pressures, particularly trade ties with early adopters, drive adoption, whereas the normative scientific-knowledge channel finds little support. Their findings show institutional isomorphism operating transnationally rather than within national boundaries and reveal that trade policy and FDI flows shape how organizational practices spread.

Li et al. (2008) - Do managerial ties in China produce value?
Li, Poppo, and Zhou (2008) examine whether managerial ties, relationships with business partners and government officials, uniformly benefit firms in China?s emerging economy. Drawing on social capital and social network theory, they propose a contingency view in which the value of ties depends on firm ownership, competitive intensity, and structural uncertainty. Empirically, domestic firms show a monotonic positive relationship between ties and performance, while foreign firms exhibit an inverted-U: moderate ties help, but heavy reliance produces diminishing returns, reflecting liability of foreignness. Competitive intensity erodes the strategic value of ties for everyone, while structural uncertainty enhances it. The study refines social network theory by emphasizing context-dependent returns to relational capital.

Johanson and Vahlne (2009) - Uppsala internationalization: liability of foreignness and outsidership
Johanson and Vahlne (2009) revise their 1977 Uppsala internationalization process model to reflect a network-based view of business. They reframe the central friction in foreign expansion from psychic distance to liability of outsidership: the disadvantage of being outside the relevant business network rather than merely unfamiliar with a host market. Markets are reconceptualized as interconnected webs of relationships in which insiders enjoy privileged access to knowledge, trust, and opportunity. The revised model retains state and change variables while introducing relationship-specific knowledge, affective commitment, and an explicit focus on opportunity development rather than risk avoidance. Internationalization paths become nonlinear, driven by ongoing relational learning and trust-based commitments.

6. Political Theories

Fagre and Wells (1982) - Bargaining power
Fagre and Wells (1982) analyze bargaining between multinational enterprises and host governments in developing countries, treating foreign ownership share as the visible outcome of negotiation. They argue that MNE bargaining power rests on firm-specific resources: technological intensity, product differentiation through advertising and brand equity, access to export markets and global coordination, capital resources, and product diversity. Greater intra-industry competition among MNEs erodes individual leverage, allowing host governments to play firms off one another. Empirically, R&D intensity, advertising, multi-product operations, and export coordination predict higher equity stakes, while industry rivalry lowers them. Their framework adds nuance to Dunning?s (1980) OLI paradigm by reconceptualizing ownership as a negotiable, externally contingent outcome.

Boddewyn and Brewer (1994) - Political behavior
Boddewyn and Brewer (1994) reframe political behavior as a core strategic dimension of international business rather than a reactive one. Crossing borders exposes multinationals to contradictory political governance environments, where firms acquire and deploy power to pursue efficiency, market power, and legitimacy. They classify political behavior into four forms, compliance, avoidance, conflict, and partnership, shaped by firm capabilities, industry structure, and the nonmarket context. Six propositions link these forms to bargaining stakes, leverage opportunities, and political competencies. Revising the traditional spatial assumption, they argue that sovereign states actively mediate market access, so the first move in cross-border activity is inherently political. Researchers should treat political strategy as a fundamental analytical category.

Ellstrand et al. (2002) - Board structure and political risk
Ellstrand, Tihanyi, and Johnson (2002) draw on agency theory to examine how board structure shapes the political risk profile of a firm?s foreign direct investment portfolio. Because diversifiable shareholders prefer higher-return strategies while undiversifiable managers favor caution, board composition mediates these competing preferences. Using a sample of U.S. manufacturing MNEs, they find that boards with more inside directors and those exhibiting CEO duality are associated with significantly lower political risk in international portfolios, consistent with managerial risk aversion. The hypothesized effect for affiliated directors is not supported, suggesting their influence on internationalization decisions is limited. Boards thus actively shape, rather than passively monitor, exposure to political uncertainty.

Li et al. (2009) - Competitive position, managerial ties, and profitability
Li, Zhou, and Shao (2009) study how foreign firms in China achieve profitability by combining competitive positioning, Porter?s (1980) differentiation and low-cost strategies, with managerial ties. Differentiation pays in China because consumers perceive foreign products as superior and many domestic competitors crowd into homogeneous low-quality niches. Low-cost positioning also works when efficiency advantages emerge from China?s manufacturing base. Critically, they distinguish political ties from business ties: political connections impose conformity costs, distract from competitive execution, and undermine both differentiation and low-cost strategies. Business ties, by contrast, reduce liability of foreignness, transfer local knowledge, and complement strategic positioning. Foreign firms should invest in business networks while avoiding excessive dependence on political relationships.

Holburn and Zelner (2010) - Political capabilities
Holburn and Zelner (2010) examine how multinational enterprises manage policy risk in international investment. They argue that firms vary in responsiveness to host-country political hazards because their political capabilities, competencies for navigating policy processes, are shaped by organizational learning and cognitive imprinting in their home country. Through six hypotheses tested in the global electric power industry, they show that firms from home countries with weaker institutional constraints, ethnic divisions, and income inequality develop stronger political capabilities and are significantly more likely to enter risky host markets. The study advances the resource-based view by recasting political capability as a firm-specific advantage and bridges institutional theory with internationalization strategy under hazardous environments.

7. Capital Markets

Miller and Reuer (1998) - Foreign exchange rate movements
Miller and Reuer (1998) examine how firm-specific strategies, rather than industry-wide attributes, shape economic exposure to exchange rate fluctuations, defined as the sensitivity of future cash flows and firm value to unexpected currency movements. They challenge the use of a single trade-weighted index as a global FX proxy, arguing that exposure is location-specific. Empirically, higher export intensity raises exposure, while greater foreign direct investment and R&D intensity reduce it by localizing operations and creating pricing power through product differentiation. Industry foreign-sales intensity also amplifies exposure. The findings recast currency risk as actively manageable through strategic decisions, FDI, R&D investment, and geographic configuration, rather than an industry-level external shock.

Purda (2008) - Risk perception and financial system
Purda (2008) examines whether a country?s financial system orientation, bank-based versus market-based, shapes firm-level credit risk as captured by credit ratings. She argues that bank-based systems create closer lender?borrower relationships, granting banks privileged access to private information, enabling early detection of distress, and supporting informal renegotiation outside costly formal bankruptcy. Market-based systems, in contrast, rely on arm?s-length public-market financing. Empirically, after controlling for size, leverage, profitability, asset tangibility, and political connections, firms in bank-based systems receive better credit ratings. Strikingly, formal legal creditor protections lose explanatory power once system orientation is controlled for, suggesting that informal monitoring and renegotiation outweigh statutory safeguards in shaping perceived credit risk.

Hutson and Stevenson (2010) - Openness, hedging incentives, and exchange exposure
Hutson and Stevenson (2010) examine how national institutions shape firm-level foreign exchange exposure, asking whether trade openness intensifies currency exposure and whether strong creditor protection reduces it. Distinguishing direct exposure (foreign-currency cash flows) from indirect competitive exposure (shifts in competitive position due to currency moves), they argue indirect exposure is harder to measure and hedge. Their multi-country firm-level analysis finds a strong positive association between trade openness and FX exposure, and a negative association with creditor rights, since high bankruptcy costs incentivize active hedging. Shareholder rights matter less consistently. The findings highlight institutional context, particularly governance quality, as a key driver of corporate risk management beyond firm characteristics.

Hejazi and Santor (2010) - Foreign asset risk exposure, DOI, and performance
Hejazi and Santor (2010) revisit the assumed causal link between degree of internationalization (DOI) and firm performance, testing whether internationalization improves performance or whether better-performing firms simply internationalize more. Using ROA, ROE, and Tobin?s q and distinguishing low-risk foreign securities from riskier loans and deposits, they find that strong domestic performance precedes foreign expansion, consistent with internalization theory. Performance gains stem from exposure to risky foreign assets rather than diversification of low-risk holdings, suggesting a risk premium rather than a pure geographic-diversification benefit. The findings caution managers against treating observed DOI?performance correlations as causal and against assuming that international expansion creates value unless incremental returns outweigh added risk.

8. Globalization

Cantwell (1995) - The globalization of technology: product cycle model
Cantwell (1995) revisits the product cycle model in light of more than a century of patent data and technological globalization. He challenges two core assumptions: that innovation originates almost exclusively in the parent?s home country, and that technology leaders alone diffuse innovation abroad. Historical evidence shows innovation has long been geographically dispersed within multinationals, though home countries continue to shape R&D direction. Modern technology leaders now actively organize globally integrated innovation networks rather than passively exporting domestic advances. Introducing the concept of ?locational hierarchy,? Cantwell recasts the MNC as a decentralized yet integrated system of innovation nodes, extending Vernon (1966) while preserving the influence of national institutional context.

Florida (1997) - The globalization of R&D
Florida (1997) reframes the globalization of innovation by analyzing foreign-affiliated R&D laboratories in the United States. Challenging demand-side explanations rooted in supporting local production or adapting products, he shows that supply-side, technology-driven motives now dominate: foreign firms seek access to U.S. scientific and technical human capital and to regional innovation ecosystems. R&D motives are sector-specific, biotechnology and information technology rely on basic science, while automotive and chemicals lean toward applied development, so foreign R&D investment is heterogeneous rather than uniform. Foreign laboratories adopt U.S.-style organizational practices to attract talent and protect creativity, mirroring local norms rather than replicating home-country systems and reinforcing the role of innovation clusters in the spatial division of knowledge work.
Christmann and Taylor (2001) - Globalization and environment: self-regulation in China
Christmann and Taylor (2001) challenge the ?pollution haven? hypothesis by examining environmental self-regulation among firms in developing countries, using survey data from China. They argue that globalization generates institutional and market pressures that push firms to exceed local environmental requirements voluntarily. MNE subsidiaries adopt stricter practices to protect parent reputation, meet higher legitimacy expectations, deploy global standards such as ISO 14000, and capture operational efficiencies. For Chinese domestic firms, exposure through exports to developed markets and sales to multinational customers transmits coercive pressure along the supply chain, especially from European and Japanese buyers. Empirical tests of seven hypotheses confirm that foreign ownership and customer exposure raise compliance and ISO adoption.

Ghemawat (2003) - Semi-globalization
Ghemawat (2003) introduces ?semiglobalization? to describe the actual state of cross-border integration, occupying the middle ground between full integration and complete fragmentation. Empirically, trade, FDI, and capital flows have grown substantially yet remain bounded by national borders, cultural distance, regulatory variation, and institutional heterogeneity. This persistent friction makes international strategy distinct from domestic strategy: firms must navigate cross-border differences in regulation, consumer preferences, and infrastructure while exploiting them through adaptation, geographic arbitrage, and localized supply chains. Residual barriers can be strategic assets, allowing firms to build trust through local embeddedness. Responding to Buckley (2002), Ghemawat positions semiglobalization as a core concept that restores distinctiveness to international business research.

9. National Culture

Hofstede (1983) - Cultural relativity
Hofstede (1983) critiques the universal application of management theories and argues that organizational behavior is embedded in national culture, defined as collective mental programming acquired early in life. Rejecting the convergence hypothesis, he identifies four dimensions, power distance, individualism versus collectivism, masculinity versus femininity, and uncertainty avoidance, that systematically shape leadership, organizational structure, and motivation. Hierarchical structures dominate high-power-distance cultures; individualistic cultures reward achievement, while collectivist cultures emphasize face and group identification. He warns that Western, particularly U.S., management theories reflect low-power-distance and high-individualism assumptions and can be ineffective when transferred without adaptation. Despite later concerns about IBM-sample generalizability, the framework remains foundational for cross-cultural management research. Three of the dimensions can be made concrete. Individualism versus collectivism contrasts societies that prize personal goals, independence, and individual achievement with those in which people are tightly tied to in-groups, family, organization, community, and loyalty and harmony dominate, with wealthier countries tending to score higher on individualism. Power distance captures how readily a society accepts unequal distributions of power: high-power-distance cultures normalize hierarchy and discourage subordinates from challenging authority, while low-power-distance cultures expect consultation, equality, and participative decision-making. Uncertainty avoidance measures comfort with ambiguity and risk: strong-uncertainty-avoidance cultures prefer rules, structure, predictability, and security, whereas weak-uncertainty-avoidance cultures tolerate ambiguity and embrace change.

Ronen and Shenkar (1985) - Clustering countries on attitudinal dimensions
Ronen and Shenkar (1985) synthesize eight comparative management studies to cluster countries based on employees? work-related attitudes, values, and goals. They identify four functions of clustering, labeling, displaying, summarizing, and predicting, and emphasize relative rather than absolute proximity, favoring techniques such as multidimensional scaling and smallest space analysis that accommodate ordinal data. Four structural variables consistently shape clustering: geography, language, religion, and technological development. Stable clusters include Anglo, Nordic, Germanic, Latin European, and Latin American groupings, while the Far East and Arab clusters are less well-defined; Israel and Japan stand alone. The framework remains foundational for cross-cultural management and offers practical guidance for multinational HR and leadership adaptation.

Shenkar et al. (2008) - From ?distance? to ?friction?: metaphors
Shenkar, Luo, and Yeheskel (2008) critique the ?cultural distance? metaphor that has dominated international management since Kogut and Singh (1988) and propose replacing it with ?cultural friction.? Distance treats culture as a static, unidirectional barrier aggregated across Hofstede?s dimensions, obscuring power asymmetries and reciprocal cultural construction. Friction, grounded in social constructivism, conceptualizes cross-cultural encounters as dynamic, context-specific interactions involving clash, negotiation, and accommodation. Their framework comprises four elements, MNE and host-country constituencies, cultural carriers such as expatriates, contact points like acquisitions or joint ventures, and ongoing exchange processes. The reorientation surfaces local voices, political resistance, and institutional embeddedness, addressing limitations of transaction cost economics in cross-cultural analyses.

Tung and Verbeke (2010) - Beyond Hofstede and GLOBE
Tung and Verbeke (2010) critique the dominant use of Hofstede?s cultural dimensions and GLOBE indices in cross-cultural research, identifying ten problematic assumptions across three categories. Generic constraints include the misguided assumption of symmetry and temporal stability of cultural distance. Methodological weaknesses cover linearity, direct causal effects on managerial choice and performance, and conflation with psychic distance. Four ?masks? address spatial homogeneity within nations, the presumed negativity of distance, uniform firm-level effects, and aggregation into single indices such as Kogut and Singh (1988). They urge researchers to disaggregate cultural dimensions and to consider alternative frameworks such as Schwartz, Inglehart, and Trompenaars, treating culture as a multilayered, evolving construct. The ten assumptions fall into three labelled categories. Type I (generic limitations) covers broad problems such as the symmetry assumption (distance from A to B equals distance from B to A, contradicted, for example, by German managers adjusting differently in the U.S. than American managers in Germany) and the stability assumption (cultural scores do not change over time, ignoring cultural evolution). Type II (remediable weaknesses in empirical design) covers issues fixable through better research design, including assumed linearity and direct causality between cultural distance and outcomes such as entry mode or performance, when in fact higher distance may produce learning or complementarities rather than mechanical decline. Type III (weaknesses requiring reconceptualization) covers deeper theoretical problems, assuming national cultures are homogeneous (ignoring intra-country variation in countries like China or the U.S.), that distance is always negative, that firm characteristics do not matter, and that the dimensions can be collapsed into a single index.

10. Social Issues

Husted and Allen (2006) - Corporate social responsibility
Husted and Allen (2006) examine how multinational enterprises manage corporate social responsibility across subsidiaries, contrasting a strategic approach to CSR with an institutional one. Drawing on Bartlett and Ghoshal?s (1989) typology of multidomestic, global, international, and transnational strategies, they test whether MNEs evaluate CSR issues independently of product-market strategy or replicate their existing organizational logic onto social engagement. Empirically, hypothesis 1 (strategic independence) is rejected, while hypothesis 2 (institutional alignment) is supported: multidomestic and transnational firms emphasize local CSR, while global MNEs prioritize globally recognized issues such as environmental sustainability. They urge global MNEs to adopt a transnational CSR posture that balances global standards with local needs.
Cuervo-Cazurra (2006) - Corruption
Cuervo-Cazurra (2006) shows that host-country corruption reshapes the composition, not merely the volume, of foreign direct investment. Drawing on the competing ?grease in the wheels? and ?sand in the wheels? perspectives, he argues that investors? sensitivity to corruption depends on the institutional environment of the home country. Empirically, FDI from signatories of the OECD anti-bribery convention declines toward corrupt destinations because of legal, financial, and reputational risks, whereas FDI from highly corrupt home countries is undeterred or even attracted by familiar institutional conditions. The findings extend Dunning?s (1980) OLI paradigm by emphasizing institutional heterogeneity in the location component and refine the liability-of-foreignness literature, exposing the limits of one-size-fits-all FDI-corruption analyses.
Vachani et al.  (2009) - NGOs? influence on MNEs? social development: transaction costs
Vachani, Doh, and Teegen (2009) apply transaction cost economics (Coase, 1937; Williamson, 1985) to analyze how non-governmental organizations shape multinational enterprises? social development strategies. NGOs can raise or reduce the costs of addressing social externalities depending on whether they advocate against or collaborate with firms. They classify MNE strategies along two dimensions, level (basic versus enhanced) and posture (reactive, responsive, proactive), and develop eight propositions linking NGO influence to host-country institutional development, institutional distance, and institutional dynamics. Advocacy NGOs in developed contexts raise transaction costs, while operational NGOs in weaker contexts reduce them. Proactive postures and NGO alliances minimize transaction costs, particularly in dynamically changing institutional environments.
Campbell et al. (2012) - Multinationals and corporate social responsibility
Campbell, Eden, and Miller (2012) examine whether distance between home and host country shapes the corporate social responsibility of foreign affiliates, using a CRA-rated sample of foreign banks in the United States. Within the liability of foreignness tradition, CSR is conceptualized as a nonmarket legitimacy strategy. Although legitimacy theory predicts that greater distance heightens CSR engagement, the authors find the opposite: cultural, administrative, geographic, and economic distances (Ghemawat, 2001) all reduce the likelihood of high CSR engagement. They explain the paradox through weakened managerial empathy and rising operational costs at distance. Prior CSR reputation moderates this relationship, sustaining engagement among firms with established stakeholder commitments.

11. Governments

La Porta et al. (1998) - Law and finance
La Porta, Lopez-de-Silanes, Shleifer, and Vishny (1998) empirically link legal origin to financial development across 49 countries. They show that common-law systems provide stronger shareholder and creditor protections than civil-law systems, particularly the French tradition, and that these differences persist after controlling for economic development. Better legal protection of investors correlates with larger and more active capital markets, more dispersed ownership, and deeper debt markets. Enforcement quality matters as much as substantive rules: countries with efficient and honest legal systems achieve better financial outcomes regardless of legal family. Their findings challenge culture- and politics-based explanations of financial underdevelopment, positioning legal infrastructure, often inherited through colonialism, as a fundamental institutional determinant.

Li and Filer (2007) - Governance environment and investment mode
Li and Filer (2007) examine how the governance environment of a country, its reliance on rule-based public ordering versus relationship-based private ordering, shapes foreign investors? choices between FDI and foreign portfolio investment. Public ordering relies on courts, codified law, and third-party enforcement, while private ordering depends on personal trust, reputation, and informal sanctions. In rule-based systems, FPI flourishes because passive investors can rely on transparent enforcement and shallow agency costs. In relationship-based systems, foreign investors prefer FDI to internalize control and substitute private agreements for weak institutions. The framework illuminates China?s ability to attract substantial FDI despite weak formal institutions through strong relational governance and guanxi-based networks.

Feinberg and Gupta (2009) - MNC subsidiaries and country risk
Feinberg and Gupta (2009) draw on transaction cost economics to explain how multinationals manage country risk after entry. They contrast political strategies, lobbying and influencing host governments, with operational strategies that internalize value through intra-firm trade and integrated network linkages. Political tools are constrained by legal context, prior experience, and regime changes, while operational integration buffers subsidiaries from external uncertainty by redirecting output through the MNE network. Testing three hypotheses on subsidiary data, they show that subsidiaries in high-risk countries are more operationally integrated; firms with political capabilities rely less on operational integration; and firms already proficient in operational integration deploy it more intensively, extending the concept of internalization to operational control.

Roy and Oliver (2009) - JV partner selection
Roy and Oliver (2009) examine how host-country institutional environments, especially rule of law and control of corruption, shape partner selection in international joint ventures. Drawing on institutional theory and transaction cost economics, they argue that strong rule of law reduces both appropriation concerns and coordination costs, while corruption control surprisingly has limited direct effect. They distinguish task-related selection criteria (capabilities and resources) from partner-related criteria (trust, reputation, goal alignment). Concerns about appropriation and coordination shift emphasis toward partner-related criteria; in weak legal environments, firms compensate by scrutinizing partner trustworthiness and value alignment. The mediation analyses reveal that macro-level institutions filter into micro-level decisions through perceived transaction risks rather than directly.

Boddewyn and Brewer (1994)
Boddewyn and Brewer (1994) argue that political behavior is not merely an optional or reactive component of international business (IB). It is a core and inherent dimension of how multinational corporations operate. Crossing borders does not just involve economic exchanges. It exposes these corporations to diverse and often contradictory political governance environments. Political behavior, therefore, is defined as the acquisition, maintenance, development, and use of power in relation to other institutions. Corporations use political behavior to achieve strategic goals such as efficiency, market power, and legitimacy. The authors present a model in which political behavior is examined from two perspectives. First, the form of political behavior (compliance, avoidance, conflict, participation). Second, the intensity of political behavior, which depends on firm-specific factors, industry dynamics, and the non-market environment (governments, interest groups, public opinion). Political behavior is shaped by the capabilities of the corporation, the strategic risks involved, and the arbitrage or leverage opportunities that exist in an organizational environment.
Compliance: Alignment with government policies when strategic salience is low. Also, when resources to challenge are insufficient. Avoidance/circumvention: Circumventing undesirable policies when benefits outweigh legitimacy costs. Conflict: Direct opposition in zero-sum situations where both parties have high stakes and resources. Partnership: Joint participation to address mutual goals in positive-sum contexts, enhancing legitimacy.Crucially, the paper reframes political behavior as a means of achieving three key strategic goals. 
First, efficiency increases when firms use political strategies to reduce their own costs (e.g., reducing transaction costs) and raise competitors’ costs through protective policies. Efficient behavior is sustainable only when the benefits outweigh the costs.
Second, market power can be reinforced by political behavior. Political behavior enables firms to gain competitive advantages by limiting competitors’ entry or access to resources. It can create an advantage for firms when scale, scope, and organizational competencies are comparable among competitors.
Third, legitimacy is defined as the congruence between a firm’s actions and the values of its institutional environment. Legitimacy is essential for sustainable operations, especially in host countries where there are no universal rights for firms. Thus, legitimacy is achieved by aligning with local values, contributing to national goals (e.g., employment, technological advancement), and respecting sovereignty. Political engagements are more effective in building legitimacy than engagement or avoidance, but they must balance costs and benefits.
The authors develop six propositions to guide the understanding of political behavior in IB. Proposition 1 states that such behavior is simultaneously influenced by firm characteristics, industry structure, and its nonmarket context. Proposition 2 suggests that firms comply with policy when the policy is desirable or when they lack the resources to resist. Proposition 3 states that firms avoid or circumvent regulations when the benefits outweigh the potential legitimacy costs. Proposition 4 explains that conflict arises when both the firm and the government have high stakes, perceive the interaction as zero-sum, and have the power to influence outcomes. Proposition 5 suggests that partnership emerges when the situation is perceived as positive-sum. Both parties have sufficient bargaining power. Finally, Proposition 6 states that political behavior is more intense when risks are high, leverage opportunities are abundant, and firms have or can develop political competencies. 
In a revision of the traditional “spatial assumption,” the authors argue that it is the existence of states, not nations, that creates the political complexity of IB. Sovereign states actively shape the institutional environment of international markets. Access to these markets is often politically mediated. Thus, the first move in international trade is political, not economic. Firms require explicit or implicit permission to engage in cross-border activities. Furthermore, Boddewyn and Brewer reject the idea that states are superior actors pursuing abstract national interests. Instead, states are reimagined as factors of production, whose cooperation can be strategically employed in the creation of global value.
Political behavior also exhibits unique characteristics. It can be pursued with fewer material resources than economic behavior. It relies instead on legitimacy, organizational ability, or privileged access. Legal forms of collusion (such as lobbying or coalition building) are possible. It may also be more durable than economic power. But it is more difficult to achieve. The effects of political decisions are often diffuse. Firms may not immediately bear the consequences of their actions. This increases the appeal of political strategy. Political behavior, when successful, can create market imperfections or correct failures. It can give firms unique access or protection that enhances performance.
Finally, Boddewyn and Brewer emphasize the need to integrate political dimensions into IB strategy research. Rather than viewing political behavior as environmental or reactive, it should be viewed as strategically instrumental. With the increasing institutionalization of international markets, firms cannot afford to ignore the political sphere. Political behavior plays a central role in shaping firm performance. For example, it can be used to secure licenses, avoid liability, lobby for trade protection, or form alliances with host governments. Therefore, researchers should broaden the theoretical scope of IB research to include political strategy as a fundamental analytical category, rather than an exogenous constraint.

Buckley (2002), JIBS
Buckley, a founding figure in international business (IB) scholarship, argues that the IB research agenda has lost momentum and needs to find a new direction. Buckley believes that the field may now be stagnant, lacking a clear and unifying research question or “big idea” that can guide future scholarly contributions. He warns that this threatens to reduce IB to a mere applied field that draws on other disciplines rather than shaping them. 
Buckley identifies three key historical phases of IB research. Each of these is marked by major empirical challenges and associated theoretical developments.  
1- Foreign Direct Investment (FDI): After World War II, the main research question was why firms invest abroad. Traditional trade theories were inadequate. So, researchers turned to industrial organization and theories of firm-specific advantage. Hymer's concept of internalized advantage and Vernon's product cycle model were crucial in explaining the flow of U.S. foreign direct investment, especially into Western Europe. FDI was often the preferred mode of entry. It enabled firms to overcome local knowledge disadvantages and barriers to entry for domestic competitors.
2- Multinational Enterprise (MNE): In the 1970s to 1990s, attention was focused on theorizing the existence, structure, and strategies of MNEs. Two main schools of thought developed. One originated with Alfred Chandler. It emphasized internal organizational evolution and structural choices (e.g., domestic versus international divisions). The second emerged from transaction cost economics, influenced by Ronald Coase and Oliver Williamson, and led to the development of internalization theory (notably by Buckley and Casson in 1976). This theory argues that firms expand by internalizing foreign markets until further internalization is no longer efficient due to rising costs. 
3- Internationalization to globalization: The third wave of IB research focused on understanding globalization, strategic alliances, joint ventures, “born globals,” and the flexibility required by modern multinational corporations. Researchers addressed how firms navigate increasingly complex and volatile global environments. Key theoretical contributions were made by scholars such as Bartlett and Ghoshal (transnational strategy). Topics such as competitiveness, national systems, and institutional transitions (e.g., in post-socialist economies) became prominent. However, this phase also marked a shift from bold theorizing to conceptual proliferation and empirical description.
Buckley argues that these past waves of research were successful because they were grounded in real-world problems. The current IB agenda lacks a central empirical or theoretical challenge. He warns that IB is in danger of becoming a “funnel” field. It imports more ideas than it exports, becoming more marginal and less distinctive. He raises the concern that IB may become a field of application in other fields (e.g., economics, management), especially if it fails to develop a distinct intellectual identity. IB has often been a “follower” discipline, not a leader in theory development. 
To reinvigorate this field, Buckley suggests a return to empirical reality and the identification of new stylistic truths to guide theoretical work. He poses four future research questions:
Can we explain the sequence of emergence of national economies? (For example, England → United States → Germany → Japan → Singapore → Korea → China). Are culturally distinct forms of business organization truly necessary or are they artifacts? Can we measure the trend towards or away from globalization? What are the key emerging challenges for global capitalism?
He also highlights methodological distinctions, particularly comparative methods and sensitivity to cultural diversity, as potential strengths that IB should embrace. However, Buckley emphasizes that these alone are not enough. The discipline must rediscover a core empirical puzzle or paradigm-defining question to maintain its relevance and intellectual vitality.

Buckley & Lessard (2005)
Buckley and Lessard raise concerns about the intellectual orientation and relevance of international business (IB) research in the post-2000 era. They argue that the IB field has developed a “missing middle.” A gap exists between subject-oriented phenomena (globalization, outsourcing, emerging markets) and the development of specific IB theory. Issues are increasingly analyzed using theories from adjacent disciplines (such as economics or strategic management). IB scholars no longer act as intermediaries who translate real-world phenomena into unique theoretical contributions. They warn that this threatens the coherence and distinctiveness of IB as a scholarly field.
The authors are based on a recursive research framework in which problem-oriented insights and theory-oriented development are intended to reinforce each other. Historically, this model has been very effective: the rise of foreign direct investment (FDI) and multinational enterprises (MNEs) led to influential IB theories such as internalization theory (Buckley and Casson, 1976) and the product cycle model (Vernon, 1966). During this time, IB researchers translated complex global phenomena into theory and linked empirical observations with structured explanations. Since the early 2000s, Buckley and Lessard have observed that many IB researchers have abandoned this intermediary role. IB researchers have adopted general-purpose theories without adapting or enriching them for the international context. 
This “missing middle” is evident in the disconnect between contemporary global issues (e.g., virtual corporations, global branding, emerging markets and cross-border) and the development of IB theory. According to the authors, these issues are increasingly explained directly by discipline-based theories (such as transaction cost economics, resource-based perspectives, or institutional theory) without the involvement of specific IB frameworks. This bypassing of IB theorists leaves the field vulnerable to intellectual marginalization. International business becomes a passive recipient of theories rather than a locus for original insights. 
To reverse this trend and regain prominence in IB research, the authors urge scholars to refocus on building and refining theory that reflects the empirical complexities of the global economy. They suggest that every theory-based IB paper should begin with a clear statement of its derivation from theory and end with a specific contribution to the theory, no matter how incremental. Similarly, topic-oriented papers should not simply describe novel phenomena. They should challenge or extend theory and make explicit reference to relevant frameworks. Without such integration, IB risks becoming a field full of “kitchen sink” studies, overly eclectic, descriptive, and lacking in theoretical coherence. 
Buckley and Lessard also emphasize the value of distinguishing between general and specific theories. General theories offer broad explanatory power. Specific theories apply under specific conditions and contexts. For example, internalization theory serves as a general model, but becomes meaningful through its application to specific industries or organizational forms. Therefore, IB scholarship should continue to refine general theories while developing empirically based specific theories that deepen their relevance.

Buckley et al. (2007)
Buckley et al. (2007) examine the determinants of China’s outward foreign direct investment (ODI). They provide one of the first comprehensive empirical studies on China’s international investment behavior during the transition from a centrally planned to a market-oriented economy. This study on FDI motives tests twelve hypotheses using panel data for 49 host countries. The authors examine whether traditional FDI motives, such as market-seeking, resource-seeking, and strategic asset-seeking, explain China’s investment patterns. The results show that Chinese ODI is mainly driven by market-seeking motives, as larger host country market sizes significantly and positively affect the level of investment. In contrast, traditional resource-seeking and strategic asset-seeking motives are not significant predictors in the Chinese context, as the variables of natural resource endowment and patenting do not show statistical significance.
An important finding concerns the impact of cultural proximity. This variable is measured by the presence of ethnic Chinese populations in the host country. It has a positive and significant impact on China’s ODI. This supports the idea that cultural ties and diaspora facilitate investment flows. The study also shows the important role of policy liberalization in shaping China’s ODI.
Interestingly, the study reveals that Chinese firms behave differently than firms from developed countries in terms of how they assess political risk and macroeconomic instability. Contrary to expectations, the coefficient for host country political risk (LPOLI) is statistically significant but negatively signed, implying that Chinese firms may be less deterred by political risk compared to Western multinationals. Likewise, inflation (LINF), typically viewed as a deterrent, is found to have a positive and significant effect on Chinese ODI. This could indicate that Chinese firms, often influenced by capital market imperfections and state-driven motives, are more tolerant of risk or even attracted to moderate inflation as a signal of economic dynamism.
Trade-related variables show a relationship with Chinese ODI. Exports from China to the host country are positively correlated with ODI. This is consistent with conventional models in which trade precedes investment, and strengthens the market search explanation. However, imports from the host country have a negative impact on ODI, contrary to expectations. This suggests that some Chinese investment may be replacing intermediate goods that have already been imported for processing.
The paper also examines institutional and geographical factors. Variables that capture geographical distance, exchange rates, and market openness show that these factors did not systematically affect Chinese ODI over the study period. These results suggest state-influenced incentives behind China’s foreign investment in the 1980s and 1990s. This contrasts with Western models where such factors are typically more important.

Campbell et al. (2012)
Campbell et al. (2012) examine the relationship between the CSR activities of multinational enterprises (MNEs) and the concept of “distance”. In essence, it examines the cultural, administrative, geographical and economic differences between the firm and the host country. Within the framework of the theory of liability for foreignness (LOF), the authors examine how CSR can be a non-market strategy for foreign affiliates to build legitimacy. It also examines how it can reduce LOF in host countries. Traditionally, LOF has been addressed through market-based strategies such as changing entry modes or emulating local firms. The paper defines CSR as voluntary social contributions beyond legal requirements (McWilliams and Siegel, 2001). It presents it as an unknown but potentially powerful tool for legitimization, especially in contexts where foreign firms face skepticism. 
According to the theory, greater distance should increase the need for legitimacy, thereby encouraging CSR activity. But this study shows the opposite. Foreign affiliates from more distant countries are significantly less likely to engage in CSR in the host country. The authors show that cultural, administrative, geographical, and economic distance negatively affects the likelihood of a foreign affiliate being rated for using CAGE. High CSR engagement This finding highlights a crucial paradox. Distant affiliates benefit the most from CSR in terms of reduced LOF. But they are also the least likely to engage in such activities due to challenges in willingness and ability. 
The authors explain this paradox through two mechanisms. First, cultural dissimilarity weakens empathetic responses and makes managers less willing to invest in social causes that are not consistent with their own experiences or values. Second, from an operational perspective, greater distance increases costs and complexity. Administrative differences create compliance challenges. Geographical distance reduces opportunities for participation and monitoring. Economic inequalities limit the ability of resource-constrained firms to invest discretionarily in CSR. They show that distance acts as a barrier to CSR, despite its potential strategic value. 
The main finding of this paper is the examination of CSR reputation as a moderating variable. The authors argue that the prior CSR reputation of the foreign affiliate weakens the negative relationship between distance and CSR activity. Strong reputations create stakeholder expectations, and the cost of violating this “social contract” through CSR exit is higher for firms with an established reputation. Furthermore, experienced affiliates can leverage learning effects to engage more effectively in CSR, increasing both willingness and ability. As a result, firms with high CSR reputations are more likely to continue their CSR efforts in distant host countries, despite the challenges posed by distance.
Methodologically, the study uses the CRA database, which ranks banks in the United States based on their contributions to underserved communities. The authors argue that a CRA “excellent” rating indicates CSR behavior that goes beyond regulatory compliance and aligns with the theoretical definition of CSR. Using a single-industry sample of foreign bank affiliates enhances the study’s internal validity by controlling for sector-specific CSR standards. Regression models and factor analyses support the authors’ hypotheses regarding the main and moderating effects of CSR distance and reputation on CSR engagement.
This paper contributes significantly to the LOF literature by identifying CSR as a viable but unequal strategy for building legitimacy, especially in conditions of distance. It also improves the CSR literature by demonstrating how managerial cognition and institutional dissimilarity interact to shape CSR decisions. Importantly, this study advances Ghemawat’s CAGE framework by empirically disentangling the effects of its four components on non-market strategic behavior. For practitioners, the findings suggest that global CSR strategies should be considered not only for economic returns but also for institutional and psychological frictions. Policymakers can also learn lessons about how regulatory and cultural environments affect the ability of foreign investors to engage in socially responsible behavior.

Cantwell (1995)
Cantwell (1995) critically examines the classical product cycle model in light of technological globalization. He also examines how patterns of innovation and international investment by multinational corporations (MNCs) have evolved. He focuses on two fundamental assumptions of product cycle theory. First, that innovations originate almost exclusively in the home country of the parent company. And second, that the international diffusion of technological activities is led by technological leaders. Cantwell uses more than a century of patent data and firm-level evidence. He challenges both assumptions. He revises them and argues that technological globalization has significantly changed the structure and strategies of MNCs.
This study rejects the first hypothesis. Not simply because of recent developments but also on the basis of long-term historical evidence. He shows that innovative activities have long been geographically dispersed within MNCs. He does not completely ignore the role of national origins. On the contrary, national origins continue to shape the geographical and sectoral structure of technological activities. The home country continues to act as the main center of technological leadership. While technological innovation is now more internationally distributed, home country contexts are deeply embedded in the strategic direction of R&D investments and innovations. Therefore, Cantwell argues, the first hypothesis should be revised rather than eliminated.
The second hypothesis, about the leadership of technology development by industry leaders, retains more validity. But it also needs to be revised. Historically, technology leaders led the globalization of innovation by using foreign investments to exploit existing competencies. However, Cantwell observes a small but significant shift. Rather than exporting innovation, modern technology leaders now actively drive globalization themselves. These companies are pioneers in integrating regional and global networks. They adapt their organizational structures to take advantage of geographically distinct knowledge bases. In doing so, they not only expand their technological capabilities abroad, but also change the way innovation is organized globally. This represents a departure from the logic based on the initial stage of the product cycle model. Innovation gradually diffuses from the home country to host countries as the market matures.
In addition, Cantwell introduces the concept of “locational hierarchy” to expand the product cycle framework. In his view, the MNC should not be viewed as a centralized structure in which subsidiaries merely replicate innovations developed at headquarters. MNCs should be seen as decentralized yet integrated systems in which various regional and national units act as innovation nodes. These units interact dynamically with each other. Instead of simply disseminating technological directives from top to bottom, they allow for two-way flows of knowledge and mutual learning.
Cantwell's interpretation of the product cycle model also extends to broader theoretical discussions. He criticizes the explanatory power of the Heckscher-Ohlin model. He argues that the model fails to capture the true evolution of international trade and technology. He extends and updates Vernon's (1966) product life cycle theory to reflect the contemporary realities of global R&D organization. He emphasizes that external industrial investment is still more common among technology leaders. But this investment now supports collaborative and location-specific innovations rather than replicating domestic successes. 
Cantwell cautions against interpreting these changes as the end of national influence. He argues that multinational corporations are not “stateless” entities. But they still reflect the institutional, cultural, and historical characteristics of their countries of origin. National context continues to influence how global R&D networks are structured, how strategic objectives are defined, and where firms allocate innovation resources.

Caves (1998)
In this article, Caves surveys the field of international business (IB) and offers a two-part contribution. First, a critical identification of its methodological problems. Second, a discussion of promising research directions. Caves focuses primarily on the multinational enterprise (MNE) as the primary focus of IB research. His goal is to identify the conceptual and empirical challenges that researchers must overcome to meaningfully advance the field.
Caves begins by discussing three major methodological issues that confront IB research. First, there is the difficulty of controlling for the set of opportunities when evaluating firm policy choices. Unlike randomized experiments, firm strategies are endogenous. They are chosen in response to opportunities, making causal inference difficult. Researchers often cannot observe what a firm would have done if it had chosen a different strategy. This problem leads to bias in attributing success or failure. Second, there is survivor bias. Most empirical work focuses on successful MNEs that are still in operation and ignores firms that have exited or failed. This leads to inaccurate conclusions about what strategies work. Not all exits are failures. Some are planned and the completion of specific projects. Third, there is a lack of longitudinal data, which limits the ability to track the evolution and causality of MNEs over time. Caves encourages researchers to use census data and national databases at the firm level. Although he says they are time-consuming and difficult to use.
In the second part of the article, Caves outlines a set of research opportunities that could address current blind spots. Among them is the need to revise the way we understand the foundations of foreign direct investment (FDI). While previous work has emphasized firm-specific intangible assets (e.g., R&D, brand name), new forms of FDI require an understanding of market-specific managerial capabilities, regulatory knowledge, and logistical coordination. Another key area is the competitive behavior of MNEs. Behaviors such as international horizontal mergers raise questions about strategic incentives, business theft, and global market power. These issues have not been empirically explored.
Caves also encourages further work on MNE financial flows and behavior. How MNEs respond to exchange rate fluctuations, raise capital in local markets, and manage liquidity and taxes in countries. The development and technology transfer effects of FDI represent another important area. How foreign investment affects institutional capacity, infrastructure, and labor force development in host countries. Alliances and joint ventures also provide a platform for testing theories of trust, control distribution, and contract design in environments of high uncertainty and cross-country differences.
A central theme running through the article is Cuse’s belief that IB should move within the breadth of its subject matter while maintaining analytical rigor. He warns against overly narrow empiricism and calls for a better alignment between empirical complexity and theoretical frameworks. In this sense, Cuse sets the tone for later critiques, such as Buckley (2002), who also laments the disappearance of the “big questions” in IB.

Christmann and Taylor (2001)
Christman and Taylor (2001) examine how globalization affects environmental self-regulation by firms in developing countries, focusing on evidence from China. Critics of globalization argue that it promotes the relocation of polluting industries to countries with weak regulatory frameworks. This is known as the “pollution haven” hypothesis. The authors challenge this view. They suggest that globalization may create institutional and market pressures that encourage firms to voluntarily improve their environmental performance, even when local regulations are weak.
The authors define environmental self-regulation as follows: Firms voluntarily go beyond compliance to improve environmental practices. They focus on two types of firms: those owned by multinationals (MNE subsidiaries) and domestic firms in developing countries that export to developed markets or sell to multinational customers. For MNE subsidiaries, self-regulation is driven by several key pressures. First, these firms are concerned about the environmental implications of their actions for the MNE’s global reputation and legitimacy. Poor performance in a single location can tarnish the image of the entire MNE. Second, firms face higher standards of legitimacy, as foreign firms often have more stringent expectations than domestic competitors. Third, transnational organizations promote the adoption of global environmental standards such as ISO 14000. MNEs may internalize it as part of a global standards strategy. Fourth, maintaining environmentally friendly practices across global operations can create operational efficiencies and reduce costs.
For domestic firms in developing countries, international exposure also creates significant self-regulatory pressure. Firms that export to developed countries or sell to multinational customers face coercive pressures to improve their environmental performance. Developed country regulations and consumer preferences often spill over into the supply chain. This requires suppliers in emerging markets to comply with global standards. This process is called “contact spreading.” It links adopters in developed economies with non-adopters in less regulated environments. It facilitates the diffusion of best practices. In addition, developed countries may use environmental regulations as de facto trade barriers. It encourages exporting firms to comply with higher standards to maintain market access.
This study tests seven hypotheses using data from Chinese firms. The findings support the idea that multinational ownership increases both compliance with local environmental regulations and the likelihood of adopting ISO 14000. Firms that export a large proportion of their output to developed countries or sell to multinational customers are also more likely to comply with environmental regulations and adopt ISO 14000. This is the case in Europe and Japan, where regulatory and market expectations are particularly stringent.

Cuervo-Cazurra (2006)
Cuervo-Cazurra (2006) examines how corruption in the host country affects foreign direct investment (FDI). It focuses specifically on the composition, not just the volume, of FDI inflows. The main argument is that the impact of corruption varies depending on the institutional background of the foreign investor’s home country. Corruption is defined as the abuse of public power for private gain. Corruption is usually considered a barrier to investment because it increases uncertainty and transaction costs. Cuervo-Cazurra presents the idea that foreign investors’ sensitivity to corruption varies significantly based on the institutional characteristics of their country. This challenges the general assumption that corruption uniformly deters FDI. 
This article uses two contrasting perspectives on corruption. The positive view, known as “grease in the wheels,” argues that in over-regulated environments, corruption can facilitate transactions, speed up bureaucratic procedures, and allow firms to bypass inefficient organizational bottlenecks. In this view, bribery acts as a lubricant for business, making operations smoother and more efficient. In contrast, the negative view, known as “sand in the wheels,” views corruption as wasteful and bad. It leads to misuse of resources, inefficient allocation of investment, and the possibility that bribes may not even secure the promised outcomes. Corruption can also perpetuate a cycle of rent-seeking behavior and regulatory proliferation, thereby further reinforcing institutional inefficiency.
This study introduces two competing hypotheses. The first assumes that foreign direct investment from countries that have enacted anti-bribery laws will be negatively affected by host country corruption. This is attributed to the increased legal, financial, and reputational risks associated with bribing foreign officials under such legal regimes. The second hypothesis claims that foreign direct investment from highly corrupt countries will be positively associated with host country corruption because these firms are more accustomed to operating in corrupt systems. They may even find institutional similarity attractive. Empirical findings support both hypotheses. Countries with strict anti-bribery laws reduce foreign direct investment to corrupt destinations. Investors from high-corruption environments are either undeterred or even attracted by similar conditions abroad.
The paper also explores potential alternative explanations, including the possibility that OECD countries only invest in other OECD countries with low corruption. However, the analysis rejects this explanation and confirms that anti-bribery laws significantly affect investment decisions only in the context of host country corruption.
One of the main contributions of this study is to reveal how corruption in a host country changes not only the volume but also the origin of foreign direct investment. Rather than causing a uniform decline in investment, corruption shifts investment flows away from countries with strict anti-bribery norms and toward countries with institutional tolerance for corruption. This creates a paradox. In that the total foreign direct investment may not decline sharply, the ethical quality and governance standards of foreign investors are skewed. The implication is profound: countries that fail to address corruption risk attracting fewer investors, potentially entrenching weak governance and undermining reform efforts. Conversely, countries that effectively reduce corruption are likely to attract investments from more transparent and law-abiding companies, thereby reinforcing cycles of institutional improvement.
The paper also links its findings to broader international trade theories. It extends Dunning’s OLI paradigm by emphasizing that the location (L) component of internationalization is not uniformly attractive, but varies based on the institutional experience of the investor in their home country. It also contributes to the liability of foreign (LOF) literature by arguing that corruption exacerbates LOF, particularly for investors from low-corruption countries, while it may reduce perceived foreignness for those in corruption-prone environments. Furthermore, this study critiques the traditional “one-size-fits-all” interpretation of FDI and corruption by demonstrating the value of disaggregated analysis based on country-level institutional factors.

New field: Sociology of the Interesting:

All of the interesting propositions the author examined were found to involve the radical distinction between seeming and being, between the subject of phenomenology and the subject of ontology.

NON-INTERESTING PROPOSITIONS
1.	Instead of denying some aspect of the audience’s assumption-ground, the proposition affirms some aspect of their assumption-ground – “That’s obvious!”
2.	Instead of denying or affirming some aspect of their assumption-ground, the proposition does not speak to any aspect of the assumption-ground at all – “That’s irrelevant!”
3.	Instead of denying some aspect of their assumption-ground, the proposition denies the whole assumption ground – “That’s absurd!”

If an audience will find interesting any proposition which attacks the assumption they hold about its topic, then presumably all one would have to do to assert an interesting proposition is, first, to specify what the audience assumption about any topic is, and second, to come up with a proposition which refutes it. 
But…specifying an audience assumption about a topic is usually difficult because this assumption is not necessarily a unitary thing. 
The fundamental division in the social structure of an audience is between those who hold the common-sense assumption about a particular topic and those whose assumption about the topic is conditioned by their intellectual specialty or discipline – “LAYMEN” vs “EXPERTS”.
The assumptions about a topic held by both laymen and experts may be too diverse or too amorphous for any proposition about this topic to be found universally interesting. The assumptions also change over time. 

Generally, an intellectual specialty begins in a dialectical relationship with an assumption of the common-sense world. It then evolves and progresses by means of an internal dialectic with its own basic assumption. 
Anyone who wishes to assert a proposition that will be found interesting by laymen as well as experts must deal with the dilemma of this double dialectic. On the one hand, this proposition will interest experts only if it denies the ground-assumption of their discipline. On the other hand, his proposition will interest laymen only if it denies a ground-assumption of the common-sense world. But since the ground-assumption of experts is already a denial of a ground-assumption of laymen, he will find that any proposition which interests experts (because it denies their ground-assumption) will not interest laymen (because it affirms their ground-assumption) and vice-versa. What will be interesting to the one will be obvious to the other. 
In the academic world, a person usually resolves this dilemma by grasping for one horn while ignoring the other, by restricting the potential audience who will consider his proposition to his fellow experts while not worrying about the opinion of laymen. 

Although there is a diversity of audience segments, on a certain level of abstraction there is only a duality of audience assumptions. Therefore, one technique a person can use to simplify the assumptions of his audience is to raise the level of abstraction at which he considers them. At a certain level of abstraction, it’s either ‘X’ or ‘non-X’.

Try for universal interest? Consensus Creation:
1.	Social theorist articulates lack of consensus into dissensus either generally (“Half think ‘X’ and the other half think ‘non-X’”) or along the lines of specific sociological categories (“The lower class think ‘X’ whereas the upper classes think ‘non-X’”) or of categories appropriate to an intellectual specialty (“The structural-functionalists think ‘X’; the symbolic-interactionists think ‘non-X’”)
2.	He claims this apparent dissensus is actually a consensus at a higher level (“but both these groups are actually saying the same thing: ‘Y’”)
3.	Having created this higher consensus of assumptions, he is now ready to deny it (“however, they are both wrong. What really is the case is ‘non-Y’”)
All segments of the social theorist’s audience will now find his proposition interesting because it refutes the basic assumption shared by all of them (which basic assumption they themselves may not have known they all fundamentally assumed until he pointed out to them that they did).

Delios & Beamish (1999)
Delios and Beamish (1999) examine the determinants of ownership strategy in Japanese foreign direct investment (FDI). They focus on how firms decide on the equity stake they take in foreign subsidiaries. Ownership strategy is conceptualized as the percentage of equity held by a firm in its foreign affiliate, from joint ventures to wholly owned subsidiaries. This study introduces a broader framework. It integrates transaction cost economics (TCE), internalization theory, institutional theory, and experiential learning perspectives to understand how firms determine ownership levels when entering foreign markets.
Historically, much of the literature on entry mode decisions has been dominated by internalization theory (Buckley and Casson, 1976). It argues that firms choose ownership structures to internalize transactions to protect firm-specific advantages (FSAs), particularly tacit or knowledge-intensive assets such as research and development and proprietary technology. According to this view, higher ownership (e.g., wholly owned subsidiaries) is preferred when firms transfer unique technical knowledge to foreign markets to prevent leakage or imitation by partners. The theory assumes that internalization reduces spillover risks and provides greater control.
Transaction cost economics (Williamson, 1975), although closely related, focuses specifically on the efficiency of transaction governance. It distinguishes between markets, hierarchies, and hybrids (e.g., joint ventures) and suggests that asset specificity, the degree to which assets are tailored to a particular transaction, is a critical determinant of governance choice. As asset specificity increases, the potential for opportunism and market transaction costs increase, leading firms to internalize operations through greater ownership. Thus, TCE predicts a positive relationship between asset specificity and the level of ownership. 
However, Delios and Beamish argue that these transactional approaches are not sufficient to fully explain FDI ownership strategies. Drawing on institutional theory, the authors note that the institutional environment, including regulatory constraints, legal systems, and enforcement mechanisms, can shape ownership decisions independently of transaction costs. For example, in countries with weak intellectual property rights, firms may prefer higher ownership to exert control and protect proprietary knowledge. Conversely, legal restrictions on foreign ownership in some countries may force firms to adopt joint ventures, regardless of the nature of the assets.
Furthermore, this study incorporates insights from the experiential learning literature, particularly the Uppsala model (Johanson & Vahlne, 1977). It emphasizes the importance of international experience. Firms with more general and host country-specific experience are better equipped to manage uncertainty and reduce coordination risks. As a result, these firms are more likely to take higher equity positions in foreign ventures and leverage their accumulated learning.
Their findings suggest that: Institutional and experience-based variables are more consistent predictors of ownership levels than transaction-related factors. Asset specificity, while theoretically central to TCE, shows weak and inconsistent effects on ownership. In some cases, contrary to predictions, it is even negatively related to ownership levels. This may be explained by the distinctive nature of Japanese firms, where control may be exercised through relational mechanisms or keiretsu networks rather than formal ownership structures. Firms operating in countries with strong institutional environments (e.g., effective property rights) are likely to adopt higher ownership structures, as such environments reduce the risk of embezzlement and deal failure. Firms with more international experience, especially experience in the host country, are more confident in managing local operations and are therefore more likely to pursue wholly owned subsidiaries.
This study makes several key contributions. First, it challenges the dominance of internalization and TCE theories in explaining ownership strategy by showing that organizational context and experiential learning play critical roles. Second, it highlights the limitations of equating ownership with control, particularly in contexts where interorganizational relationships can substitute for equity control. Third, it proposes a more integrated model that reflects the multidimensional nature of ownership decisions in international business.

Devarakonda et al. (2017)
Devarakonda et al. examine how differences in the legal institutions of the countries of origin of international joint venture (IJV) partners affect the adoption of arbitration provisions in IJV contracts. This study addresses an important governance question in international trade: how does institutional mismatch across borders shape the prior choice of dispute resolution mechanisms? The authors argue that because IJVs are inherently imperfect contracts and are often subject to dispute, especially in international contexts, companies should consider the relative efficiency of different mechanisms, public courts, IJV boards, and private arbitration, when designing their governance structures. 
This study suggests that legal institutions coordinate economic transactions by facilitating public order through enforceable contract law and reliable court systems. However, when partners come from countries with different legal traditions, high procedural uncertainty, costly litigation processes, or weak shareholder protection, the efficiency and predictability of public courts as a dispute resolution channel is reduced. This institutional incoherence leads companies to adopt arbitration, a private, tripartite mechanism for resolving disputes. Arbitration offers several advantages. It is perceived as more neutral, flexible, faster, and cost-effective than litigation. This is particularly true in transnational environments where legal ambiguities and enforcement challenges often complicate court-based solutions.
The authors use transaction cost economics (TCE) to theorize that firms actively include arbitration clauses in IJV contracts when public legal institutions are inefficient. Or when institutional asymmetries between partners increase the risk of misinterpretation, opportunism, or poor enforcement. They identify four key institutional variables that influence the arbitration decision. (1) Differences in legal traditions (e.g., common law versus civil law). (2) Procedural uncertainty in courts. (3) High litigation costs. (4) Weak shareholder protection. When these factors exist, they create uncertainty and potential inequities in court-based adjudication. They make arbitration a more efficient and fairer option.
By examining 104 IJVs, the authors empirically test and confirm their hypotheses. They find that greater institutional distance between partners actually increases the likelihood that arbitration will be chosen as a prior dispute resolution mechanism. The study also compares arbitration with internal governance mechanisms such as IJV boards and finds that while arbitration choices are shaped by institutional/legal factors, board-based governance is often a function of transactional characteristics such as asset specificity or partner familiarity. This distinction emphasizes that different governance instruments are appropriate for distinct types of uncertainty: institutional versus transactional. 
This study contributes to the literature on alliance governance and institutional theory by demonstrating how institutional heterogeneity, rather than partner trust or resource asymmetry, plays a significant role in shaping dispute resolution choices in international joint ventures. They show that arbitration acts as a strategic adaptation to organizational risk. It enables partners to circumvent the inefficiencies of public legal systems that may be unfamiliar, biased, or poorly functioning. This study deepens our understanding of how firms use contractual foresight to reduce conflict and manage uncertainty in cross-border partnerships. It highlights the importance of considering not only the economic logic of transactions but also the institutional context in which international trade is embedded.

Dunning (2009)
Dunning reassesses the role of location in the behavior and strategy of multinational enterprises (MNEs). He argues that recent scholarship has focused disproportionately on the ownership and internalization aspects of the OLI (Ownership-Location-Internalization) framework. It has neglected the important “L” component, location. Dunning warns that the OLI paradigm risks losing its explanatory richness if location is not restored as a central explanatory variable. This article is both a theoretical revision and a call to rebalance the “three pillars” of OLI, particularly in light of the transformative changes of the past two decades. 
Dunning identifies three structural developments that have changed MNE behavior and location considerations. (1) The emergence of intellectual capital as the main wealth-creating asset in industrial economies. (2) Increased globalization, enabled by technological advances and the liberalization of trade and investment. (3) The reemergence of labor alliances with rising capitalism. Based on hierarchical structures, these changes have made intangible and mobile assets (such as knowledge and innovation) more important. They have changed the logic of foreign direct investment (FDI) and the geography of economic activity.
At the microeconomic level, Dunning notes the shift in MNE behavior toward strategic asset foreign direct investment in knowledge-based sectors. Unlike traditional market or resource-seeking investments, these new forms of foreign direct investment are motivated by the desire to enhance firm-specific advantages through access to location-bound complementary assets, such as skilled labor, R&D networks, organizational quality, and innovation clusters. The increasing generalization of foreign affiliates in host countries has deepened value chains. It has led to the geographical dispersion of higher-order activities such as R&D and design, especially in developed economies. As a result, the strategic importance of location-based assets (rather than natural endowments) has grown.
Dunning also shows the changing nature of spatial transaction costs, driven both by market liberalization and the increasing complexity of economic activity. Firms now weigh the efficiency of internalizing intermediate markets against cross-border outsourcing. They often choose internalization to reduce coordination, information, and execution costs. The formation of spatial clusters of firms, encouraged by government policies, reduces these costs and facilitates learning. Thus, location itself becomes a dynamic source of competitive advantage, particularly when it supports knowledge transfer and co-creation.
In macroeconomics, Dunning challenges traditional trade theories that are based on comparative advantage and assume factor immobility and perfect competition. These models fail to fully explain the modern international allocation of economic activity, particularly the role of MNEs in the configuration of global value chains. He calls for a re-thinking of international economics that highlights the distinctive contributions of MNEs, such as their ability to internalize cross-border trade.
Dunning revisits the four classic FDI motives, resource search, market search, efficiency search, and strategic asset search. While traditional motives still explain much of the FDI in developing countries, advanced economies are increasingly attracting investment driven by knowledge acquisition and shared innovation. This evolution emphasizes the need to refocus the “L” in firm-level strategy and national policy, as the spatial configuration of a firm’s activities may affect or even constitute the firm’s ownership advantage.
Finally, the paper offers implications for IB researchers and policymakers. Researchers are encouraged to integrate spatial dimensions into theories of firm behavior. Recognize that location can both shape and be shaped by firm strategy. Policymakers are urged to create macro policies that build place assets such as education systems, research and development infrastructure, and institutional quality. Work with MNEs in ways that align national development goals with firm-level competitiveness. Dunning concludes that MNE “externality” affects not only the volume of investment but also the structure and outcomes of global economic activity.

Ellstrand, Tihanyi, & Johnson (2002)
This study examines how a firm’s board structure affects the level of international political risk in a firm’s foreign direct investment (FDI) portfolio. The authors use agency theory. They show that differences in board composition: 1) the presence of domestic directors, 2) affiliated directors, and 3) CEO duality (when the CEO also chairs the board) can significantly affect a firm’s approach to political risk in international operations. Political risk is conceptualized as the probability of disruption due to instability or unpredictability in the political environment of a host country. It may harm a firm’s productive investments.
Agency theory provides the basis for the hypotheses. It assumes that shareholders (principals) and managers (agents) often have conflicting interests, particularly with regard to risk preferences. Shareholders who can diversify risk across a portfolio may prefer strategies that maximize returns, even when it is risky. Managers, in contrast, tend to be more risk-averse because their jobs are tied to the fate of the firm and they cannot diversify their jobs in the same way. Boards, as governance mechanisms, are expected to mediate these interests. This paper argues that inside managers (current employees of the firm) prefer low-risk international strategies. Outside or affiliated managers may be more neutral or less influential in such decisions.
Three hypotheses are proposed and tested. First, the authors argue that a higher proportion of inside directors on the board is associated with lower levels of political risk in a firm’s international investment portfolio. This hypothesis is supported by the data. This is consistent with the view that inside directors, who are aligned with the CEO, support risk-averse strategies. The second hypothesis predicts that affiliated directors, those with indirect ties to the firm, will also be associated with lower levels of political risk. This hypothesis is not supported. This suggests that affiliated firms may lack the influence or relevant knowledge to significantly shape international risk. The third hypothesis states that CEO duality leads to lower levels of political risk. Based on the notion that when the CEO also chairs the board, they can stabilize decision-making and implement conservative strategies. This hypothesis is supported. It suggests that CEO dominance may lead to more cautious international investments.
This study uses a sample of US manufacturing firms with international operations. Political risk is measured through a foreign political risk rating. It is associated with characteristics of the board structure. The findings suggest that board composition, in particular, the presence of a dual role of local directors and the CEO, plays an important role in reducing exposure to international political risks.
Implications: First, the findings suggest that managers and boards actively shape a firm’s international risk profile. Not just reactively, but through deliberate strategic choices. Boards that are dominated by insiders or led by the CEO are more cautious. They prefer host countries with less political uncertainty. This supports the expectations of broader agency theory that managers aim to protect their own interests, including job security and predictable performance outcomes. Second, the rejection of the second hypothesis suggests that not all forms of board affiliation have the same impact. It draws attention to the different roles of managers based on their proximity to managerial decision-making.

Fagre & Wells (1982)
Fager and Wells (1982) analyze the bargaining dynamics between multinational enterprises (MNEs) and host governments in developing countries. They focus on the extent of foreign ownership as a key outcome of such negotiations. The main assumption is that the bargaining power of MNEs depends largely on their strategic resources and the competitive structure of their industries. The authors aim to examine how technology, market control, and other firm-specific factors affect the extent of MNE ownership in foreign investments. Ownership is viewed as a proxy for the outcome of bargaining interactions. It reflects the ability of MNEs to secure favorable terms when entering foreign markets.
This study categorizes entry modes into three main types: 1) wholly owned subsidiaries (including greenfield investments and acquisitions), 2) joint ventures (where control is shared with local partners), and 3) non-equity modes such as exporting, licensing, or franchising. The authors argue that the ability to pursue a wholly owned strategy is a function of bargaining success. They test the hypothesis that firm-specific characteristics translate into stronger bargaining positions and, consequently, higher ownership shares.
Five main sources of bargaining power are identified in this study. 1. Technology is a critical resource. Firms with high R&D intensity or those operating in technologically advanced industries bring more value to host economies. They guarantee greater autonomy and ownership. 2. Product differentiation, especially in sectors where advertising and branding are crucial, gives MNEs a competitive advantage. Firms with strong brand equity and marketing capabilities can exert greater control. They resist host demands for joint ownership. 3. Market access, especially the ability to export or coordinate international operations, strengthens bargaining power. Subsidiaries that act as export hubs or product transfer hubs in global networks are more likely to have full ownership. 4. Capital resources (measured by asset size or affiliate sales) are influential. But they are not always decisive. While large investments can indicate power, they do not guarantee high ownership in politically sensitive environments. 5. Product diversity, the number of product lines offered by the affiliate, is positively associated with foreign ownership. Due to the coordination and complex management knowledge required in multi-product operations, full ownership is more likely.
An important countervailing factor is industry competition. The more MNE competitors are present in a given industry, the less individual bargaining power each firm has. Host governments may exploit this by playing firms off against each other. This reduces the leverage that each MNE can exert. This effect explains why even resource-rich MNEs may agree to joint ventures or partial ownership in sectors with a high foreign presence.
The authors' analysis also aligns with the OLI (Ownership, Location, Internalization) framework by adding small differences to the “Ownership” component. We can consider it not only as a firm capability but also as a negotiable outcome dependent on external dynamics.

Feinberg and Gupta (2009)
Feinberg and Gupta (2009) examine how MNCs manage country risk after entering host countries. They use the theory of transaction cost economics (TCE). The authors argue that institutional risks, stemming from weak political and regulatory environments, pose persistent threats to MNC operations. These risks include the risk of asset expropriation, unexpected restrictions on business activities, and accelerated asset depreciation. Despite the fact that MNCs avoid high-risk environments, the authors note that many companies actually establish subsidiaries in countries that are politically or economically unstable.
The authors distinguish between two main strategic approaches to reducing external organizational risks: 1) political strategies and 2) operational strategies. Political strategies involve lobbying, financial incentives, or public mobilization to influence host country decision-makers. These strategies aim to directly reduce the level of country risk. However, political strategies face significant limitations. They may be constrained by the laws of the host country. They may backfire if the political leadership of the host country changes. They require prior experience to be effectively employed. Thus, not all multinationals can or should rely on political influence to protect their investments. In contrast, operational strategies focus on reducing reliance on the external environment by increasing internal control through integration. This involves mechanisms such as intra-firm trade, contractual arrangements, ownership structures, and building trust. These strategies divert the output of the focal subsidiary away from the host country market. Instead, they direct it through the MNC's internal global network, known as operational integration. This integration minimizes exposure to unpredictable external conditions and increases the MNC's control over critical resources and value chains. The authors conceptualize internalization not only as ownership but also as operational control over the day-to-day routines and transactions within the multinational corporation.
Feinberg and Gupta test three main hypotheses. First, they assume that the higher the country risk, the higher the level of operational integration of the subsidiary. The rationale is that internal trade with other units in the MNC network helps to protect against organizational fluctuations in the host environment. Second, they assume that an MNC’s ability to deploy political strategies weakens the relationship between country risk and operational integration. Firms with political influence can reduce their exposure without having to internalize operations. Third, they suggest that an MNC’s prior experience and ability in operational integration reinforces the positive relationship between country risk and operational integration. Such firms are more able to implement internal buffer mechanisms when needed.
Empirical analysis supports all three hypotheses. It shows that subsidiaries in high-risk countries tend to have higher levels of intra-firm trade, which is indicative of greater operational integration. Furthermore, firms with more developed political capabilities are less likely to rely on operational integration in response to risk. Firms that already excel at operational integration are more likely to strengthen their use of this strategy in high-risk environments.
Theoretical contributions. Shows that firms actively adapt their internal structures to deal with ongoing uncertainty. Also extends the concept of internalization to encompass operational mechanisms beyond stock ownership. Emphasizes the importance of intra-firm relationships and process control. Shows how strategic capabilities in political influence and operational integration interact with organizational environments to shape firm behavior.
In practice, this paper suggests that MNCs entering high-risk countries should assess both their political lobbying capabilities and their internal integration systems. In situations where political influence is limited or precarious, operational integration becomes a critical tool for risk management. Policymakers may also learn from the findings: stronger institutions and clearer policies can reduce the need for firms to protect themselves through internal integration and encourage more direct engagement with local economies.

Florida (1997)
Florida’s (1997) study provides a significant empirical contribution to our understanding of the globalization of innovation by analyzing the motivations and organizational dynamics of foreign-affiliated R&D laboratories in the United States. The traditional foreign direct investment (FDI) literature has emphasized demand-side factors, such as the need to support domestic production or adapt products to local markets, as key drivers for establishing R&D operations abroad. However, Florida’s findings challenge this view by showing the increasing prominence of supply-side motivations, particularly the desire to access scientific and technical human capital. His study shows that technology-driven goals now dominate firms’ decisions to globalize R&D. In particular, firms increasingly seek to leverage regional concentrations of expertise and innovation ecosystems rather than simply supporting existing manufacturing bases.
Florida also emphasizes the heterogeneity of R&D activities across sectors. He notes that the sources of innovation vary considerably across industries. For example, industries such as biotechnology and information technology often derive their innovations from basic science. While other industries, such as the automotive or chemical sectors, are more concerned with applied research and incremental development. This sectoral diversity means that foreign direct investment in R&D is not a uniform process. Rather, it is shaped by the specific knowledge needs and innovation logic of individual industries. Therefore, the nature, intensity, and orientation of foreign R&D laboratories vary greatly depending on their technological areas.
The main theme of the paper is the organizational challenge of managing international R&D. Florida illustrates the tension between maintaining corporate coherence and ensuring local autonomy, which is crucial for creativity and adaptability. While these foreign R&D units are expected to communicate with other divisions of their global parent companies, the study shows that excessive control and complex reporting requirements can hinder innovation. They also make it difficult to attract top scientific talent. As a result, many foreign-affiliated laboratories operating in the United States adopt U.S.-style R&D organizational models rather than attempting to transfer and replicate their home-country organizational systems. This reflects a broader pattern of adaptation and local learning. This contrasts with the pattern often observed in foreign manufacturing ventures, where replication of domestic best practices is more common.
The study also contributes to the broader debate about globalization and localization by demonstrating that human capital considerations are now shaping the geography of innovation. The United States continues to attract foreign investment in R&D, largely due to its abundant technical talent and world-class research infrastructure. The implication is that cities and regions with strong innovation ecosystems, including elite universities, pools of specialized labor, and shared industrial clusters, will continue to play a central role in the global division of innovative labor.

Ghemawat (2003)
In this article, Ghemawat introduces the concept of semi-globalization to describe the current state of cross-border economic integration. He challenges the traditional dichotomy in international trade thinking. This thinking often assumes that markets are either fully integrated or fully fragmented. Ghemawat argues that real-world globalization neither achieves the theoretical ideal of full integration nor resembles complete isolation. Instead, the global economy operates in an “intermediate” of incomplete integration. Significant but uneven levels of trade, capital flows, and market connectivity persist. This condition is what Ghemawat calls semi-globalization. It provides a more realistic framework for analyzing international strategy.
Ghemawat supports this argument by providing empirical evidence that shows how global integration has grown over time. But it still falls far short of expectations based on economic models. Measures such as cross-border trade, foreign direct investment (FDI), and capital flows have increased dramatically. But they are constrained by longstanding national boundaries, cultural distances, regulatory changes, and institutional heterogeneity. He emphasizes that even in industries that are often considered highly globalized, such as telecommunications or banking, local responsiveness still plays an important role. This persistent residual friction emphasizes the importance of location-specific considerations in shaping firm behavior. It forms the basis of Ghemawat’s argument that international business strategy should be fundamentally different from domestic or corporate strategy.
Semi-globalization creates the need for companies to develop differentiated international business strategies that are sensitive to location-specific factors. Traditional business strategy considers the context of a country and emphasizes domestic diversity and synergies. International strategy must deal with cross-border differences in regulations, consumer preferences, infrastructure, and economic conditions. Ghemawat argues that these differences not only create challenges that companies must adapt to, but also create opportunities to exploit competitive asymmetries. For example, companies can tailor products or services to regional tastes. Engage in geographic arbitrage. Or create specialized supply chains that take advantage of local cost structures and competencies.
A new insight in this article is Ghemawat’s emphasis on the “residual barriers” left behind by semi-globalization. Rather than viewing these barriers simply as obstacles to overcome, he suggests that they can be used strategically to advantage. For example, companies may build brand trust by demonstrating local embeddedness, or defend their market position by navigating complex local regulations better than foreign companies. In addition, product markets and factors that have not been fully globalized, such as labor or health care, provide fertile ground for strategic differentiation.
He posits semi-globalization as a core concept that brings distinction back to international business research. Some scholars, such as Buckley (2002), believe it is losing relevance. Ghemawat argues that by embracing the complexity and location-specific nature of global strategy, scholars can deepen their understanding of how firms navigate international markets. This involves not only updating institutional and transaction cost theories, but also recognizing the interplay between global integration and local responsiveness.
For multinationals: Success in the global economy requires more than scale or replication; it requires contextual sensitivity, appropriate strategies, and the ability to work within the constraints of global integration. Companies must decide when to adapt locally, when to standardize, and how to manage the tension between coordination and responsiveness. For policymakers, Ghemawat’s framework encourages a different way of thinking about global competitiveness. Strengthening domestic competitiveness requires recognizing the limits of global convergence and investing in the location-specific capabilities that matter most in a semi-globalized world. He argues that recognizing the reality of semi-globalization is not a constraint but an opportunity to create more effective and grounded international strategies.

Griffith & Harvey (2001)
Griffiths and Harvey (2001) explore the concept of global dynamic capabilities (GDC). GDC is defined as the ability of a firm to develop difficult-to-imitate combinations of resources on a global scale that provide sustainable competitive advantage. GDC consists of two main components: 1. Achieving global system coherence across operations while allowing for customization in local environments. 2. Enabling firms to adapt, integrate, and reconfigure internal and external assets to exploit opportunities in the global marketplace. They use the resource-based view (RBV) (Barney, 1991; Wernerfelt, 1984) and the market-based view (MBV) (Srivastava et al., 1998). The authors argue that power in international relations is a key mechanism through which GDCs are realized.
This study integrates separate traditional streams of research. From the RBV, power arises from firm-level assets that are valuable, rare, inimitable, and non-substitutable (VRIN). Two RBV-based constructs are emphasized. 1. Asset specificity, defined as investments made for a specific relationship without alternative use (Williamson, 1979; Peteroff, 1993). 2. Predictability, or the ability to predict partner behavior and future conditions, which contributes to strategic certainty and reduces transaction costs (Coase, 1992, 1939; Parvatiyar). From the MBV, power arises from market interactions and external relational assets. Two key constructs are examined. 1. Market knowledge gap, or information asymmetry between partners regarding local market conditions, is particularly important in cross-border contexts (Srivastava et al., 1998). 2- Market type, based on Kalle's (1986) distinction between seller's markets (demand exceeds supply, typical in emerging and developed markets). 
The authors propose and test four hypotheses. These hypotheses relate variables to the power that foreign distributors have relative to U.S. manufacturers. Three of the four hypotheses are supported based on the results. Greater asset specificity, greater predictability, and larger market knowledge gaps all significantly increase distributor power. However, the hypothesis that distributors have more power in seller-side markets than in buyer-side markets was not supported. This suggests that the effect of market structure may be more complex and interdependent than initially assumed.
This research provides both theoretical and practical contributions. Theoretically, it is one of the first attempts to unify the RBV and MBV within a dynamic capabilities framework. It advances the idea that both internal and external resources contribute to relationship strength and strategic flexibility in international operations. In practice, it suggests that firms seeking to build GDC should not only invest in relationship-specific assets and predictable governance structures, but also cultivate local knowledge advantages. Actively manage inter-organizational dynamics. In global distribution networks, where there are asymmetries in knowledge and infrastructure, such strategies can shape power relations and long-term competition.

Griffith, Cavusgil, & Xu (2008)
Griffiths, Cavusgil, and Zhou (2008) respond to ongoing debates about the direction of international business (IB) research. In particular, they engage with criticisms by Buckley (2002), who argued that the field may have exhausted its postwar “big questions.” In contrast to this view, the authors argue that while fundamental questions have been raised, they have not been addressed at a sufficiently fundamental level. Many of the existing answers are outdated in the context of today’s global business environment. Rather than abandoning the past, they suggest that meaningful progress in IB requires building on the strong theoretical and empirical foundations that have already been laid.
This study aims to answer two key questions. 1. What recent contributions have shaped the IB research agenda? 2. What emerging issues are likely to shape future IB scholarship? The authors address these questions using a two-part empirical approach. 1. A citation analysis of six leading IB-related journals from 1996 to 2006. 2. A Delphi study involving the most prolific researchers in the field. The aim is to document the theories, structures, and levels of analysis that have historically been dominant. It also anticipates areas that hold promise for future research. Their findings show that while the Journal of International Business Studies (JIBS) remains the most cited journal, journals such as the Strategic Management Journal (SMJ) also contribute significantly to the core IB conversation.
From the citation analysis, the authors find that international business scholarship continues to be shaped by foundational theories, such as transaction cost economics, Dunning's eclectic model, Hofstede's cultural dimensions, the internationalization process, the resource-based view, and the knowledge-based view of the firm. These theoretical lenses have primarily guided research toward entry-mode strategies, foreign market expansion, and knowledge transfer. While these topics align with Buckley's earlier three-stage classification of the IB field (foreign direct investment flows, MNE theory, globalization processes), the authors argue that there are still numerous subtopics within these areas that are deeply relevant to business.
The Delphi study, based on input from leading scholars, identifies emerging topics that are likely to shape the future of IB research. These emerging topics include: 1) the management and performance of international firms (particularly in relation to knowledge, trust, and supply chain coordination); 2) the process of globalization and its measurable consequences; 3) the emergence of multinational firms from emerging markets; and 4) the strategic behavior of born globalists and international entrepreneurs.
Griffiths and colleagues emphasize that future research should continue to apply existing theoretical frameworks to new contexts. It should develop new theories that reflect the rapidly changing global landscape. While most IB focuses on the firm level of analysis, the authors emphasize the need for multilevel and interdisciplinary approaches that take into account institutional, cultural, and macroeconomic factors. They also call for greater methodological sophistication and contributions that are theory-based rather than merely descriptive.

Guler, Guillén, and Macpherson (2002)
(2002) examined how ISO 9000 quality certifications, an internationally recognized standard for quality management, are disseminated across countries, firms, and industries. The authors aim to explain this dissemination as a technical response to quality management needs. More importantly, as an institutional process shaped by global interdependencies and competitive pressures. They draw on new institutional theory, particularly the concepts of isomorphism (coercive, imitative, and normative). They suggest that firms adopt global standards such as ISO 9000 not only for rational efficiency-based incentives but also to gain legitimacy in the eyes of stakeholders embedded in international networks.
This study outlines three main mechanisms driving the global diffusion of ISO 9000: 1) coercive isomorphism, imposed by powerful actors such as governments and multinational corporations that pressure local firms to conform to global standards; 2) normative isomorphism, resulting from the presence and spread of technical and scientific knowledge about quality management; and 3) imitative isomorphism, in which firms imitate others in equivalent competitive positions across countries to manage uncertainty or maintain parity in global markets. Notable is their novel application of the concept of role equivalence. The idea that firms occupying similar positions in the global trade network imitate each other's practices as a form of competitive benchmarking.
Their results provide strong support for the role of coercive pressures (e.g., from foreign governments and multinational corporations) and imitative pressures (from trade-based role equivalence) in the adoption of ISO 9000. Countries with stronger trade ties to early adopters or competing in similar markets showed higher adoption rates. While scientific and technical knowledge was hypothesized to contribute to normative isomorphism, this study finds no strong evidence to support this effect.
This paper demonstrates that institutional isomorphism is not confined within national boundaries but operates transnationally, influenced by global trade networks and international competition. It challenges the notion that globalization produces a uniform diffusion process, and shows that adoption patterns vary significantly across countries depending on their global interconnectedness and institutional dependencies. The authors also emphasize that national policies on trade and foreign investment can significantly shape the speed and pattern of diffusion of organizational performance by changing how firms are exposed to these global pressures.
These findings have practical implications for policymakers and multinational corporations. Governments interested in promoting the adoption of ISO 9000 can use trade relations and foreign direct investment as indirect channels for the dissemination of quality standards. For managers, this research highlights that gaining legitimacy in global markets may require adopting internationally recognized practices, not simply for technical superiority but also for symbolic alignment with global expectations.

Hejazi and Santor (2010)
Hejazi and Santoor (2010) examine the hypothesis, widely held in the international business literature, that a firm’s degree of internationalization (DOI) leads to improved performance. The authors examine not only whether this relationship holds but, more critically, whether the observed relationship is causal. They analyze the relationship between foreign expansion, asset risk, and profitability. The main concern is that the prior literature often implicitly assumes that internationalization improves performance without fully considering endogeneity and reverse causality. That is, whether better-performing firms are simply more likely to expand internationally. The authors argue that without addressing this direction, the DOI-performance link may be overstated.
They focus on three main performance measures: return on assets (ROA), return on equity (ROE), and Tobin's q, and consider the mix of foreign assets. In particular, they distinguish between low-risk securities and high-risk assets such as loans and deposits. Their results suggest a causal relationship whereby firms that perform well domestically are more likely to expand internationally, rather than the other way around. This finding is consistent with internalization theory, which suggests that firms develop firm-specific advantages internally (e.g., innovations, competencies, efficiencies). They then go abroad to exploit these capabilities through FDI or other entry methods. 
The study shows that the positive relationship between DOI and performance is driven primarily by exposure to risky foreign assets, not low-risk assets. Banks with riskier foreign loans and deposits have stronger performance gains. This suggests that the increased returns are a risk premium rather than a pure benefit of geographic diversification. In contrast, banks that invest in low-risk foreign securities, such as U.S. Treasuries, do not experience a similar performance improvement.
Another important implication concerns strategy. The results suggest that if managers rely solely on the observed positive correlation between DOI and performance without considering causality or risk, they may adopt flawed international expansion strategies. In reality, the observed performance gains may simply reflect a self-selection effect. Firms that have previously been successful domestically are more capable and confident in undertaking international ventures. Thus, initial domestic performance should be considered when assessing the impact of internationalization. Furthermore, while international expansion can indeed enhance firm performance, it may also increase firm risk. The key strategic insight is that expansion may not create value for the firm unless the incremental returns from internationalization outweigh the additional risk incurred.
This study makes an important contribution by empirically demonstrating that performance precedes internationalization, countering the traditional assumption of reverse causality. It also introduces a risk-based lens for evaluating foreign asset portfolios and shows that foreign asset risk exposure is a critical variable mediating the internationalization-performance relationship. These findings call for more nuanced and context-sensitive strategic decision-making. Managers should look beyond average patterns. They should consider the type of foreign assets held, the country-specific risk involved, and the trajectory of their own firm’s performance before committing to cross-border expansion.

Hofstede’s (1983)
Hofstede's 1983 paper presents a fundamental critique of the universal application of management theories across national boundaries. It introduces the notion that organizational behavior and performance are deeply embedded in cultural contexts. At the core of his argument is the idea of national culture as "collective mental programming," shared values and assumptions acquired early in life that distinguish one society from another. Hofstede challenges the convergence hypothesis prevalent at the time, which assumed that sound management practices were universal and that economic development would lead to culturally homogeneous organizational behaviors. Instead, his research revealed persistent national differences in work-related values and managerial expectations, despite economic convergence.
To systematically analyze cultural differences, Hofstede introduces four dimensions of national culture: 1. Power distance, 2. Individualism versus collectivism, 3. Masculinity versus femininity, and 4. Uncertainty avoidance. Power distance indicates the extent to which less powerful members of organizations accept and expect hierarchical inequalities. In societies with high power distance, hierarchies are seen as natural and legitimate, while low power distance cultures emphasize equality and participatory decision-making. Individualism versus collectivism contrasts societies in which individuals are expected to take care of themselves and their immediate families with societies in which individuals are integrated into strong in-groups that protect them in exchange for loyalty. The third dimension, masculinity versus femininity, concerns the distribution of emotional roles between the sexes. In masculine cultures, assertiveness, competition, and material success are prioritized, while feminine cultures value quality of life, interpersonal relationships, and caring for the weak. Uncertainty avoidance describes how societies cope with the inherent unpredictability of life. Societies with strong uncertainty avoidance maintain rigid codes of belief and behavior to minimize ambiguity. Societies with weak uncertainty avoidance are more relaxed, open to change, and accepting of different perspectives. These dimensions help explain why organizational practices such as leadership, motivation, and communication vary so much across cultures. 
Hofstede applies these dimensions to key areas of organizational behavior. For example, in leadership, individualistic cultures may emphasize visionary and autonomous leaders. But collectivist cultures view leadership as a group function embedded in networks of mutual commitment. Organizational structure is strongly influenced by power distance and uncertainty avoidance. Hierarchical structures are dominant in societies with high power distance. While flat, decentralized structures are more common in egalitarian and uncertainty-tolerant cultures. In terms of motivation, individualistic cultures respond to rewards based on achievement and self-actualization, while collectivist cultures emphasize face maintenance, group identification, and conformity.
Hofstede criticizes the ethnocentric bias of many Western management theories. These theories often reflect assumptions rooted in U.S. cultural values, such as low power distance and high individualism. When such theories are applied globally without adaptation, they risk being out of sync with local expectations and behaviors. Hofstede warns that culturally insensitive applications of theory may not only be ineffective but also counterproductive, as they ignore deeply held societal norms and values. 
Hofstede’s (1983) work represents a fundamental shift in management thinking that emphasizes the cultural relativity of organizational theories. His empirical framework for understanding culture, based on large-scale survey data, provided a pioneering way to quantify cultural differences and their implications for business. While some critics have raised concerns about the generalizability of his findings (given that the data were drawn from a single multinational corporation, IBM), the four-dimensional model remains one of the most influential in cross-cultural management research. Hofstede’s message is clear: effective management must begin with an understanding of the cultural context, and one-size-fits-all theories are not enough in a globalized world.

Holburn and Zelner (2010)
Holborn and Zellner (2010) provide a review of how multinational corporations manage policy risk in their international investment strategies, particularly in politically unstable environments. Policy risk is defined as the likelihood that opportunistic policy changes will expropriate corporate profits or assets. Their central idea is that MNEs are not uniformly deterred by host country policy risk. But instead vary significantly in their responsiveness depending on their political capabilities. These capabilities are defined as firm-level competencies to use political resources to manage the policy process. These are shaped by two main country-based mechanisms: 1) organizational learning and 2) cognitive role. Through experiential learning in domestic country environments with weak institutional constraints, firms become skilled at navigating complex and uncertain political systems. Similarly, internal imprinting influences how managers perceive and respond to political uncertainty, embedding norms and assumptions in the firm's strategic toolbox.
The authors compare two theoretical mechanisms, learning theory and organizational influence theory, to explain how political capabilities develop. Learning theory suggests that firms acquire capabilities that enable better adaptation and survival through direct experience with political turbulence. Imprinting theory, on the other hand, argues that a country’s institutional structures, particularly norms, conflicts, and policymaking practices, become embedded in the firm’s routines and mental models. They influence future behavior in similar foreign contexts. Thus, firms from home countries with weaker institutional constraints, or where income inequality and ethnic dispersion generate high political rents, tend to develop stronger political capabilities. These capabilities, in turn, reduce the deterrent effect of host country political risk. They can even encourage entry into risky markets, especially when firms are confident in their ability to interact with and influence local political stakeholders.
The authors show that firms from home countries with institutional weaknesses are significantly more likely to enter host countries with high policy risk. Notably, they also find that this relationship is conditional. Firms from institutional environments characterized by weak constraints and socioeconomic divisions are more likely to perceive policy risk not as a threat but as a manageable challenge, one that they can exploit through well-developed policy routines. This paper proposes six testable hypotheses that demonstrate the moderating effects of home country institutional characteristics on the relationship between host country policy risk and investment inflows.
Contribution of the study: Theoretically, it advances the resource-based view by conceptualizing political capabilities as firm-specific advantages that are both rare and difficult to imitate. It also bridges institutional theory and shows how domestic country contexts shape firm behavior abroad. In practice, the findings suggest that firms from countries such as Indonesia or the Philippines, which are traditionally considered high-risk markets, may have strategic advantages in navigating similar institutional fluctuations in other countries. Finally, from a policy perspective, host countries that might otherwise be shunned due to political risk can attract FDI by aligning with investors who are able to interact effectively with political actors. In essence, it suggests that for politically capable MNEs, institutional turmoil can be a source of opportunity rather than avoidance.

Husted & Allen (2006)
Husted and Allen (2006) examined how multinational enterprises (MNEs) integrate social responsibility into their organizational strategies. The authors begin by identifying a key gap in the literature. Although CSR has become a visible component of corporate performance, particularly in global contexts, little is known about how MNEs actually manage CSR across subsidiaries. Drawing on strategic management theory and institutional theory, they examine whether CSR policies are aligned with an MNE’s product-market strategy or are instead shaped by constitutive pressures from the external environment. The study emphasizes the dual pressures that MNEs face, global integration versus local responsiveness. It seeks to understand how these pressures influence the prioritization of local versus global CSR issues.
The authors define CSR as a firm’s commitment to respond to external factors, positive or negative, that arise from its market actions. They make a conceptual distinction between local CSR, which responds to the needs and expectations of the host country society, and global CSR, which reflects globally accepted standards, such as environmental sustainability or labor rights. Drawing on Bartlett and Ghoshal’s (1989) typology of multinational, global, multinational, and transnational strategies, the authors assess whether CSR practices follow the same logic of integrated responsiveness that drives product-market strategies. In doing so, they compare two competing hypotheses. The first approach is a strategic approach. It states that MNEs assess global and local CSR issues independently of their product strategy and respond based on the importance of the issue. The second approach, however, is an institutional approach. It suggests that firms are likely to replicate their existing product-market organizational logic in CSR management, leading to institutional isomorphism.
This study tests two hypotheses. Hypothesis 1 suggests that the strategic importance of local and global CSR issues is unrelated to a firm’s product-market strategy and is consistent with strategic logic. Hypothesis 2 states that organizational strategy is related to the strategic importance placed on local CSR, with domestic and multinational corporations placing greater emphasis on local issues than global corporations. The findings reject Hypothesis 1 and support Hypothesis 2, which suggests that organizational factors are more influential than strategic considerations in shaping CSR priorities. Specifically, domestic and multinational corporations are more aligned with local CSR concerns, such as job creation and community development. But global MNEs tend to focus on globally recognized CSR issues, such as environmental sustainability, with less adaptation to local needs.
One insight is that global CSR issues, such as environmental protection, are generally prioritized across all types of MNEs. However, local CSR issues are more prominent in the strategic plans of firms that follow multi-domestic or transnational strategies, suggesting that responsiveness to local institutional contexts influences CSR engagement. This supports the view that CSR is often institutionally embedded, shaped by pressures for legitimacy rather than rational strategic planning. The findings suggest that MNEs often apply the organizational logic of their product markets to their CSR policies without independent analysis of social issues. For example, firms with strong global integration may adopt uniform CSR practices that fail to address host country concerns, leading to public backlash, as illustrated in cases such as Nestlé and Nike.
Husted and Allen argue that such institutional homogeneity arises from both internal and external pressures. Internally, CSR units often imitate successful structures within the company, such as marketing or operations. This is especially true when CSR objectives are vague or weak. Externally, companies respond to coercive, normative, and imitative pressures in their host countries, leading to conformity to local expectations.The authors conclude by emphasizing the need for companies to manage CSR more strategically, especially in an era when local communities and stakeholders are increasingly vocal. They recommend that global MNEs adopt a transnational CSR strategy and align CSR policies with global standards and local needs through careful analysis of the issue. This allows companies to achieve integration and accountability.

Hutson and Stevenson (2010)
Hutson and Stevenson (2010) examine how institutional characteristics at the national level (trade openness and the strength of creditor protection) affect firm-level foreign exchange (FX) exposure. This paper addresses two main questions: 1. Do firms located in open economies characterized by higher trade intensity have greater exposure to exchange rate fluctuations? 2. Does a strong corporate governance environment, particularly one in which creditors and minority shareholders are well protected, reduce this exposure? 
The authors distinguish between direct exchange rate exposure and indirect (competitive) exposure. Direct exchange rate exposure includes receivables, payables, and expected cash flows denominated in foreign currency. Indirect (competitive) exposure arises from changes in the firm’s competitive position due to currency fluctuations. For example, a firm that produces and sells locally will still face significant risk if a strengthening domestic currency makes imported goods cheaper. Indirect exposure is more complex, harder to measure, and more difficult to hedge because it requires a comprehensive understanding of the firm’s international competitive environment.
The findings show a strong and statistically significant positive relationship between trade openness and firm-level exchange rate exposure. Trade openness is measured by the ratio of exports plus imports to GDP. This suggests that firms in more open economies are more exposed to FX movements, likely due to their deeper integration with global markets and the prevalence of indirect channels. Furthermore, the authors show that strong creditor rights are negatively associated with firm-level FX exposure. This inverse relationship suggests that firms in countries with strong legal protections for creditors have stronger incentives to hedge risk because the costs of financial distress, such as bankruptcy or default, are more severe in those environments. Therefore, these firms engage more actively in risk management practices such as hedging.
Another contribution of this paper is the examination of institutional incentives. In countries with high bankruptcy costs, creditors have stronger enforcement rights. Managers are more likely to act in the interests of shareholders by actively managing risk to avoid distress. This supports the idea that the quality of corporate governance shapes hedging behavior and thus exchange rate exposure. While shareholder rights also have an effect, creditor protection is more consistent and economically important. 
The authors show that hedging is valuable in the presence of market imperfections, where financial distress and agency costs are real concerns. In contrast, assuming perfect capital markets, hedging should be redundant because investors can hedge risk on their own. However, in real-world settings, hedging helps stabilize cash flows, reduce the likelihood of distress, and ultimately preserve firm value. Most importantly, this study confirms that the institutional context, not just firm characteristics, plays a major role in shaping firm-level risk management decisions.

Johanson & Vahlne (2009)
Johansson and Vahlne revisited their classic 1977 Uppsala internationalization process model in order to reflect how business realities and theoretical paradigms have evolved. The original model was originally developed to explain how Swedish firms internationalize through gradual learning and commitment. It emphasized psychic distance and a gradual expansion path, progressing from temporary exports to wholly owned foreign subsidiaries. In the face of globalization and the increasing importance of relational and network-based business strategies, the authors revise their framework to highlight a new obstacle: liability of outsidership. This updated model claims that the real challenge in internationalization is not simply unfamiliarity with foreign environments (i.e., psychological distance). Rather, it is the firm’s removal from relevant business networks in target markets.
The revised model adopts a business network perspective. In it, markets are conceived as interconnected networks of relationships rather than isolated firms competing independently. In this relational context, successful internationalization is contingent on the presence of insiders, firms that are embedded in the relevant networks. Firms that are outside these networks face greater uncertainty and costs due to lack of access to shared knowledge, trust-based relationships, and market opportunities. Thus, being an outsider is re-imagined as an important liability, potentially more restrictive than being an outsider itself. This theoretical shift is supported by extensive empirical work in the business network literature.
The updated Uppsala model retains its original design of state and change variables. Johansson and Vahlne advance the model in three significant ways. First, they introduce the concept of relationship-specific knowledge that is developed interactively between firms through trust-based interactions and complements general internationalization knowledge. Second, they explicitly acknowledge the affective dimensions of relationships, such as affective trust and mutual commitment, which influence commitment decisions and learning processes. Third, they shift the emphasis from risk and uncertainty avoidance (a dominant concern in the 1977 model) to opportunity development, identification, and co-creation of opportunities through ongoing relationships.
This model better accommodates new internationalization paths. It allows for nonlinear and relational entry into foreign markets. It often uses the traditional “establishment chain” of representatives → sales offices → subsidiaries. Existing business relationships, rather than an objective assessment of country characteristics, often determine which markets a company enters and which entry method it uses. Furthermore, the model makes it clear that learning and commitment are deeply connected to identifying and exploiting opportunities. Commitment to partners fosters shared knowledge, trust, and the development of capabilities, and forms the basis for further international expansion. The internationalization process is less about overcoming psychological distance and more about achieving network position, becoming a trusted insider in foreign networks.
They argue that uncertainty in internationalization does not arise solely from environmental differences, but rather from the limited knowledge of firms and the external situation in a foreign network. Therefore, the firm must gradually learn through interaction and experience as it adapts to local contexts and strengthens relational ties. Two mechanisms still drive this process: (1) experiential learning from ongoing activities, and (2) relationship-based commitment decisions.  
This reconceptualization opens many avenues for future research. Johansson and Wallene propose to explore the intersection between their model and internalization theory (Buckley and Casson, 1976), as well as Dunning's (1980) eclectic paradigm, to better understand how firm-specific benefits are created and transferred through international networks. They also suggest deeper research into the distinction between external responsibility and external accountability. They recommend studies on how relationships function as a means of learning about cultural and organizational environments. Furthermore, the dynamics of international supply networks are an important phenomenon. This can be fruitfully studied using this revised model, through longitudinal case studies.

Kogut & Zander (1993)
Kogut and Zander (1993) developed an evolutionary theory of the multinational corporation (MNC). They argued that firms are essentially social communities that specialize in the creation, internal transfer, and recombination of knowledge. This theory challenges the traditional internalization view of the firm. Internalization holds that multinational firms are created primarily to circumvent market failures and transaction costs associated with cross-border transactions (Buckley and Casson, 1976; Williamson, 1975). Instead, Kogut and Zander argue that firms exist and expand internationally because they are more efficient than markets at transferring tacit knowledge, knowledge that is difficult to codify, articulate, and teach.
Their framework is a knowledge-based view (KBV) of the firm, which is aligned with a broader conceptual tradition that sees knowledge as a firm’s most important strategic resource (Grant, 1996). Kogut and Zander distinguish between information, declarative and codifiable content, and technical or procedural knowledge embodied in organizational routines. The complexity and embedded nature of knowledge make it less susceptible to market transactions and better suited to internalization. To operationalize tacit knowledge, the authors develop three dimensions: 1) codability, or the extent to which knowledge is expressed in documents; 2) teachability, or the ease with which others can be taught to use it; and 3) complexity, which refers to the number and interdependence of knowledge components involved in a task. These dimensions are used to predict whether knowledge is transferred internally (e.g., to wholly owned subsidiaries) or externally (e.g., through licensing agreements).  
Kogut and Zander found that the less codifiable and teachable knowledge is, and the more complex it is, the more likely it is to be transferred within the firm. Technologies that are relatively explicit and easy to teach are likely to be licensed to third parties. Other variables, such as the age of the technology and the number of previous transfers, were not statistically significant. They emphasized that it is the intrinsic nature of knowledge, not merely its maturity or history, that shapes the mode of transfer. These findings support the claim that multinational corporations do not arise simply because of savings in transaction costs, but because firms have unique, path-dependent capabilities to process and transfer knowledge more effectively than markets or unrelated firms (Nelson and Winter, 1982).  
Kogut and Zander do not completely reject transaction cost economics (TCE). They suggest that TCE and KBV are complementary. While TCE focuses on minimizing opportunism and contracting costs in imperfect markets (Williamson, 1975), KBV emphasizes the firm’s productive capacity to create and use knowledge. Firms do this not just to avoid internal risks, but also because internal governance structures provide a better platform for the transfer of complex and uncodified knowledge through shared routines and norms (Grant, 1996; Teece, Pisano & Shuen, 1997). In this way, MNCs become firm-specific knowledge repositories and tools. They evolve as experiential learning accumulates and is applied across borders.
Implications. Horizontal foreign direct investment (FDI), in which firms replicate their operations in new markets, should be viewed primarily as a process of knowledge transfer and embedding, not just as a market expansion tactic. When firms choose to establish wholly owned subsidiaries rather than license, they do so not only to protect intellectual property but also to build organizational capabilities in foreign markets. Internal transfer allows firms to develop empirical knowledge about local conditions. This knowledge can be reused for future innovations and products. Conversely, licensing arrangements, while often efficient in the short term, may result in missed learning opportunities and reduced flexibility in rapidly changing environments.

Kostova and Zaheer (1999)
Kostova and Zaheer (1999) develop a basic theoretical framework for understanding the legitimacy of multinational enterprises (MNEs). They highlight their unique complexities in securing and maintaining legitimacy in multiple organizational environments. Traditional theories of organizational legitimacy, particularly those rooted in sociology and economics, generally assume a relatively homogeneous environment and a single level of analysis. MNEs, however, by their very nature, challenge these assumptions. They operate across national boundaries and multiple institutional contexts, and are composed of semi-autonomous sub-units that must simultaneously respond to internal corporate expectations and external social norms.
Legitimacy, as defined by the authors, refers to the general perception or assumption that an organization’s actions are desirable, appropriate, or proper within a socially constructed system of norms, values, beliefs, and definitions. In the context of MNEs, legitimacy operates at multiple levels. First, there is external legitimacy at the MNE level, which refers to the acceptance of the MNE by the global “metaenvironment.” This includes not only the institutional environments of the home and host country, but also global actors such as the international media. Second, external legitimacy at the subunit level, where a subsidiary seeks acceptance by the specific institutional environment of its host country. Third, the authors introduce the concept of internal legitimacy, which is defined as the acceptance and support that a subsidiary receives from other parts of the MNE, especially from the headquarters. This internal dimension is particularly critical because MNE subunits are often dependent on the parent company for resources, strategic direction, and survival. The authors structure their theoretical contribution around three main sources of complexity: 1) environmental complexity, 2) organizational complexity, and 3) legitimacy process complexity.
Environmental complexity: The first source of complexity arises from the organizational diversity that MNEs must navigate. Unlike domestic firms, MNEs must operate in multiple institutional environments, such as the home country, host countries, and the global external environment (such as the media and transnational corporations). Each of these institutional environments includes three key dimensions outlined by Scott (1995): regulatory, cognitive, and normative pillars. The regulatory pillar includes formal rules, laws, and policies. The cognitive pillar reflects shared mental schemas, taken-for-granted assumptions, and culturally embedded logics. The normative pillar encompasses social values and expectations regarding appropriate behavior. The complexity here arises from the fact that each host country defines legitimacy differently across these three dimensions. The more institutional environments an MNE operates in, the greater the challenge in achieving legitimacy, but also the greater the opportunity to develop competencies through exposure to different norms.  Proposition 1 states that the legitimacy of an MNE as a whole depends on its ability to gain legitimacy in multiple host-country environments, each defined by distinct regulatory, normative, and cognitive institutions. Proposition 2 argues that the greater the institutional distance between home and host countries, the more difficult it will be for an MNE or its subsidiaries to gain legitimacy. Proposition 3 introduces a dynamic insight: while operating in many countries increases environmental complexity, it also allows the MNE to develop transferable legitimacy-related competencies, thereby reducing the challenges faced by individual subsidiaries.
Organizational Complexity: The organizational complexity of the MNE exacerbates legitimacy challenges. The authors describe MNEs as having different orientations. Ethnocentric MNEs impose their country’s practices abroad, polycentric MNEs adapt to each host country separately, and geocentric MNEs attempt to construct a unified global identity. Each orientation requires different concepts for legitimacy management. MNEs are not single entities but rather complex organizations composed of geographically dispersed subunits that are both embedded in local environments and integrated into a larger corporate structure. Each subsidiary must gain legitimacy from two distinct domains: external legitimacy from host country stakeholders, and internal legitimacy from the parent company and peer subunits. This dual requirement can create tension if the local adaptation required for external legitimacy conflicts with the global standardization desired by headquarters. This is exacerbated in ethnocentric MNEs, where practices and values are centered in the home country, and reduced in geocentric or polycentric MNEs that allow for greater local flexibility. Proposition 4 states that the tension between internal and external legitimacy increases with the structural and operational complexity of the MNE. Proposition 5 posits that geocentric MNEs, those with a global, cosmopolitan orientation, are more likely to manage legitimacy tensions effectively than ethnocentric or polycentric firms. Proposition 6 emphasizes that subsidiary survival is contingent on achieving both internal and external legitimacy; failing to satisfy either can undermine a subsidiary’s standing and longevity within the MNE.
Complexity of the Legitimacy Process: The source of complexity lies in the limited social and rational nature of legitimacy. Gaining legitimacy is not simply a matter of following the rules. It is a social process shaped by perceptions, stereotypes, and information asymmetries. This leads to phenomena such as liability of foreignness (LOF) and legitimacy spillover. Because host-country actors often lack in-depth knowledge of MNEs, they use cognitive shortcuts to assess legitimacy. For example, negative behavior by a foreign subsidiary can tarnish the reputation of other subsidiaries or even all firms in the same country. Importantly, negative spillovers are generally stronger and more damaging than positive ones, making reputational risk a major concern for MNEs.  Proposition 7 highlights that the legitimation process is boundedly rational, as host-country stakeholders use heuristics, stereotypes, and incomplete information when evaluating foreign subsidiaries. Proposition 8 asserts that negative legitimacy spillovers (e.g., a scandal in one subsidiary) have stronger and broader impacts than positive spillovers. Proposition 9 states that a subsidiary’s legitimacy may be affected by the legitimacy of other subsidiaries of the same MNE in the same host country or region. Proposition 10 adds that a subsidiary may also be judged based on the legitimacy of other MNEs from the same home country, reinforcing the importance of country-of-origin effects.
Implications. First, MNE managers need to recognize that legitimacy is not a uniform or fixed attribute. It must be actively cultivated at different levels and in diverse organizational environments. Second, there is no one-size-fits-all strategy. If organizational requirements differ significantly, success in one area may lead to failure in another. Third, firms need to be particularly vigilant about managing reputational risk. Because negative legitimacy spillover from a subsidiary or peer firm can have far-reaching consequences. Finally, the authors encourage firms to invest in organizational designs and cultures that facilitate legitimacy across borders. By fostering geocentric or polycentric orientations that are better aligned with host-country expectations.

La Porta et al. (1998)
La Porta et al. (1998) provide an empirical investigation of how differences in legal origins affect financial development across countries. Their paper argues that the effectiveness of financial markets, both equity and debt, is strongly influenced by the legal traditions inherited by countries. Whether they derive from English common law or various forms of civil law (French, German or Scandinavian). The authors suggest that legal systems shape investor protection. This affects the structure, depth and efficiency of financial markets.
Financial markets perform better when investors are confident that they will not be expropriated by insiders (such as managers or large shareholders). Such confidence depends on the existence and enforcement of legal protections. The authors argue that common law systems generally provide stronger protections for investors through legal rules and judicial efficiency. In contrast, civil law systems, particularly the French type, are more controlled by the state and shareholders and provide weaker investor rights, thus leading to underdeveloped financial systems.
La Porta et al. develop several indicators to compare the legal systems of 49 countries. These include shareholder rights measures (such as proxy voting, requirements to call shareholder meetings, and mechanisms for redressing minority shareholders), creditor rights (such as the availability of collateral and bankruptcy protections), and the quality of law enforcement. They find that common law countries are significantly superior to civil law countries in shareholder and creditor rights. These legal differences persist even after controlling for levels of economic development, suggesting that legal origin has an independent effect.
This analysis links legal protections to outcomes in financial markets. The authors find that countries with stronger investor protections, again typically common law countries, have larger and more active capital markets. These countries exhibit higher ownership dispersion among listed firms, larger stock markets relative to GDP, and more developed debt markets. Conversely, countries with weaker legal protections (particularly French civil law countries) tend to have more concentrated ownership and smaller and less efficient capital markets. These findings support the argument that legal systems directly affect the willingness of foreign investors to participate in financial markets.
This paper examines the mechanisms through which legal rules matter. One key channel is enforcement. Even if a country has strong laws, they may not foster financial development unless those laws are enforced reliably. They find that countries with efficient and honest legal systems also tend to have better financial outcomes. Thus, both the content of the law and the capacity of the legal system to enforce it are critical.
La Porta et al. challenge dominant development theories that emphasize cultural or political explanations for financial underdevelopment. They acknowledge that many factors influence financial systems, but they show that the origin of law, often an artifact of colonialism, has a powerful and persistent influence. They show that legal infrastructure is fundamental to economic development, not merely a technical detail of governance. They provide a framework for future comparative research, one that emphasizes legal institutions as key determinants of financial structure and corporate governance.

Li & Filer (2007)
Li and Filer (2007) examine how the nature of a country’s governance environment, such as its reliance on public ordering (rule-based systems) versus private ordering (relationship-based systems), shapes foreign investors’ preferences between foreign direct investment (FDI) and foreign portfolio investment (FPI). The old wisdom is that weak governance deters investment equally. But the authors argue that countries with underdeveloped public governance systems can still attract significant FDI because of compensatory mechanisms of private order.
Public ordering refers to systems of governance in which rules are created and enforced by formal government institutions. These are rule-based systems that rely on laws, courts, and government enforcement mechanisms to structure and protect economic and social transactions. Key features of public order: 1. Law-based enforcement: Legal systems (e.g., contract law, property rights) govern transactions. They provide predictability and deterrence through courts and regulatory institutions. 2. Third-party arbitration: Disputes are resolved by impartial arbitrators (e.g., judges, regulators). These arbitrators are not tied to ongoing relationships between the parties. 3. Transparency and formality: Rules are codified and publicly known. Institutions ensure due process and equality under the law. 4- High connectivity in developed economies: These systems are dominant in advanced markets where there is a strong institutional infrastructure (legal, regulatory, financial). 5- Support for unprecedented transactions: Companies can trade without personal knowledge of their partners, because the execution mechanisms are external and standardized.
In contrast, private ordering refers to systems of governance based on personal trust, informal relationships, and social norms rather than state institutions. These are relationship-based systems. They typically operate where public institutions are weak, unreliable, or absent. Key features of private order: 1. Relationship-based enforcement: Trust, reputation, and long-term relationships form the basis of cooperation and deterrence. 2. Informal governance: Disputes are resolved within or through community norms, not the courts. Contract terms may be implicit or verbal. 3. Opportunism control through embeddedness: Social sanctions and the threat of exclusion from future transactions deter bad behavior. 4. Common in emerging and weak institutional environments: Often seen where the rule of law is not trusted or corruption is widespread. 5. Support for frequent transactions: Business partners prefer to work with known individuals. Expansion is slower and limited to the network.
In rule-based systems, MNEs can operate with a higher degree of trust in property rights, contracts, and dispute resolution. This enables them to scale, outsource, and structure operations effectively across partners. In relationship-based systems, MNEs must rely on local partners, joint ventures, or vertical integration to mitigate risks from uncertain implementation and opportunism.
The authors make an important distinction between these two types of investment. Foreign direct investment typically involves long-term commitment and operational control by the investor. This enables the firm to reduce reliance on foreign governance structures. In contrast, FPI is characterized by passive investment and a strong dependence on transparent and enforceable rule of law institutions. As a result, in weak rule-based governance environments, investors are more likely to invest in foreign direct investment. It allows them to reduce risks through direct control rather than relying on public institutions for support. This leads to the first hypothesis of the paper: countries dominated by relationship-based systems tend to attract more foreign direct investment than FPI. This is because investors prefer to operate in structures that they can influence internally rather than being dependent on unreliable legal systems.
In countries with rule-based systems, FPI becomes more attractive. In such circumstances, reduced transaction costs and reduced agency problems allow investors to confidently participate in stock markets without the need for operational controls. This constitutes the second hypothesis: countries with strong rule-based governance experience deeper stock markets and higher levels of FPI, as reflected in a higher ratio of stock market value to GDP. Importantly, Li and Filer avoid a binary distinction between public and private order. Instead, they suggest that governance systems exist along a continuum. Most countries exhibit a combination of both forms, with their respective efficiencies and limitations. The authors emphasize that private order, while flexible and often effective in low-trust environments, suffers from diseconomies of scale. As relational networks expand beyond close ties, the cost of monitoring and enforcing norms increases rapidly. Conversely, public order benefits from economies of scale, allowing rules-based systems to efficiently manage a growing number of impersonal transactions.
In rule-based systems, companies can rely on legal protections for intellectual property, labor rights, and dispute resolution. This allows for decentralized strategies, including licensing and partnerships. In contrast, companies entering relationship-based systems often prefer subsidiaries or joint ventures to maintain operational control and protect assets through private agreements and relationships. 
They explain how investor behavior reflects the “agency problem,” the challenge of ensuring that managers act in the best interests of shareholders. In rule-based systems, public regulation serves as an external governance tool to mitigate agency risks. In contrast, in private-order environments, internal mechanisms such as ownership concentration and managerial oversight become more important. Thus, FDI, with its internal control structures, becomes a more effective way to invest in such settings.
Li and Filer’s research also considers the peculiar case of China, a country that lacks a strong rule-based governance system but has consistently attracted large amounts of foreign direct investment. The authors suggest that China’s reliance on strong private ordering mechanisms, including government-business relationships and networks of relationships, compensates for its formal institutional deficiencies. Other developing countries can similarly attract foreign direct investment by building trust-based governance structures, even in the absence of a fully mature legal framework.

Li et al. (2008)
Management ties refer to the social and professional connections that managers establish with individuals and organizations. This can affect the operations and performance of their company. These ties act as a form of social capital and enable managers to access valuable resources, information, and opportunities that may not be available through formal organizational channels. According to Lee et al. (2008), management ties are generally classified into two main types. 1.1 Business ties: Relationships with stakeholders in the business ecosystem, including buyers, suppliers, and competitors. 2. Government ties: Relationships with government officials, industry bureaus, regulators, and other public sector entities. 
This paper examines the common belief that managerial ties are equally beneficial in emerging economies. It focuses specifically on the institutional context of China. Drawing on social capital and social network theory, this study examines how managerial ties, categorized into business ties (with buyers, suppliers, and competitors) and government ties (with officials, regulators, and industry bureaus), affect firm performance under different conditions of market heterogeneity, including firm ownership, competitive uncertainty, and structure. The main argument challenges the assumption that managerial ties always create value. Instead, it proposes a contingency view in which the effectiveness of such ties varies depending on the firm’s internal or external situation and environmental conditions.
The empirical findings highlight key differences between domestic and foreign firms. While both types of firms use managerial ties to a similar extent, their outcomes differ significantly. For domestic firms, the relationship between ties and performance is monotonic and positive. Ties consistently enhance performance by facilitating access to critical resources, market information, and regulatory support. In contrast, for foreign firms, the effect follows an inverted U-shape. It suggests that while moderate use of ties can improve performance, excessive reliance on ties leads to diminishing or even negative returns. This curvilinear pattern highlights the liability of being foreign. Foreign firms struggle to extract similar value from their networks due to cultural incongruence, limited integration into local institutions, and a tendency to mismanage or overuse relational strategies.
The effectiveness of relationships is moderated by market conditions. Under high competitive intensity, the value of managerial relationships for both foreign and domestic firms decreases. As more firms gain access to similar networks, the strategic advantage of linkages is lost due to information redundancy and leveling of the playing field. However, in conditions of high structural uncertainty, when market conditions are unstable and unpredictable, managerial relationships become more valuable. In such environments, relational governance compensates for institutional gaps by providing adaptability, informal enforcement, and timely access to local knowledge and support.
This study makes two significant contributions to the literature. First, it enriches international business research by showing that while foreign firms can establish and leverage managerial ties, they remain at a competitive disadvantage relative to domestic firms. Second, it extends social network theory by proposing a probabilistic view of managerial ties. Their value is context-dependent and varies with firm ownership, competition, and uncertainty. The paper also adds nuance to the discussion of institutional gaps, arguing that while relational governance can substitute for weak formal institutions, its effectiveness is neither universal nor unlimited. Managers, especially those in foreign firms, should be cautious not to overinvest in relationships or rely on them unduly, especially in highly competitive environments where strategic differentiation is critical.

Li et al. (2009)
This study examines how foreign firms achieve profitability in China by adopting competitive strategies (differentiation or low-cost positioning). And how these strategies interact with managerial ties, in particular, political ties and business ties, in the unique institutional and market environment of an emerging economy. It draws on Porter’s (1980) competitive positioning theory. The authors argue that occupying an attractive position in the industry, whether through product differentiation that commands a premium or by offering comparable products at a lower cost, can lead to superior performance.
In China, a differentiation strategy is particularly powerful for foreign companies. Because local consumers often perceive their products as superior and are willing to pay a premium. Given the oversupply of homogeneous and low-quality goods in the Chinese market, largely due to the widespread focus on low-cost production among domestic companies, foreign companies that differentiate can stand out and satisfy unmet consumer demands. This hypothesis, which states that differentiation positively affects profitability, is supported. Meanwhile, when companies can achieve efficiency and offer comparable value to competitors by taking advantage of China’s cheap labor and manufacturing base, the low-cost strategy remains viable. However, as Porter warns, offering low prices without meeting initial customer expectations is not enough. This hypothesis, which shows the positive impact of a low-cost strategy on profitability, is also supported.
The authors introduce an interactional perspective and emphasize how the institutional context in China necessitates a reliance on managerial relationships. These relationships fall into two categories: political relationships, connections with government officials and bureaucratic institutions, and business relationships, connections with suppliers, buyers, and even competitors. While previous research recognizes the benefits of such ties, this study assesses how they moderate the relationship between strategic positioning and firm performance. Political ties, although essential for overcoming administrative barriers and accessing restricted sectors, may also come with significant costs. Heavy reliance on political actors can expose firms to bureaucratic interference, favoritism, or pressure to adhere to non-market agendas that may conflict with the firm’s competitive objectives. The authors find that political ties negatively impact firm profitability and undermine the effectiveness of differentiation and low-cost strategies. This is because political conflicts may distract firms from creating real competition in the market and create inefficiencies through adherence to informal commitments.
In contrast, trade ties significantly enhance performance by reducing external liability. These ties facilitate the transfer of local knowledge. They enhance resource sharing. They support adaptive learning in the dynamic and uncertain Chinese market. The findings confirm that firms with strong trade ties can implement both differentiation and low-cost strategies more effectively. Trade ties help firms identify market gaps, gain better access to suppliers, improve distribution, and even benefit from local innovations. These benefits are achieved without the coercive costs often associated with political ties, as trade ties in China are largely based on reputation and cooperation.
The conceptual model of this study illustrates these interactions: while differentiation and low-cost positions have a direct impact on profitability, their effects are filtered through the nature of the firm's external relationships. The model suggests that firm performance in emerging markets cannot be understood solely through internal strategies, but must also account for the firm's institutional embedding and network position.
Success depends not only on adopting appropriate competitive strategies but also on aligning those strategies with relational resources. Political ties, while providing initial market access, may inhibit long-term strategic execution. In contrast, business ties act as strategic complements. They help firms refine their offerings. Compete effectively. The study recommends that foreign firms prioritize building deep local business networks and avoid over-reliance on political connections, especially as China’s market mechanisms continue to mature.

Meyer (2001)
Mayer (2001) examines how institutions and transaction costs jointly shape the entry strategies of foreign firms in transition economies, with a particular focus on Eastern Europe. Mayer draws on institutional theory and transaction cost economics (TCE). He argues that the unstable and evolving nature of institutions during economic transition affects the transaction costs faced by multinational corporations (MNCs). This affects their preferred modes of market entry.
Meyer begins by placing the discussion in the institutional context of transition economies (where countries are changing from centrally planned to market-oriented systems). In such environments, the institutional framework, including regulatory, normative, and cognitive institutions, is often inconsistent, ambiguous, and rapidly changing. These underdeveloped or transitional institutions cannot provide the stability needed to reduce uncertainty, enforce contracts, or support predictable business operations. As a result, they increase search, negotiation, and contracting costs for foreign investors.
From a transaction cost perspective, these higher costs, especially in the form of market failures, asymmetric information, and regulatory ambiguities, drive firms to internalize operations through hierarchical entry modes, such as wholly owned subsidiaries. These modes allow firms to maintain control over their operations, especially when transferring tacit knowledge, technological expertise, or managerial skills that are difficult to codify or protect in weak organizational environments. In line with internalization theory, Meyer argues that technology-based firms or those associated with the protection of intellectual property rights are particularly inclined toward full ownership to reduce leakage and ensure operational stability.
Mayer emphasizes that internalization is not without costs. Establishing a subsidiary involves high internalization costs, adjusting strategy, adapting organizational culture, and building local capabilities. In the early stages of transition, when organizational gaps are more severe, joint ventures or network-based coordination may be preferable because companies do not have sufficient knowledge of local conditions or access to reliable partners.
This paper emphasizes the dynamic nature of entry-mode decisions. As transition economies stabilize and institutions become more reliable (e.g., better contract enforcement, stronger property rights, reduced regulatory uncertainty), the transaction costs of market operations decrease. This encourages multinational firms to shift from joint ventures to wholly owned subsidiaries. This is consistent with the theory of institutional evolution, which assumes that firms adapt their governance structures in response to changes in the organizational environment.
One of Meyer’s contributions is to show the role of institutional familiarity, or psychological distance. When reforms in host countries reduce institutional distance from Western markets, firms benefit from lower adjustment costs, reduced cultural friction, and easier training of local employees. This further incentivizes multinationals to internalize operations over time. Meyer also shows that not all transaction costs are institutionally driven. Some arise from the nature of the business itself. For example, the need to transfer and embed managerial knowledge, which is inherently tacit and context-dependent, requires direct interaction, making wholly owned subsidiaries the optimal choice even in the absence of strong institutions.

Miller and Reuer (1998)
Miller and Reuer (1998) examined how firm-specific strategies, rather than industry-wide characteristics, affect a firm’s economic exposure to foreign exchange rate changes. Previous literature assumes a homogeneous response across industries to exchange rate fluctuations. However, the authors argue that individual firms in the same industry can adopt distinct strategies, such as different levels of foreign direct investment (FDI), export intensity, and R&D involvement. This shapes their actual exposure to exchange rate fluctuations.
The authors define economic exposure as the degree to which unexpected currency fluctuations affect the firm's future cash flows and market value. In contrast to accounting or transaction exposure, which are more short-term and contractual. Their main argument is that strategic decisions, particularly in terms of international integration, moderate a firm's vulnerability to currency risk. For example, while some firms may rely heavily on exports, which are sensitive to exchange rate changes, others may invest directly in foreign markets through foreign direct investment, which allows them to localize operations and reduce their exposure to currency fluctuations.
One view of this paper is the invalidity of using a single currency or weighted currency index as a global proxy for foreign exchange risk. This is because different currencies and the markets they represent have unique and non-uniform impacts on multinational operations. The authors emphasize that foreign exchange impacts are location-specific, and therefore exchange rate exposure should be assessed in light of the firm’s geographic and strategic footprint.
Empirically, this study supports several hypotheses. First, firms with higher export intensity exhibit higher economic exposure, as their revenue streams are more directly affected by exchange rate changes. Second, firms with higher levels of foreign direct investment (FDI) exhibit lower exposure, as FDI facilitates the localization of revenues and costs and thus buffers against exchange rate effects. Third, higher R&D intensity is associated with lower exposure, suggesting that innovation leads to differentiated products and pricing power, reducing sensitivity to exchange rate changes. Fourth, a firm’s exposure increases in proportion to the intensity of its industry’s foreign sales, suggesting that industry-level globalization can increase exposure, even if a firm is less globally integrated.
The finding that FDI reduces economic exposure is significant. Through FDI, firms can circumvent foreign market uncertainties, such as currency fluctuations, by localizing operations, production, and sales in the same currency area. This reduces reliance on cross-border transactions and allows for greater strategic control.
Currency risk is not simply an environmental threat, but one that can be actively managed through strategic decision-making. They advocate a more nuanced understanding of FX exposure that encompasses firm-level behavior and strategic intent, challenging the simplistic view that all firms in a given industry face equal exposure.

Peng (2001)
Peng (2001) examines the growing influence of the resource-based view (RBV) in international business (IB) scholarship and positions it as a transformative theoretical innovation. It has not only shaped IB research but has also been enriched by it. He addresses three main questions: (1) How influential has the RBV become in IB? (2) What explains its widespread diffusion? (3) What is the current state of RBV research in IB and where is it headed? 
To address the second question, Peng draws on Rogers’s (1983) innovation framework and describes the RBV as a “theoretical innovation.” Its acceptance can be explained by five perceived features: comparative advantage, consistency, simplicity, testability, and observability. First, the RBV’s comparative advantage lies in its shift from traditional industry-level determinants of performance, emphasized by industrial organization economics, to firm-level determinants, such as capabilities and resource endowments. Second, the RBV is consistent with existing behavioral and economic traditions and extends insights from Penrose’s (1959) theory of firm growth while integrating ideas from five major theories of IO economics (Connor, 1991). Third, the RBV is deceptively simple and conceptually intuitive, which facilitates its acceptance. Fourth, the RBV’s flexibility and testability allow it to be used in combination with other theories, such as TCE and institutional theory. Finally, its widespread exposure through major academic events and high-visibility publications has accelerated its dissemination.
To illustrate the application of the RBV to international business research, Peng introduces a 2×2 framework that categorizes RBV-based IB studies by firm size (large vs. small firms) and level of international experience/complexity (low vs. high). This matrix organizes the literature into four quadrants: (1) large, experienced multinational enterprises (MNEs), where the RBV has been used to examine global strategy, knowledge transfer, and subsidiary evolution. (2) large but relatively inexperienced firms, often in emerging markets, where the RBV highlights the challenges of building global capabilities. (3) small, highly internationalized firms (e.g., “born globals”), where the RBV explains rapid internationalization as rooted in unique entrepreneurial capabilities. and (4) small and inexperienced firms, where the RBV can help assess the conditions under which global expansion may be viable.
Peng compares RBV with transaction cost economics (TCE) and highlights three important differences. First, TCE sees firms’ choice to internalize as a response to opportunism and market failure. But RBV sees it as a result of resource heterogeneity. Some firms are simply better at using and protecting their valuable knowledge. Second, TCE focuses on discrete input decisions under static conditions. But RBV emphasizes dynamic processes and capability building, where each international move contributes to cumulative learning. Third, TCE focuses on exploiting existing advantages for the firm. RBV emphasizes continuous exploitation and development, and sees firms evolving through resource recombination and experiential learning.
The RBV has helped to increase the theoretical rigor of IB research. IB studies have historically been criticized as fragmented and phenomenon oriented. They have now benefited from the unifying power of the RBV. The RBV provides a unifying framework for analyzing diverse topics, from MNC global strategies and spin-off initiatives to entrepreneurial internationalization and knowledge transfer. This has allowed researchers to connect disparate empirical observations through a consistent theoretical lens that emphasizes VRIN (valuable, rare, inimitable, and non-substitutable) resources as the foundation of global competitive advantage (Barney, 1991).
Another point is that Peng does not see IB as a passive recipient of the RBV logic. He emphasizes the reciprocal contribution of IB to RBV theory, particularly through the notion of international knowledge and local embeddedness. IB scholars have long examined how firms create firm-specific advantages through learning domestically, adapting to local institutions, and overcoming external commitments, insights that predate the RBV but are directly aligned with its emphasis on inimitable sources of path-dependent knowledge.

Peng (2004)
Peng (2004) directly engages with Peter Buckley's (2002) claim that the field of international business (IB) may be “ending” due to the lack of a single, unifying question. Peng agrees with Buckley that a clearly defined research question is essential for scholarly progress. But he disagrees with the claim that such a question does not exist. Instead, Peng argues that the field of IB has always been centered around a persistent question. That is, “What determines the international success and failure of firms?”
Peng argues that Buckley’s historically defined stages in IB research, explaining the flow of foreign direct investment, understanding the nature of MNEs, and analyzing globalization, are in fact manifestations of this broader, unifying question. Rather than seeking a new paradigm, Peng suggests that scholars should recognize this question as the core of the discipline and use it to organize and advance future work. He notes that IB is uniquely positioned to answer this question, given its comparative advantage in addressing the complexities of firm performance in cross-border contexts.
Peng also responds to Buckley's three proposed "candidate questions" for the future of IB. He criticizes the first, which explains the sequence of countries' entry into the global economy. He sees it as outside the core of IB because it relies on nation-level analysis and is more appropriate for political science, economic history, and institutional economics. The second question, concerning the relationship between cultural background and firm organization, is rejected as overly descriptive and conceptually ambiguous, especially given the declining use of culture in mainstream IB theory. The third question, the empirical measurement of globalization processes, is rejected as a methodological rather than theoretical question, with limited capacity to structure the long-term intellectual development of a field.
Peng argues that IB, as a discipline, is still often considered pre-paradigmatic due to its methodological diversity and broad interdisciplinary scope. Drawing on Thomas Kuhn’s theory of scholarly development, Peng emphasizes that adopting a guiding question is essential for a discipline to mature into a distinct academic discipline. He suggests that embracing the corporate-centric question of international success and failure provides the continuity, novelty, and scope necessary to fulfill this role. According to Peng, this question bridges micro and macro perspectives and resonates with theoretical and practical concerns.
Peng also responds critically to what he sees as Buckley’s “scientific mercantilism,” the idea that IB should seek to “export” more theory than it imports from other fields to establish its legitimacy. Instead, Peng promotes an open and integrative approach, emphasizing IB’s comparative advantage in studying cross-border dynamics at the firm level rather than its need to protect disciplinary boundaries. He encourages IB scholars to remain interdisciplinary and collaborative, arguing that intellectual openness is a strength, not a weakness.
Peng offers several recommendations. He suggests that IB’s boundaries should remain permeable to allow for a vibrant exchange of ideas. The focus of the discipline should remain on its comparative advantage, namely, understanding firm performance in international contexts, rather than seeking to isolate itself. He concludes that as long as IB provides meaningful insights, it will remain a vital and respected discipline, united by its constant pursuit to determine the success and failure of international business.

Peng, Wang, and Jiang (2008)
Peng, Wang, and Jiang (2008) argue for the formal integration of the institution-based view (IBV) into the theoretical framework of international business strategy (IB) to better understand firm behavior in emerging economies. The institution-based view complements two traditional perspectives, the industry-based view and the resource-based view. It creates what they call a “strategy tripod.” This tripod provides a more comprehensive understanding of firm strategy and performance in environments where formal market-supporting institutions may be weak or evolving. The authors define institutions as the formal (laws, regulations) and informal (norms, values) “rules of the game” (North, 1990; Scott, 1995) that shape and constrain strategic behavior. In emerging economies, these “rules” are often in play. Firms are forced to address not only competitive and resource-based factors, but also institutional gaps and transitions.
Peng et al. address two main IB questions. 1. “What drives firm strategy in international trade?” 2. “What determines the success and failure of firms around the world?” In developed economies, where institutions are often fixed, traditional theories are sufficient. However, in emerging markets, where institutions are imperfect or in rapid transition, this assumption no longer holds. Therefore, the authors suggest that firm strategies should be understood in light of the evolving institutional context. They emphasize that institutional transitions in emerging markets, such as reforms in property rights, corporate law, and government transparency, create uncertainty that directly affects foreign entry strategies, corporate governance, and competitive advantage.
The paper illustrates this framework using four strategic areas: (1) anti-dumping as barriers to entry, where legal instruments are used to discriminate against foreign firms (an example of liability of foreignness is institution-based). (2) competition within and outside India, where both domestic reforms (e.g., investment in higher education, economic liberalization since 1991) and international drivers (e.g., globalization) have helped to develop India’s IT/BPO sector. (3) corporate growth in China, where weak formal institutions give rise to informal norms such as guanxi (interpersonal communication), which initially compensate for institutional gaps but gradually diminish in importance as formal institutions develop. (4) corporate governance in emerging economies, where the Anglo-American model of dispersed ownership is often unworkable. Instead, emerging economies often exhibit centralized ownership structures. This leads to principal and principal conflicts (e.g., between controlling shareholders and minority shareholders), rather than the more familiar principal and agent conflicts.
Theoretically, Peng et al. make three significant contributions. First, the institution-based perspective does not replace, but rather complements, existing theories. It strengthens the strategy tripod and helps researchers understand the importance of institutions, especially under conditions of uncertainty. Second, they link this perspective to transaction cost and internalization theory (Williamson, 2000; Buckley and Casson, 1976) by linking firm-level decision-making to broader institutional structures that govern information asymmetry, enforcement, and legitimacy. Third, they align IBV with evolutionary theory (Lewin and Welberda, 1999). It suggests that firms not only adapt to their institutional environments but also shape them, a dynamic that is particularly prevalent in the institutional transitions that are common in emerging markets.
Peng et al. conclude that in rapidly changing and organizationally volatile contexts the “rules of the game” are not fully understood or stable. The institution-based perspective then offers an essential lens for understanding IB strategy. It draws attention to how formal legal systems and informal norms simultaneously constrain and enable strategic choices. For both domestic firms expanding abroad and foreign firms entering emerging markets, understanding these institutional dynamics is essential for creating and sustaining competitive advantage.

Purda (2008)
Purda (2008) examines how the orientation of a country’s financial system, bank-based versus market-based, affects firm-level risk perceptions through the lens of credit rating assignment. This research examines firm-level credit risk in a systematic way. It asks whether the institutional structure of a country’s financial system shapes how credit rating agencies evaluate firms.
This paper argues that firms operating in bank-based financial systems have lower credit risk than similar firms in market-based systems. This is because bank-based systems create closer and more informative relationships between lenders and borrowers. As financial intermediaries, banks have privileged access to nonpublic (inside debt) information and can monitor firms closely. Furthermore, early detection of financial problems in these systems allows banks to engage in informal renegotiation of loan terms. This reduces reliance on costly and cumbersome formal bankruptcy processes.
In contrast, in market-based financial systems, firms rely on public stock and bond markets for financing. This involves unprecedented transactions and offers limited channels for information exchange or renegotiation. As such, credit rating agencies perceive higher levels of risk for these firms. Purda’s empirical analysis shows that even after controlling for traditional indicators of credit risk, such as firm size, leverage, industry, profitability, political connections, and tangibility of assets, the orientation of the financial system remains a statistically significant determinant of a firm’s credit rating.
One finding is that formal legal protections for creditors, including insolvency regimes and judicial transparency, do not affect credit ratings once the type of financial system is controlled for. This suggests that informal institutional mechanisms, such as the ability to renegotiate debt in a bank-based system, play a more central role than formal legal safeguards. This is in contrast to previous literature that has emphasized the importance of legal infrastructure in reducing information asymmetries and protecting creditors.
The article also shows how a country’s legal origin, for example, common law versus civil law, shapes its financial system. Common law countries tend to have larger stock markets. They tend to adopt market-based systems. While countries with weaker legal standards rely more on bank-based financing. It is this legal environment that is the structural nature of financial intermediation that best explains how credit risk is perceived at the firm level.
Purda (2008) provides evidence that firms in bank-based financial systems are perceived as less risky, not just because of economic principles or legal protections, but also because of the monitoring, renegotiation, and informational advantages inherent in banking relationships. This has significant implications for international trade scholars and practitioners, particularly in interpreting credit ratings across countries. However, the study cautions that the findings may not generalize to emerging markets. In these markets, the institutional roles of banks may be different and regulatory frameworks are still evolving.

Reuer, Klijn, & Lioukas (2014) 
This study by Royer, Klein, and Liukas (2014) examines the determinants of board involvement in international joint ventures (IJVs). The authors integrate insights from the corporate governance and alliance governance literature to examine IJV boards..
The authors conceptualize IJV boards as formal governance mechanisms. IJV boards are at the helm of an organization that is jointly owned by two or more parent companies. They provide the administrative control necessary to coordinate activities, monitor performance, and align strategic goals with the goals of the parent companies. Boards can also help resolve conflicts and poor performance by acting as a neutral platform for problem-solving. Due to cultural, geographic, and organizational distance, boards may be limited in their capacity to closely monitor or intervene in local operations. As a result, delegating authority to local management may have advantages. In settings where timely responsiveness or contextual knowledge is critical. This trade-off between control and delegation forms the core of the authors’ theoretical framework.
The paper suggests that a fundamental tension drives board involvement in IJVs. This tension stems from two defining features of IJVs: (1) common ownership by independent firms operating under imperfect contracts, which leads to transactional risks such as opportunistic behavior or strategic incoherence. (2) the transnational nature of IJVs, which complicates governance due to institutional and cultural diversity. These features make IJVs particularly vulnerable to coordination and control issues. On the one hand, when the potential for conflict or opportunism is high, such as in cases of end-market overlap or technology sharing through research and development, greater board oversight is needed to protect the interests of the parent company. On the other hand, when board members have difficulty understanding or responding to local conditions, such as when cultural distance or host country uncertainty is high, delegating decision-making to local management may be a more effective approach.
To investigate, the authors test four hypotheses. Hypotheses 1 and 2 suggest that board involvement increases in settings where closer monitoring is required. The increase in involvement is greater when there is significant overlap in the end market between the parent firms (increasing competitive tension and risk of misalignment). Board involvement also increases when the IJV involves research and development activities that increase the complexity of collaboration and the risk of knowledge leakage. Hypotheses 3 and 4 suggest the opposite effect under conditions that limit the efficiency or feasibility of centralized control. Board involvement is expected to be lower when the parent firms are separated by substantial cultural differences. When the IJV operates in a highly uncertain environment in the host country, where responsiveness and contextual knowledge are crucial. 
The authors found strong empirical support for all four hypotheses. They showed that board participation in greenfield IJVs is nonlinear in relation to the number of partners (increasing with coordination needs, but decreasing when complexity becomes unmanageable) and is not affected by risk-taking factors.
This study contributes to theory and practice. It extends corporate governance research to hybrid organizational forms such as IJVs. Joint ownership and strategic interdependence pose unique governance challenges. At the same time, it deepens the alliance governance literature by showing how contractual safeguards and informal relationship norms complement each other in aligning partners’ interests. In practice, the findings suggest that board structure should not be applied uniformly across ventures. Instead, governance design should reflect the strategic stakes and environmental complexity of each collaboration. 
The authors identify directions for future research. These include longitudinal studies of how board participation changes over the IJV life cycle, analyses of the functional consequences of board activity for both the investee and parent firms, and detailed fieldwork on the specific roles of individual directors. They also suggest comparative work across entry modes, such as examining whether similar board dynamics emerge in wholly owned subsidiaries.

Robinson (1981)
Robinson's article provides a historical and conceptual overview of the evolution of international business (IB) since World War II. He divides the development of international business into four distinct periods, each characterized by the number and nature of key actors influencing firm decision-making. These periods reflect changes in the global political, economic, and technological environments.
Period One: Robinson calls it the Two-Actor Era (immediately after World War II until about 1955). This period was defined by the dominance of the United States in global trade and investment. During this period, the key players were the firm itself and its foreign trading partners, such as customers, suppliers, and joint venture partners. U.S. companies benefited from high demand for their goods, stable exchange rates, and limited international competition. Foreign governments, especially in market-oriented countries, were generally receptive to U.S. capital and technology. During this phase, international trade strategy was often export-oriented. Foreign direct investment occurred reactively in response to large foreign market opportunities or trade barriers. This period was characterized by a lack of academic attention to international trade.
The second period is the era of the three actors (1955–1970). This period saw the emergence of multinational corporations (MNCs) and the increasing influence of host states as the third actor. During this period, the political and economic impacts of multinational activities became more pronounced. This prompted host countries to impose conditions on foreign investment. After recovering from the war, Japanese and European companies began to enter global markets, intensifying competition. Meanwhile, newly independent countries emerged in the wake of decolonization. MNCs developed globally integrated production and marketing systems, aided by advances in communications and transportation. Academic research remained dominated by economic perspectives, often identifying foreign direct investment with multinational operations.
The third period: the four-player era of the 1970s, described as the “time of trouble.” The parent states were introduced as the fourth major player. Several global developments converged during this period. The end of the Bretton Woods fixed exchange rate system, rising oil prices and resource nationalization, the emergence of global interest groups such as the Group of 77, and increasing public concerns about environmental and social issues. In response, governments in industrialized countries began to directly regulate international trade. The political dimensions of IB became impossible to ignore, and companies began to establish public affairs departments and recruit political scientists. These dynamics added new layers of risk and uncertainty and changed the nature of corporate planning.
Fourth Period: Robinson argues that by the early 1980s, international trade had entered a multi-actor era, defined by the influence of a wide range of international and transnational actors beyond states and corporations. These include non-governmental organizations, environmental groups, labor unions, consumer advocacy coalitions, cartels, and new international regulatory bodies. The breakdown of the nation-state and the rise of issue-based interdependencies further complicated the international business environment. In this context, traditional stock-based centralized control over global production systems became increasingly inefficient and costly. Robinson points to a shift from MNCs to international trading and service corporations. They emphasize flexible coordination, market access, and information flows over ownership and control of physical assets.
Robinson suggests that the main source of profit in international trade is the shift from production to information. He argues that future firms will derive value from real-time access to global data, capital, labor, technology, and market opportunities rather than from the exploitation of large-scale integrated production systems. In this sense, the classic MNC may give way to leaner, more adaptable international service and trade firms. This transformation is also driven by what Robinson sees as a slowdown in technological innovation, the decline in the comparative advantage of technology-rich firms from industrialized countries, and the increasing role of less developed countries in global production.
The paper also examines how managerial perceptions of risk, uncertainty, and control affect international investment decisions. Robinson notes that decision makers often avoid foreign investments unless they are familiar with the market and that risk perceptions are shaped by experience, organizational incentives, and cultural distance. For example, American companies typically prefer to invest initially in culturally similar countries such as Canada or the United Kingdom. Reward systems also shape strategy: while multinationals are motivated to maximize the efficiency of globally integrated systems, other international companies may pursue market-to-market profitability, leading to more decentralized strategies such as joint ventures or contractual partnerships.
Reflecting on the role of theory and the university, Robinson is critical of how management education and research have been slow to adapt to the evolving nature of international business. He argues that the academic focus on the multinational corporation remains concentrated, even as that model becomes less relevant. He calls for greater emphasis on the study of new organizational forms, such as transnational corporations and trading companies, and on understanding the complex interactions between environmental pressures and strategic responses. He emphasizes that theories, once adopted by decision makers, can become self-fulfilling and shape behavior, even if they are incomplete or incomplete.

Ronen and Shenkar (1985)
Ronen and Shenkar (1985) presented a combination of empirical research focused on clustering countries based on employees’ work-related attitudes, values, and goals. They review eight major studies in the field of comparative management and international organizational behavior to assess whether similar patterns exist across countries. Their main thesis is that although individual and occupational differences are important, countries can still be meaningfully grouped based on shared cultural characteristics.
This paper identifies four functions of country clustering: naming, displaying, summarizing, and predicting. 1. Naming provides a common label for countries in a cluster (e.g., Northern Europe, Latin Europe) that simplifies reference in research and practice. 2. Displaying helps visualize country relationships and similarities and aids comparison. 3. Summarizing country-level data into clusters reduces analytical complexity. 4. Predicting enables managers and researchers to infer likely similarities in work values and organizational behavior for countries that are in a cluster.
The main strength of Ronen and Shenkar’s study is their analytical approach to clustering, which relies on both statistical re-analyses and conceptual validity. They argue that clustering is not based on absolute cultural similarity. Rather, it is based on relative proximity, that is, how close two countries are compared to other countries in the data set. Relative similarity is important because it shows that cultural clustering is context-dependent and can be influenced by sample composition. The authors also caution against over-reliance on absolute criteria. They advocate flexible, iterative methods such as multidimensional scaling and smallest space analysis (SSA), which accommodate ordinal data and highlight visual patterns.
The authors identify four key dimensions that influence cultural clustering: geography, language, religion, and technological development. These structural variables have been shown to consistently shape work-related values and attitudes. For example, countries with similar linguistic and religious backgrounds often exhibit convergent values in job satisfaction and hierarchical preferences. Technological development also influences managerial practices and attitudes, as advanced economies exhibit more autonomy-based work cultures. Based on their composition, Ronen and Shenkar suggest several strong clusters, Anglo, Nordic, Germanic, Latin European, and Latin American, that are well-defined and stable. Other clusters, such as the Far East and Arab, are less well-defined and require further study. Some countries, such as Israel and Japan, are considered “stand-alone” because of their unique cultural characteristics and resistance to classification.
From a practical perspective, this study offers valuable implications for multinational companies and international HR professionals. Understanding which countries fall into a cultural cluster can help organizations adapt management styles, leadership approaches, and HR policies more effectively to local contexts. For example, a manager from a German-speaking country working in Austria may encounter fewer cultural barriers than a manager working in Brazil due to greater cultural proximity. The paper also highlights the need for greater cultural intelligence as multinational companies expand into less studied and developing regions. Managers need to understand the values of local employees to create effective communication, motivation, and retention strategies.
Ronen and Shenkar (1985) provide a basic framework for cultural clustering in international business. They show that despite national differences and methodological differences between studies, consistent clusters emerge based on shared values and work attitudes. Their work emphasizes the importance of cultural relativism in organizational behavior and strategy. They encourage future research to refine cluster boundaries, especially for ambiguous or underrepresented regions. By integrating structural and attitudinal factors, this paper advances the field of comparative management and remains a cornerstone of cross-cultural organizational analysis.

Roy and Oliver (2009)
Roy and Oliver (2009) examined how the institutional environment of a host country, particularly the rule of law and the control of corruption, shape the criteria that multinational enterprises (MNEs) use when selecting partners for international joint ventures (IJVs). The main argument of this study is that macro-level institutional environments influence micro-level managerial decisions through two critical concerns: 1) appropriation risks and 2) coordination costs. These concerns, in turn, lead firms to emphasize specific partner selection criteria. Drawing on institutional theory and transaction cost economics, the authors argue that a strong rule of law reduces both appropriation concerns (fear of opportunistic behavior and difficulty in enforcing contracts) and coordination costs (anticipated challenges in managing interdependence with partners). In contrast, this study shows that the control of corruption is often associated with organizational quality. But it does not have a significant effect on partner selection. This finding suggests that the rule of law directly affects firms' trust in their potential joint venture partners. But levels of corruption may not carry the same predictive weight in the partner evaluation process.
This research conceptualizes partner selection criteria into two distinct dimensions: task-related and partner-related. Task-related criteria refer to operational and tangible capabilities such as access to distribution channels, facilities, government permits, or other resources necessary to establish and execute an investment. Partner-related criteria, in contrast, focus on the potential partner’s relational and strategic attributes, trustworthiness, goal compatibility, reputation, and financial capacity. The study shows that concerns about allocation and coordination increase the emphasis on partner-related criteria. But they do not significantly affect task-related criteria. This distinction is important because it makes it clear that when operating in environments with weaker institutional supports, firms shift their focus from what partners can provide (task-related) to who they are and how reliably they can behave (partner-related).
The authors further examine the mediating role of these partnership concerns in the association of institutional quality (rule of law) with partner selection behavior. They find strong support for the idea that the relationship between rule of law and partner-related selection criteria is mediated by both allocation cost and coordination concerns. This insight suggests that the impact of institutional frameworks at the macro level is not direct, but rather filtered through the perception and assessment of risk by decision makers within firms. Thus, a high-quality legal environment does not just attract more IJVs. It also changes the logic of partner evaluation. For example, in countries with strong legal systems, firms are more confident in enforcing contracts and resolving disputes, which reduces their need to emphasize subjective attributes such as trust or value alignment.
The study finds no significant direct or indirect effects of corruption control. This suggests that even when corruption is present, as long as there is trust in the overall rule of law, firms may still perceive a reasonable level of protection against allocation and coordination risks. For practitioners, this suggests that although corruption is undesirable, it may not fundamentally hinder IJVs if firms believe that legal institutions still operate reliably.
They show that firms are not simply rational economic actors, but are shaped by the formal institutions in which they operate. This institutional embeddedness is particularly evident in international ventures where firms must navigate unfamiliar and potentially risky regulatory systems.
The paper recommends that in countries with weak rule of law, managers should increase their emphasis on assessing the trustworthiness, alignment, and reputation of potential partners. In such contexts, building personal relationships, seeking long-term alignment, and conducting due diligence on potential partners become critical success factors. The authors call for future research to examine additional aspects of institutional environments beyond rule of law and corruption, such as political stability, regulatory quality, and cultural distance. They also suggest that cultural norms may interact with legal institutions to shape how partnership concerns are perceived and managed.

Shaver & Flyer (2000)
Shaver and Flyer (2000) reexamine the accepted hypothesis that the geographic clustering of firms enhances firm performance because of economies of scale. Traditionally, agglomeration has been associated with positive externalities such as knowledge spillovers, specialized labor pools, and access to specific suppliers, as emphasized by Marshall (1920). Previous literature, including Porter (1998), has viewed these agglomeration effects as generally beneficial. It thus assumes that firms naturally seek proximity to industrial clusters. However, this paper challenges this assumption by introducing firm heterogeneity as a critical moderator. Shaver and Flyer argue that while firms do indeed benefit from economies of scale, they are also helped, and the net benefits of clustering are not distributed symmetrically across firms. Firms with superior technological capabilities, skilled human capital, and established supply chains already have internal access to many of the external resources provided by clusters. For these firms, clustering is a competitive risk because it can lead to the spillover of valuable resources, such as trained employees or proprietary technologies, to competitors. As a result, high-performing firms have less incentive to locate in clusters despite the existence of economies of scale.
Conversely, firms with weaker capabilities, those lacking technology, human resources, or distribution, benefit disproportionately from the externalities of agglomeration. These firms have little to lose. And they have much to gain from being located in industrial clusters, where they can benefit from shared infrastructure, knowledge, and labor. This asymmetry leads to what the authors describe as “adverse selection” in agglomeration. Stronger firms avoid clustering to protect their competitive advantages, while weaker firms are drawn to clusters to access external support. This contrasts sharply with the conventional wisdom that all firms should cluster if such economies of scale exist. The authors test their theory using empirical data on foreign greenfield investments in U.S. manufacturing industries. Their analysis of location choice and investment survival supports their predictions. Less capable firms are more likely to choose cluster locations, while more capable firms systematically avoid them. Furthermore, firms that avoid clustering have higher survival rates, especially in industries with strong economies of agglomeration.
This paper formulates four testable hypotheses: (1) many firms cluster, (2) some firms do not choose to cluster, (3) firms that cluster benefit from agglomeration externalities, and (4) firms that avoid clustering will be the best-performing firms in their industries. The authors find empirical support for all four, particularly the critical insight that firm performance generally does not improve with clustering. This challenges the large body of empirical literature linking geographic clustering to superior performance by arguing that such findings may obscure the effects of underlying selection. The study also offers strategic and policy implications. The reluctance of high-quality firms to cluster may destabilize existing industry clusters. Policymakers seeking to attract leading companies may need to rethink how cluster incentives are structured.
Shaver and Flyer also address two alternative explanations for their results. One is that resource constraints, not agglomeration externalities, may prevent firms from clustering. Input shortages and higher prices due to agglomeration may make clusters less attractive. Another explanation relates to market concentration. Smaller firms serving local markets may be more likely to cluster than larger firms serving national or regional markets. While plausible, the authors argue that these options do not fully explain the pattern of firm behavior observed in their data set. Overall, the paper's main contribution is to show that the benefits of agglomeration are not evenly distributed. Strategic location decisions are shaped by firm-level differences in capabilities.

Shenkar et al. (2008)
Shenkar, Luo, and Yeheskel (2008) critically evaluate the widely used metaphor of “cultural distance” in international management (IM) research. They propose a significant conceptual shift to “cultural friction.” The authors argue that while the distance metaphor, which originated with Kogut and Singh (1988), has popularized culture as a measurable research variable, it has also made it more difficult to study intercultural interactions within a narrow, static framework. This reliance on distance as a measure often aggregates differences across Hofstede’s cultural dimensions (e.g., power distance, uncertainty avoidance, individualism, masculinity). This leads to an incomplete understanding of culture as a stable set of characteristics and a unidimensional barrier to international operations.
The authors criticize the cultural distance metaphor for its theoretical and empirical limitations. They note that despite contradictory findings and weak explanatory power, the concept has remained unchallenged due to its methodological convenience and alignment with mainstream theories such as transaction cost economics. The metaphor tends to simplify culture into a measurable gap between nations. This often neglects power asymmetries and the reciprocal construction of cultural meaning. Furthermore, it reinforces ethnocentric biases by evaluating host countries in terms of deviations from the MNE’s core culture. It thereby silences the “hidden voice” of local actors and undermines the complexity of intercultural relations.
In contrast, the proposed metaphor of “cultural friction” is based on a social constructivist perspective. Friction is conceptualized not as a static measure but as a dynamic process and specific context of intercultural interaction. It emphasizes the encounter itself, where cultural systems encounter and interact with each other. Sometimes they clash, negotiate, or accommodate. This lens brings to the fore aspects that are often overlooked at a distance, such as the role of power hierarchies, political resistance, institutional embeddedness, and the iterative evolution of cultural interaction over time. Friction recognizes that globalization is not a smooth terrain of uniform movement, but rather is characterized by tensions, obstacles, and conflicting meanings.
The metaphor of friction offers several analytical advantages. It enables researchers to examine cultural relations in a more dialectical and nuanced way, taking into account not only objective incompatibilities but also the evolving nature of identity, legitimacy, and control in cross-cultural contexts. By shifting the focus from abstract national averages to the specifics of organizational and interpersonal encounters, friction encourages richer theorizing and empirically based research. For example, it helps explain why some cultural encounters lead to conflict, resistance, or misunderstanding, while others may foster mutual adaptation and innovation.
In practice, this reorientation has important implications for multinational enterprises (MNEs). Managers operating in global environments must move beyond simple assumptions about cultural difference and consider the relational, political, and institutional dynamics that exist in host countries. Friction makes them aware of the importance of local stakeholder perspectives, power imbalances, and the nonlinear path of cultural adaptation. 
This article describes a framework of “cultural friction” consisting of four elements: (1) the multinational corporation and the host country constituencies, which often have divergent interests; (2) cultural carriers (such as expatriates and local employees), who transmit cultural values and act as mediators of friction; (3) points of contact where friction occurs, for example, entry modes such as acquisitions or joint ventures; and (4) the process of cultural exchange, which involves ongoing negotiation over meaning, identity, and control.
The authors show how traditional theories such as transaction cost economics ignore the relational and political dynamics of cross-border investments. For example, focusing on “control” in entry mode decisions obscures the impact of host country resistance or strategic behavior. Furthermore, viewing culture merely as an “information cost” marginalizes local voices and reduces culture to an obstacle to management rather than a dynamic part of the interaction.

Tung and Verbeke (2010)
Tung and Verbeke (2010) provide a critical analysis of the state of cross-cultural research in international business (IB), focusing on the limitations and misuses of Hofstede's cultural dimensions and GLOBE. While the authors acknowledge Hofstede's fundamental influence, as evidenced by over 54,000 citations as of 2010, the authors caution that reliance on aggregate, static, and categorical cultural indicators (e.g., Hofstede's dimensions or the Cogut Singh index) may lead to erroneous, uninformed, or misleading conclusions.
The authors identify and challenge ten common assumptions in applied cross-cultural research. They categorize them into general limitations (e.g., the assumption of symmetry and stability of cultural scores), weaknesses in experimental design (e.g., the assumption of linear causality), and deeper conceptual flaws, referred to as "masks." 
The first category, generic constraints, involves assumptions that oversimplify the nature of culture. The first assumption is that cultural distance is symmetric, that is, the distance perceived by actors in country A relative to country B is equivalent to the perception in the opposite direction. This assumption has been shown to be unrealistic in many empirical contexts. For example, studies show that adjusting to life in the United States is easier for German immigrants than it is for Americans to adjust to Germany, despite the same formal cultural distance scores. Such asymmetry suggests that managerial perceptions and adjustment processes are not mutually exclusive. The second generic assumption concerns the temporal stability of cultural distance measures. It is assumed that scores obtained from datasets such as Hofstede's remain valid over time. However, in a rapidly globalizing and dynamically changing world, cultures evolve. Tung and Verbeke point to the narrowing of the cultural gap between Indonesian and American managers over time as evidence of "cross-cultural convergence..
The second category, experimental design weaknesses, represents methodological issues that limit the robustness and relevance of cross-cultural findings. The third hypothesis states a linear relationship between cultural distance scores and managerial outcomes. However, actual relationships are often non-linear. For example, cultural distance may create adaptation costs and innovation synergies that lead to curvilinear or threshold effects. The fourth assumption is that cultural distance directly drives managerial choices, such as decisions about entry modes or employee strategies. However, in practice, these choices are often the product of strategic considerations and firm-specific capabilities, not just cultural criteria. For example, a firm may choose a joint venture over a wholly owned subsidiary not because of cultural distance but because of local institutional pressures or resource complementarities. Treating cultural distance as a direct causal driver can lead to misattribution of effects, thereby oversimplifying the decision-making process. The fifth assumption is that cultural distance is a direct predictor of performance outcomes, such as profitability or subsidiary survival. This ignores problems of reverse causality and endogeneity. Successful firms may simply perform better in diverse environments regardless of cultural distance. Furthermore, performance outcomes are shaped by a variety of factors, including market competition, regulatory frameworks, and leadership agility, which may moderate or even overshadow the role of cultural distance. The sixth assumption conflates cultural distance with psychic distance. While cultural distance is usually operationalized through indicators such as those based on Hofstede's dimensions, psychic distance includes additional, subjective factors such as perceptions of organizational or educational differences. Studies have shown that Hofstede-based indicators capture only part of the broader psychological distance construct, meaning that relying solely on cultural distance may leave out crucial explanatory variables.
The third and final category, “conceptual masks,” refers to the uncritical transfer of cultural frameworks across contexts without regard to local complexity or institutional embeddedness. The seventh assumption, or “mask 1,” is the notion of spatial homogeneity within nations. Most mainstream cross-cultural studies assume that a country’s population is culturally uniform. However, globalization of the labor force, internal migration, and diaspora communities have created considerable intra-national diversity. For example, ethnic Chinese in Australia and Canada often display cultural attitudes more in line with Chinese nationals than with their Caucasian compatriots. The assumption of homogeneity in such contexts can produce misleading results. “Mask 2,” or assumption 8, suggests that cultural distance always leads to negative outcomes. While it is true that greater distance can increase transaction costs and coordination challenges, it can also stimulate innovation through exposure to different perspectives. The assumption only reinforces the negative effects of managerial conservatism and can become a self-fulfilling prophecy. The authors caution against using the hemophilia principle, which states that people strongly prefer similarity in managerial decisions.  The ninth assumption, “Mask 3,” assumes that cultural distance has a uniform effect across firms. This ignores differences in firm-specific advantages, such as international experience, leadership diversity, and immigrant networks, that can reduce or even negate cultural challenges. For example, a U.S.-based firm led by Taiwanese-born managers with active ties to Taiwan may not experience cultural friction as much as others. “Mask 4” criticizes the common practice of aggregating cultural dimensions into a single index, such as the Cogut-Singh (K-S) index. This simplification obscures the different effects of individual dimensions, such as uncertainty avoidance versus collectivism, on managerial behavior. Not all cultural dimensions interact with organizational processes in the same way, and aggregating them can obscure valuable insights. Researchers are asked to “unpack” the indicators and analyze each component separately to uncover more detailed and actionable findings.
The contribution of this paper is a reconceptualization of how cultural differences are operationalized and measured. Rather than treating culture as a fixed, national characteristic, the authors call for treating it as a multilayered, evolving construct. They urge researchers to embrace more sophisticated models that incorporate the interaction of culture with institutional, economic, and firm-specific variables.
The article also discusses alternative cultural frameworks, such as Schwartz's Values, Inglehart's Global Values Survey, and Trompenaars's typology, and encourages researchers to go beyond Hofstede and GLOBE when selecting conceptual tools. By comparing studies that stem from these diverse sources, Tong and Verbeke show that insights can be fundamentally different depending on the choice of framework and measurement method.

Vachani et al. (2009)
Vachani et al. (2009) examined how non-governmental organizations (NGOs) influence the formulation and implementation of social development strategies of multinational enterprises (MNEs), particularly through the lens of transaction cost economics (TCE). Drawing on the foundational theories of Coase (1937) and Williamson (1985), this paper examines how NGOs shape MNEs’ strategic decisions by increasing or decreasing the transaction costs associated with addressing social externalities in host countries. NGOs are positioned as important actors that can, depending on the context, challenge MNEs. Collaborate with them. and thereby change the costs of social engagement. These costs, in turn, influence whether to internalize social development efforts, partner with NGOs (hybrid governance), or rely on a priori market exchanges. This study demonstrates the role of TCE assumptions such as bounded rationality and opportunism in explaining how people manage uncertainty in their social strategies under different institutional conditions.
The authors classify MNE social development strategies along two dimensions: level and posture. 1. The level dimension refers to whether the firm adopts a basic strategy, simply addressing the negative externalities of its operations, or an enhanced strategy that actively contributes to the development of the wider community. 2. The posture dimension encompasses the firm’s stance, ranging from reactive (responding to NGO pressures only when necessary), to responsive, and proactive (anticipating and voluntarily addressing issues before they escalate). The authors argue that proactive firms are more effective in minimizing transaction costs and managing legitimacy threats, particularly when forming alliances with NGOs. In contrast, reactive firms incur higher costs due to inefficiencies in information gathering, delays in engagement, and higher reputational risk.
The host country institutional environment significantly influences the transaction costs associated with NGO interactions. In highly developed institutional contexts, there is typically a stronger presence and effectiveness of advocacy NGOs. These organizations exert pressure on MNEs and increase the transaction costs associated with advocacy or compliance with public oversight. Conversely, less developed institutional environments tend to foster operational NGOs, which play a more supportive role in service delivery and can reduce transaction costs by helping MNEs overcome poor governance and implementation challenges. This study suggests that NGOs in these contexts help MNEs by enhancing legitimacy, mediating social relations, and bypassing inefficient public institutions.
Institutional distance, the divergence between the institutional environments of the host and home countries, increases transaction costs. MNEs operating in distant environments face unfamiliar regulations, cultural norms, and civil society expectations. This complexity exacerbates the pressures on NGOs, especially in countries with developed civil societies. The authors argue that the greater the institutional distance, the higher the cost of developing effective and legitimate social strategies, unless mitigated by strategic alliances of NGOs. Furthermore, institutional dynamics, the extent and unpredictability of institutional change in the host country, add another layer of uncertainty. In dynamic environments, the influence of NGOs becomes more difficult to predict, increasing transaction costs for MNEs, who must continually adjust strategies to maintain legitimacy.
Vachani et al. (2009) present several theoretical propositions that illustrate how NGOs influence MNEs’ social development strategies by altering transaction costs, particularly in different organizational contexts. The first proposition states that when NGOs pressure industries to reduce negative side effects (e.g., environmental damage or labor violations), MNEs that are reactive, that is, those that respond only when forced to, incur higher costs. These costs arise from delays, adversarial interactions with NGOs, and difficulties in formulating appropriate responses under pressure. In contrast, the second proposition argues that MNEs that adopt a proactive stance, particularly those that form alliances with NGOs, experience lower transaction costs. These alliances help prevent conflict, increase legitimacy, and facilitate smoother strategy implementation.
The third proposition suggests that in highly developed countries, where NGOs are strong and active supporters, MNEs face increased transaction costs due to the scrutiny, public awareness, and regulatory pressure exacerbated by NGO activities. In contrast, the fourth proposition suggests that in environments with low institutional development, NGOs tend to be more operational. These NGOs assist in the implementation of social development programs, thus reducing transaction costs by serving as local intermediaries, helping MNEs bypass inefficient government systems, and gaining societal acceptance. 
The fifth proposition shifts the focus to institutional distance, the gap between the institutional environments of the MNE’s home and host countries. Greater institutional distance entails higher transaction costs, as MNEs must adapt to unfamiliar norms and regulations while managing increasing mismatches with local expectations. Proposition 6 explains this by stating that the negative effects of institutional distance are more profound when the host country has a developed civil society. A strong civil society empowers NGOs and enables them to hold MNEs accountable, thereby increasing the cost of mismatches.
Proposition 7 addresses institutional dynamics, the fluctuations in the host country’s institutional environment. In dynamic environments, frequent regulatory changes or political instability make it more difficult for MNEs to plan and sustain social initiatives, thereby increasing transaction costs. Proposition 8 states that the impact of institutional dynamics becomes more pronounced when civil society is highly developed, as NGOs can mobilize quickly in response to changes, holding companies accountable and increasing the associated pressure and costs.

Verbeke (2003)
Verbeke (2003) revisits Kogut and Zander’s (1993) influential knowledge-based view of the multinational enterprise (MNE) and advocates for a more evolutionary and integrative approach to international trade theory. He argues that future research on MNEs should combine insights from transaction cost economics (TCE) and KBV to better explain the evolution of MNE strategies, governance structures, and knowledge processes. Central to his proposal is the idea that a firm’s technological competence base (aligned with KBV) and its overarching governance structure (aligned with TCE) should co-evolve over time. This co-evolution is managed through organizational routines. Defined as stable behavioral patterns used to initiate, coordinate, and selectively control activities, these enable firms to align internal knowledge development and learning processes with appropriate governance mechanisms.
Verbeke highlights three main contributions of Kugot and Zander (1993) that challenge traditional internalization theory. First, they expand the conceptual boundaries of MNE analysis beyond a narrow focus on minimizing transaction costs. Instead, they argue for an emphasis on value creation through knowledge recombination and learning. Second, they criticize internalization theory for being overly prescriptive. It relies on bounded rationality and opportunism to explain assertive behavior. In many international knowledge transfers, opportunism is not a major concern, and bounded rationality is better seen as a constraint rather than a driving force. Third, internalization theory traditionally treats each transaction as independent. But Kugot and Zander emphasize the importance of situating decisions in the past experience, future trajectory, and social context of the firm, recognizing that knowledge transfer decisions are shaped by path dependence and embedded routines.
Verbeke does not reject internalization theory but argues for its enrichment. He claims that internalization theory is essential for understanding firm boundaries and governance choices, especially where market imperfections exist. However, he endorses a broader evolutionary perspective in which internalization theory is complemented by KBV’s emphasis on learning, knowledge accumulation, and capability development. The key to this integration lies in recognizing that MNEs are not simply instruments for overcoming market failure. They are dynamic entities that grow by developing and recombining knowledge through repeated interactions.
Verbeke suggests that the choice between using a TCE or KBV lens should depend on the purpose of the research. If researchers aim to analyze MNE boundaries and governance mechanisms, such as the decision to internalize versus outsource, TCE is a more appropriate framework. However, if the focus is on understanding how MNEs learn, adapt, and transfer knowledge across units, KBV provides deeper insights. Thus, an eclectic approach that combines both theoretical lenses allows for a more nuanced and richer explanation of MNE behavior. Verbeke sees the knowledge-based perspective not as a substitute for internalization theory, but as a complement that can address its limitations and expand its analytical power.
In addition to situating the work of Kugot and Zander as a pivotal extension of internalization theory, Verbeke grounds his integrative perspective in broader theoretical developments in IB, such as Dunning’s OLI framework. While OLI brings together the advantages of ownership, location, and internalization, Verbeke emphasizes that the “ownership” component naturally aligns with KBV (particularly through the possession of tacit and inimitable knowledge), while the “internalization” component stems from TCE. Furthermore, Verbeke’s framework encourages scholars to view MNEs not simply as a set of contracts (per TCE), but as evolving systems in which knowledge accumulation and governance design go hand in hand.

Vernon (1994)
Vernon identified three main areas in IB:
 (1) the familiar problems of national firms in foreign trade, (2) the management challenges at the headquarters of multinational enterprises (MNEs) that oversee global operations, and (3) comparative business practices in foreign countries. Each area addressed different business needs and relied on different conceptual foundations. The third area, comparative perspectives, was largely ignored because foreign markets were not yet considered vital to American firms and American business principles were considered universally applicable.
During the 1950s and early 1960s, the dominance of U.S.-based corporations and the global admiration of American management models led many scholars to assume the universality of domestic concepts. As a result, “international” business cases simply applied American frameworks to foreign settings. Vernon observed that many U.S. faculty and business leaders viewed international changes as exceptions rather than legitimate alternatives, which limited the development of a robust IB curriculum.
During the 1960s, Vernon focused his research on MNEs, particularly through the creation of the Multinational Enterprise Project, which collected data on 400 companies and supported more than 150 academic journals. This initiative helped to train a generation of IB scholars. At the same time, American companies began to realize the growing importance of foreign markets. Many of them moved toward global product divisions, which in turn made traditional international divisions obsolete. This mirrored what had happened at Harvard, where the separate international business department was dissolved with the expectation that functional departments (e.g., marketing, finance) would automatically internationalize. Vernon supported this shift, but later acknowledged that it probably happened too soon, as few departments had the capacity or interest to truly integrate global content.
In the 1970s, some multinationals experienced failures in foreign operations, often due to excessive organizational influence or a lack of cultural and institutional understanding. Similarly, the integration of IB into mainstream curricula stalled. Vernon likened the position of IB scholars to that of international vice presidents in MNCs, who were seen as having but no real influence. In the 1980s, Vernon, now in his seventies, argued that IB research needed to be revived, particularly in the area of comparative business systems, which continued to suffer from American-centric blind spots.

Zaheer (1995)
Zaheer (1995) examines the concept of liability of foreign (LOF), which is a major concern in international business research. LOF refers to the additional costs and disadvantages that multinational enterprises (MNEs) face when operating abroad compared to local firms. These costs arise from four main sources:  
Geographic distance (such as time zone and coordination challenges or costs directly related to geographic distance, such as travel, transportation, and coordination costs across distance and time zones), Company-specific unfamiliarity with the host market (company-specific costs based on the company's unfamiliarity and lack of roots in the local environment), Discriminatory practices or legitimacy issues in the host country (costs arising from the host country environment, such as the illegitimacy of foreign companies and economic nationalism), and Reputational constraints or burdens arising from the home country (costs arising from the home country environment, such as the illegitimacy of foreign companies and economic nationalism). 
The main question of Zahir's research examines whether foreign subsidiaries can better overcome these obligations by importing firm-specific organizational practices from their parent companies or by imitating the successful practices of domestic competitors. 
Using the resource-based view (RBV) and institutional theory, this paper presents two competing strategic approaches to reducing LOF. According to RBV and internalization theory, MNEs can reduce LOF by transferring unique capabilities, procedures, and control systems, organizational knowledge embedded in the firm’s administrative heritage, to their foreign subsidiaries. In contrast, institutional theory argues that MNEs may overcome LOF by adapting to the expectations of the host environment through isomorphic adaptation. This involves imitative isomorphism, which voluntarily imitates successful local firms, especially when organizational uncertainty is high or when legitimacy is needed.
Zaheer tests two hypotheses. The first (H1) predicts the existence of LOF, i.e. foreign chambers of commerce have lower profits than local chambers. The second involves two alternatives: H2a assumes that foreign subsidiaries will perform better by imitating their own firm’s domestic organizational practices, while H2b argues that imitating local host country practices will yield better results.
The analysis focuses on two specific types of organizational control practices: market controls (e.g., individual performance-based incentives and recruitment from open labor markets) and bureaucratic controls (e.g., close supervision, micromanagement, and position restrictions). Western banks in New York used market controls primarily, while Japanese banks in Tokyo relied more heavily on bureaucratic controls. These distinct systems provide a natural diversity in organizational practices, which enables the study to assess the impact of transfer performance versus compliance with routines.
The results support H1 and confirm that LOF does indeed exist, even in globally integrated and information-rich industries such as foreign exchange trading. Foreign subsidiaries had lower profits on average than local chambers of commerce in the same location. Findings for H2 are more mixed. H2a received more empirical support than H2b: foreign subsidiaries that retained firm-specific organizational practices, such as market control systems from the parent company, were more successful in reducing LOF than those that adapted to local practices. Imitation of host-country routines did not consistently increase performance and in some cases even reduced profitability due to incompatibility with existing firm structures or inefficiencies introduced by unfamiliar systems.
This study makes several theoretical contributions. It provides one of the earliest empirical validations of LOF as a measurable performance penalty in international markets and reinforces Heimer’s (1976) foundational theory of the costs of doing business abroad. Furthermore, this paper challenges the uncritical assumption in institutional theory that conformist adaptation always leads to better local performance. Instead, Zahir shows that firm-specific advantages rooted in organizational routines and capabilities may create more sustainable and transferable value across markets. This research also highlights the complexity of managing LOF, which is not simply a matter of “adaptation or non-adaptation” but also depends on the nature of the desired routines, the firm’s ability to implement them effectively, and the organizational context of the host environment.
Zaheer (1995) advances the understanding of international firm performance by showing that while LOF is a real and measurable weakness, it can be reduced through strategic choices about transferring organizational performance. Importing firm-specific capabilities provides stronger performance outcomes than imitating local firms, especially when these capabilities are strongly integrated into the firm's global strategy. However, this study cautions that the effectiveness of any approach is context and practice dependent, suggesting the need for further research into how multinational firms manage the dual pressures of global adaptation and local adaptation.
